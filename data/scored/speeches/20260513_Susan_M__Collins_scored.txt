GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Recent indicators suggest that economic activity has been
   expanding at a solid pace." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Collins: "real GDP...grew 2 percent at an annual rate in the
    first quarter of 2026 after advancing at a similar pace last
    year." Calls growth "resilient" despite supply shocks.

Own language:
  "Despite a series of still-unfolding supply shocks — including
   tariffs in 2025 and the oil shock more recently — economic
   growth has remained resilient."
  → Offsets named: AI-related fixed investment, household wealth,
    and the U.S. being a net energy exporter limiting the drag.
  → Downside risk qualifier: "the longer the conflict persists
    the greater the likelihood of more substantial negative
    spillovers."

Shift vs. prior speech:    UNCHANGED — "resilient" framing stable.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:        UNCHANGED — same characterization as

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, on average, and the unemployment
   rate has been little changed in recent months." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Collins: "the labor market has clearly softened from the
    overheated conditions of 2023 and 2024, but the unemployment
    rate remains low by historical standards. At 4.3 percent through
    April, it has also been relatively stable since mid-2025."

Own verdict:
  "This situation — low job creation but a stable unemployment
   rate — largely reflects a slowdown in labor demand and a
   commensurate decline in labor supply growth, linked to the
   significant reduction in immigration."
  → "Unusual balance" framing. Labor demand down, supply down
    roughly in tandem — the balance preserves low unemployment
    but is inherently fragile.

Own qualifier / addition:
  "The unemployment rate has increased notably for college
   graduates...especially the case for those aged 25 to 34...This
   is an atypical pattern that could reflect a shift in employers'
   desired skill mix."
  → AI-driven skill mismatch flag. New addition vs. March 6.
    Potential leading indicator of broader labor demand shift.

  "A risk that job-finding challenges could become more widespread."
  → Explicit downside risk to the "unusual balance."

Shift vs. prior speech:    NEUTRAL — March 6 said "relatively
  balanced labor market conditions." May 13 adds the AI skill
  mismatch risk and underlines fragility of the supply-demand
  balance. Dovish lean at the margin.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:        DOVISH LEAN — new labor fragility
  language not in Mar 6 baseline.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase
   in global energy prices." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Collins: "inflation has been above target for over five years
    — and has increased recently due to the jump in energy prices."
    Adds tariff attribution: "ongoing effects of tariffs, especially
    on goods prices."

Own language — KEY:
  "I believe it is important to durably return inflation to the
   FOMC's 2 percent target in a reasonable amount of time."
  → Clear price stability commitment. "Reasonable amount of time"
    is her bar — not indefinite patience.

  "More than five years of above-target inflation has reduced my
   patience for 'looking through' another supply shock."
  → Explicit look-through fatigue. Five-year duration as the
    key constraint. This is her version of Waller's "sequence of
    shocks" reasoning — multiple shocks stacked, patience exhausted.
    Uses "looking through" in quotes to signal she's aware of the
    conventional wisdom but declining to fully apply it.

  "Services inflation excluding housing prices...remains above
   levels consistent with 2 percent inflation. I find this notable
   because a considerable portion of labor productivity improvements
   have accrued to the services sector. To me, this suggests that,
   with relatively solid demand, firms retain pricing power and have
   less incentive to fully pass efficiency gains on to consumers."
  → Services inflation persistence explained by pricing power —
    not tariffs, not energy. This is the non-supply-shock channel.
    Aligns with the April 29 minutes' staff note on "emergent price
    pressures in a few categories unrelated to tariffs or energy."

  On tariff pass-through: "my staff estimates that much of the
   pass-through of tariffs into total inflation, perhaps about 70
   percent, has already taken place...should wane going forward."
  → 70% pass-through estimate with fading pressure ahead is
    constructive for core inflation — partial dovish qualification.

Shift vs. prior speech:    HAWKISH LEAN — March 6 said "second half
  of the year" for evidence of disinflation progress. May 13 adds
  explicit look-through fatigue and tightening scenario language.
  The "five years above target" framing is more hawkish than March 6.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:        HAWKISH — March 6 was neutral-patient on
  inflation. May 13 introduces explicit patience limits.

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  Easing bias language not echoed. Does not use "additional
  adjustments" or any forward guidance language from the April 29
  statement. Consistent with Paulson May 19 in this respect.

Own language — KEY:
  "It will likely be necessary to maintain the current, slightly
   restrictive monetary policy stance for some time."
  → Rate characterization: "slightly restrictive." In March 6 she
    used "mildly restrictive." "Slightly" and "mildly" are
    equivalent in her vocabulary — stable characterization.

  "More than five years of above-target inflation has reduced my
   patience for 'looking through' another supply shock."
  → The direct policy implication of her inflation framing.

  "While I hope policy normalization can resume late this year,
   I can also envision a scenario that requires some policy
   tightening to ensure that inflation returns durably to 2 percent."
  → TIGHTENING SCENARIO EXPLICITLY NAMED. This is the same
    escalation seen in Paulson May 19 — both officials now name
    tightening as a possible outcome, not just extended hold.

  "At the moment, I see monetary policy as well positioned to
   adjust as needed to evolving economic conditions."
  → The "well positioned" standard holding formula. The preceding
    tightening language is the signal; this is the boilerplate exit.

Cut signal status: ABSENT. Policy normalization "late this year"
  is aspirational, explicitly conditional.

Shift vs. prior speech:    HAWKISH — March 6 had no tightening
  scenario, no look-through fatigue. May 13 introduces both.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:        HAWKISH — clear escalation from Mar 6
  neutral-patient baseline.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH LEAN
  The key escalation vs. March 6 baseline is the explicit naming
  of a tightening scenario — "I can also envision a scenario that
  requires some policy tightening." This is paired with explicit
  look-through fatigue ("five years above target has reduced my
  patience") and an inflation services analysis pointing to
  non-supply-shock price persistence.

  This puts Collins alongside Paulson (May 19) in explicitly
  validating tightening scenarios — both non-Troika, both analytical
  quality, both post-April-29. The convergence is notable: neither
  echoes the April 29 easing bias language.

Language watch:
  • "slightly restrictive" — same as Mar 6 "mildly restrictive."
    Both Paulson and Collins now use this label. Watch for Troika
    adoption, which would confirm there is no longer meaningful
    restriction to remove.
  • "five years above target has reduced my patience for looking
    through" — look-through fatigue language. First use by Collins.
    Semantically close to Waller Apr 17 "sequence of transitory
    shocks." Language cycle stage: EMERGING — Collins + Waller,
    not yet Troika.
  • "I can also envision a scenario that requires some policy
    tightening" — tightening scenario named. Collins + Paulson.
    Language cycle stage: DISPERSED among non-Troika voters.

Analyst flag:  YES
  → Collins (non-voter, high analytical quality) and Paulson
    (voter, estimated median voter) both explicitly name tightening
    scenarios in the same week. Neither echoes the April 29 easing
    bias language. This is consistent with the minutes' revelation
    that "many" participants opposed the easing bias on substance.
    Together these speeches reinforce the minutes signal: the
    effective committee bias has moved away from the formal easing
    bias language within ~2 weeks of the April 29 meeting.
