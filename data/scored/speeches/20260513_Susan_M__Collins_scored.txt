PER-SPEECH ANALYSIS

2026-05-13 | Susan M. Collins | Boston Fed President | Non Voter
"The U.S. Economy: Resilience Amid Risks and Uncertainty"
Boston Economic Club, Boston, Massachusetts
SOURCE: https://www.bostonfed.org/news-and-events/speeches/2026/us-economy-resilience-amid-risks-and-uncertainty.aspx

CLASSIFICATION: Type A — Full Economic Outlook (three-topic structure)
  with detailed energy shock and productivity analysis sections.

Reference FOMC:    April 29, 2026
Pre-FOMC baseline: March 6, 2026  "Observations on the Economic Outlook,
                   and Small Businesses" (scored)
Post-FOMC prior:   N/A — first post-April-29 Collins economic outlook speech
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Economic activity has continued to expand at a solid pace." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Collins version: "real GDP grew 2 percent at an annual rate in the
    first quarter of 2026 after advancing at a similar pace last year.
    Despite a series of still-unfolding supply shocks...economic growth
    has remained resilient."
  → Near-verbatim echo of committee language. No personal departure.

Own language:
  "underlying fundamentals remain healthy overall, despite some pockets
   of stress. Firms' cash holdings are elevated. And households' financial
   asset holdings remain high."
  → Structural supports to demand are intact. Pockets of stress are
    acknowledged but not specified as the leading signal.

  "the positive demand effects already at work could persist, with
   implications for inflation."
  → Productivity-driven demand growth is a two-sided story: it supports
    activity but sustains inflationary pressure. Not a clean growth positive.

Modal outlook:
  "If the Middle East conflict is resolved relatively soon, I expect
   continued solid economic growth over the remainder of this year,
   with possibly a modest rise in the unemployment rate."
  → Growth outlook is explicitly conditional on conflict resolution.
    The base case is solid growth with modest labor market softening.

Shift vs. prior speech (Mar 6):  UNCHANGED — Mar 6 also expected
  "continued solid growth." The modal is stable; the conditional qualifier
  (Middle East) is the new addition.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 6):      UNCHANGED on modal; risk distribution
  wider (energy shock adds downside to activity tail).

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained slow, and the unemployment rate has been little
   changed." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Collins: "The labor market has clearly softened from the overheated
    conditions of 2023 and 2024, but the unemployment rate, shown in the
    right panel, remains low by historical standards. At 4.3 percent through
    April, it has also been relatively stable since mid-2025 despite slow
    hiring."
  → "Clearly softened" is Collins' own intensifier on top of committee
    language. The April 29 statement says "slow" hiring; Collins characterizes
    the broader arc as having "clearly softened" — more directional.

Own verdict:
  "This situation — low job creation but a stable unemployment rate —
   largely reflects a slowdown in labor demand and a commensurate decline
   in labor supply growth, linked to the significant reduction in immigration."
  → Endorses the dual-slowdown explanation. Both sides of the labor market
    are decelerating together — this is her "unusual balance" framing.

Own qualifier / addition:
  "the unemployment rate has increased notably for college graduates, and
   the duration of their associated unemployment spells has risen. This is
   especially the case for those aged 25 to 34... This suggests that more
   educated workers who are early in their careers are having particular
   difficulty finding jobs — which is an atypical pattern that could reflect
   a shift in employers' desired skill mix."
  → NEW SIGNAL: AI-driven skill-mix shift is beginning to show in the data.
    This is a compositional concern within a stable headline number — the
    first such granular observation in the 2026 scored corpus for Collins.
    Hawkish-lean implication: if this pattern broadens, it represents a
    structural labor market softening that is harder to look through.

  "there is a risk that job-finding challenges could become more widespread."
  → Forward-looking downside risk on labor, not current condition.

Shift vs. prior speech (Mar 6):  DOVISH LEAN — Mar 6 said "relatively
  balanced labor market conditions" with no fragility framing. The May 13
  speech adds "clearly softened" and the college-graduate skill-mismatch
  signal. The headline read remains stable but the forward risk framing
  is new.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 6):      Same — dovish lean vs. baseline.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Collins: "inflation has been above target for over five years — and
    has increased recently due to the jump in energy prices."
  → "Above target for over five years" is her personal duration emphasis —
    stronger than the committee's "somewhat elevated." Duration framing
    is a hawkish intensifier (implies persistence, not transience).

Own language — KEY:
  "core PCE inflation...remains elevated. [No] progress since 2024."
  → "No progress since 2024" on core inflation is the sharpest
    characterization in the 2026 Non Voter corpus. It directly contradicts
    any narrative of gradual disinflation on the demand side.

  "services inflation excluding housing prices...remains above levels
   consistent with 2 percent inflation. I find this pattern notable because
   a considerable portion of the labor productivity improvements have accrued
   to the services sector. To me, this suggests that, with relatively solid
   demand, firms retain pricing power and have less incentive to fully pass
   efficiency gains on to consumers."
  → NOTABLE ANALYTICAL ADDITION: Productivity-inflation disconnect in
    services. Firms are capturing efficiency gains as margin, not passing
    them through as lower prices. This is a structural explanation for why
    services inflation is sticky — not easily addressed by rate policy.

  "More than five years of above-target inflation has reduced my patience
   for 'looking through' another supply shock."
  → Near-identical language to Powell's Apr 29 Q&A. This is now a third
    official (alongside Waller Apr 17, Powell Apr 29) to explicitly adopt
    the "reduced patience for look-through" framework. Language is spreading.

  "I am particularly concerned about inflation."
  → Her own explicit priority statement. Unambiguously hawkish lean.

Tariff pass-through analysis:
  "my staff estimates that much of the pass-through of tariffs into total
   inflation, perhaps about 70 percent, has already taken place."
  → Quantitative estimate: 70% of tariff effects are now in prices.
    Remaining 30% should wane — this is conditionally disinflationary
    going forward. But she immediately qualifies: near-term risk if tariff
    rates revert to pre-Supreme Court levels.

Shift vs. prior speech (Mar 6):  HAWKISH LEAN — Mar 6 said "second half
  of the year" for disinflation evidence. May 13 says "little to no
  reduction in inflation this year" and "no progress since 2024" on core.
  This is a material downgrade of her disinflation timeline.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 6):      Same. Energy shock and services persistence
  have materially worsened the inflation picture vs. baseline.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "[The Committee] judges that the risks to achieving its employment and
   inflation goals are roughly in balance." / "attentive to risks on both
   sides." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Collins: "I am always focused on both sides of our mandate from
    Congress, but at this point, am particularly concerned about inflation."
  → She acknowledges the dual mandate but tilts explicitly to inflation —
    personal departure from the symmetrical committee framing.

Own language:
  "It will likely be necessary to maintain the current, slightly restrictive
   monetary policy stance for some time."
  → "Slightly restrictive" is a downgrade from her Mar 6 "mildly restrictive."
    The semantic difference is small but note the direction: "slightly" is
    a weaker characterization of restriction than "mildly." [Inference: this
    could imply she views the stance as less well-positioned to address the
    inflation risk than before the energy shock — consistent with her
    subsequent mention of a tightening scenario.]

  "While I hope policy normalization can resume late this year, I can also
   envision a scenario that requires some policy tightening to ensure that
   inflation returns durably to 2 percent."
  → EXPLICIT RATE HIKE SCENARIO — first mention of potential tightening from
    a Non Voter in the 2026 post-FOMC corpus. "I hope" signals this is not
    the modal but a tail risk she is taking seriously enough to name. This is
    the most hawkish policy language from Collins in the 2026 corpus.

  "at the moment, I see monetary policy as well positioned to adjust as
   needed to evolving economic conditions."
  → Standard committee holding formula. Restores ambiguity after the
    tightening mention — she is not pre-committing.

Cut signal status: ABSENT. Hold with explicit tightening tail risk now
  on the record. No timeline for cuts offered.

Shift vs. prior speech (Mar 6):  HAWKISH LEAN — Mar 6 said "I do not see
  an urgency for additional policy adjustments" and "mildly restrictive."
  May 13 adds an explicit hike scenario and upgrades the hold signal to
  "necessary...for some time." Materially more hawkish.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 6):      HAWKISH LEAN. The energy shock and
  services inflation persistence have pushed her policy language meaningfully
  toward the hawkish end of her historical range.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH LEAN (vs. March 6 baseline)

Collins has moved materially since her March 6 baseline in three dimensions:
  (1) Inflation timeline downgraded — from "second half of year" for
      disinflation evidence to "little to no reduction this year" on core.
  (2) Services inflation flagged as structural — productivity gains being
      captured as firm margin, not passed to consumers, explains stickiness.
  (3) Explicit rate hike scenario on the record — first from a Non Voter
      in the 2026 post-FOMC corpus.

On labor, she is slightly more cautious (college-graduate skill-mismatch
signal) but the headline read remains "stable." The growth modal is unchanged
(solid), now made explicitly conditional on conflict resolution.

Her "reduced patience for looking through" formulation is the third official
adoption of this framework (Waller Apr 17, Powell Apr 29, Collins May 13).
This is an emerging committee-wide language shift that is approaching but not
yet at the "in-statement" stage.

Language watch:
  • "Reduced my patience for 'looking through' another supply shock" —
    third adoption after Waller (Apr 17) and Powell (Apr 29). Language
    cycle stage: EMERGING SHARED (3 officials). Watch for Jefferson/Williams.
  • "No progress since 2024" on core inflation — stark characterization of
    disinflation stall. If this spreads to voters, it would signal committee
    is prepared to hold significantly longer than markets price.
  • "Envision a scenario that requires some policy tightening" — tail risk
    hike language. First from a Non Voter. If this appears from a voter
    (Paulson, Hammack, Logan), it would be a material signal to markets.
  • "Slightly restrictive" — minor semantic downgrade from "mildly
    restrictive." Watch for whether this language migrates or reverses.

Analyst flag: YES
  → Explicit tightening scenario language from a Non Voter is the headline
    analytical question: Is this (a) a genuine tail risk Collins is flagging
    based on private data/models, or (b) standard optionality language that
    all officials maintain? The context — services stickiness + no-progress
    on core + productivity-margin-capture thesis — suggests it is more
    substantive than boilerplate. Analyst should assess whether the 70%
    tariff-pass-through estimate is credible and how much of the remaining
    services stickiness it leaves unexplained.
