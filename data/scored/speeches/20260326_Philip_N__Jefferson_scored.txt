LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, and the unemployment rate has
   been little changed in recent months." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Jefferson: "The unemployment rate has been relatively low
    and little changed in recent months."
    (Near-verbatim echo of the Mar 18 statement.)

Own verdict:
  "I see the labor market as roughly in balance and expect
   the unemployment rate to remain near its current level
   throughout this year."
  → Stable, neutral characterization. Same "roughly in balance"
    as Feb 6.

Own qualifier (⚠ new addition vs. Feb 6):
  "The risks to my labor market forecast, however, are skewed
   to the downside."
  → "Skewed to the downside" — this is new language not present
    in Feb 6. A meaningful addition: his symmetric "roughly
    in balance" forecast now has an explicit downside tail.
  "The labor market is roughly in balance but susceptible to
   adverse shocks."
  → "Susceptible to adverse shocks" also new. Adds fragility
    qualifier not present before.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 6):   MILD DOVISH LEAN — same baseline
  verdict ("roughly in balance") but new downside risk flags
  ("skewed to the downside," "susceptible to adverse shocks").

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Jefferson: "progress on disinflation has stalled over the
    past year, mainly because of tariffs, and inflation remains
    somewhat elevated relative to our target."
    (Closely echoes the statement's framing.)

Own language — KEY:
  "It has been my expectation that the disinflationary process
   would resume once higher tariffs are no longer pushing up
   consumer prices."
  → States his prior expectation — about to be complicated.

  "The ongoing trade policy uncertainty and geopolitical
   tensions, however, pose upside risk to my inflation
   forecast. At least in the short term I expect overall
   inflation to move higher, reflecting a rise in energy
   prices stemming from the conflict in the Middle East."
  → KEY: admits the energy shock is complicating his prior
    expectation. "Upside risk to my inflation forecast" is
    his personal acknowledgment that his baseline has
    deteriorated. Not in the FOMC statement.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 6):   HAWKISH — Feb 6 was confident
  disinflation would resume as tariff pass-through completed.
  Mar 26 adds energy shock as a new complication and explicitly
  flags "upside risk to my inflation forecast."

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  → Jefferson: "I am confident that our current policy stance
    is well positioned to respond to a range of outcomes."
    ("Well positioned" is the committee's stable holding formula.)

Own language:
  "I confront an outlook where there is downside risk to the
   labor market and upside risk to inflation."
  → First use of his dual-risk formula. Becomes his stable
    characterization through Apr 7.

Cut signal status: ABSENT — "well positioned" holding formula.
  No forward guidance introduced.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 6):   MINIMAL — same "well positioned"
  formula. Feb 6 referenced "range of neutral" rate framing;
  Mar 26 drops this and uses only "well positioned." Slight
  hawkish lean in monetary policy section.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL — stable hold; energy shock adds
               complexity but doesn't change the stance

Directional:   Jefferson's post-FOMC debut establishes the
  "dual risk" framework ("downside to labor, upside to
  inflation") that becomes his stable holding formula for
  subsequent speeches. The energy shock added two new
  elements vs. Feb 6: labor downside tail ("skewed to
  downside," "susceptible to shocks") and inflation upside
  risk ("upside risk to my forecast"). Net effect: increased
  caution on both sides, confirming the hold.

Language watch:
  • "Downside risk to labor, upside risk to inflation, well
    positioned" — his stable dual-risk formula. Appears in
    Williams Mar 30 onward. Track as potential committee-wide
    language.
  • "Upside risk to my inflation forecast" — personal
    acknowledgment. Not yet in the FOMC statement.
  • "Susceptible to adverse shocks" — new labor qualifier.
    Watch for it in Apr 7 (it appears there too).

Analyst flag: NO — trajectory clear and consistent.
