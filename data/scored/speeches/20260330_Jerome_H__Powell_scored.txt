INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Powell: "We think it's [tariff inflation] really just a one-time
    price increase. That's been our thinking since the beginning.
    Right now we think it's adding somewhere between a half and a
    full percentage point to inflation."
    Echoes committee consensus language on tariff pass-through.

Own language — KEY (series of shocks):
  "The tendency is to look through any kind of a supply shock. But
   a critical essential aspect of that is you have to carefully
   monitor inflation expectations because you can have a series of
   these supply shocks and that can lead the public generally...to
   start expecting higher inflation over time. Why wouldn't they?
   At the end of a certain number of years the inflation is now just
   higher and that can happen. So you monitor that very very
   carefully."
  → IMPORTANT: Powell articulates the "series of supply shocks"
    concern on March 30 — the same analytical framework Waller
    formalizes into "sequence of transitory shocks / more vigilant"
    on April 17. This dates the concept to the Chair himself,
    three weeks before Waller's speech.

Own language — expectations:
  "Inflation expectations do appear to be well anchored beyond the
   short term, but nonetheless, it's something we will eventually
   maybe face the question of what to do here. We're not really
   facing it yet because we don't know what the economic effects
   will be, but we'll certainly be mindful of that broader context."
  → Maintains the dovish anchor ("well anchored beyond the short
    term") while acknowledging the monitoring challenge. More
    guarded than the standard "well anchored" boilerplate — the
    qualifier "beyond the short term" is a mild softening.

Direction: neutral with hawkish undertone — "series of shocks"
  concern explicit; "well anchored beyond the short term" qualifier
  is slightly more cautious than standard committee language.

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  → Powell: "We feel like our policies are in a good place for us to

    "Wait and see" = "well positioned" in plain English.

Own language:
  "There's sort of downside risk to the labor market, which suggests
   keep rates low, but there's upside risk to inflation, which
   suggests maybe don't keep rates low. You've got tension between
   the two objectives."
  → Powell articulating the dual-risk framework in plain, unscripted
    language for a student audience. Consistent with Jefferson and
    Williams' formal framing. No new policy signal.

  "We're not really facing it [the hard policy choice on the energy
   shock] yet because we don't know what the economic effects will be."
  → Explicit patience signal: not yet at a point of having to choose.

Cut signal status: ABSENT — "wait and see" holding formula.

Direction: neutral — patient hold confirmed in plain language.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL — patient hold; "wait and see" on energy
               shock; "series of shocks" monitoring concern

Directional:   This is the Chair's only 2026 appearance with
  economic outlook content. His framing is consistent with
  committee consensus — tariffs are one-time, energy shock is
  uncertain, wait and see. The notable addition is his explicit
  articulation of the "series of supply shocks → expectations risk"
  concern, in plain language, three weeks before Waller formalizes
  the same concept. This does not change the policy stance but is
  significant for the language cycle: the "look through" qualification
  was in the Chair's mind from at least March 30.

LANGUAGE CYCLE — KEY FINDING:
  Goolsbee (Mar 7, Bloomberg): "it can lead you in a stagflationary
    direction...that's always the worst case scenario for the central
    bank" [earliest explicit stagflation naming from any official]
  Barkin (Mar 27): "latest in a series of cost-increasing supply
    shocks"
  Powell (Mar 30, Harvard): "series of these supply shocks...can lead
    the public to start expecting higher inflation"

  As of March 30: The "series of shocks → expectations risk" concept
  has appeared across Goolsbee (Mar 7), Barkin (Mar 27), and now
  Powell (Mar 30). It is at the dispersed-to-emerging stage — three
  officials independently articulating the same concern. Not yet in
  the FOMC statement. Not yet adopted by Jefferson or Williams in
  formal speeches.

Language watch:
  • "Series of supply shocks → expectations risk" — Powell's
    articulation confirms this is not Waller's invention but a
    concept circulating at the Chair level. Its absence from the
    formal FOMC statement (so far) is the key gap to watch.
  • "Well anchored beyond the short term" — Powell's qualifier is
    slightly more cautious than the standard "well anchored." If this
    phrasing appears in post-FOMC speeches or the statement, it
    would signal growing expectations concern at the Troika level.

Analyst flag: YES
  → The "series of shocks" language cycle is the key analytical

    concept was at the Chair level by March 30 — before Waller's
    Apr 17 formalization. Analyst to assess: given that Powell
    himself was articulating this concern, why did it not appear in
