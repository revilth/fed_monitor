GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Economic activity is expanding at a solid pace despite elevated uncertainty
   … conflict in the Middle East. Productivity growth and capital investment are
   strong." [Jun 17 stmt]
  → Williams: "the U.S. economy has so far absorbed these shocks and has remained
    resilient amid the uncertainty … consumer spending has held up, and business
    investment has remained robust, largely fueled by AI-related investments."

Own language:
  Real GDP "around 2-1/4 percent this year and over the next two years," with
  "growth running modestly above its potential rate of 2 percent," and the
  unemployment rate edging "down gradually to 4 percent in 2028."
  → A firm, slightly-above-potential growth path; AI investment named as the
    engine of business investment (consistent with his May 4 framing and the
    June 17 statement).

Shift vs. prior speech (May 4):  unchanged — same "resilient," AI-investment-led
  framing; GDP forecast steady at ~2.25%. If anything a hair firmer ("modestly
  above potential," unemployment edging DOWN), but not a directional change.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (May 4):      unchanged.

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "the unemployment rate has changed little." [Jun 17 stmt]
  → Williams: "At 4.3 percent, the unemployment rate has changed little over the
    past year." (Does not use the statement's "kept pace with the workforce"
    phrasing, but is consistent with it.)

Own verdict — KEY SHIFT (the soft-data slack concern recedes):
  "Other labor market measures — including job openings, unemployment claims, and
   job-finding and separation rates — have likewise been stable or have modestly
   strengthened. And survey measures of job and worker availability, along with
   the New York Fed's 'job security gap,' — both of which had been flashing
   warning signs earlier — have also largely stabilized, albeit at low levels."
  → This is the most important change vs. May 4. On May 4 these SAME indicators
    were his dovish leg: perceptions of job availability "continued to trend
    downward," "we have yet to see signs of sustained stabilization in the 'job
    security gap'… these indicators suggest increasing labor market slack." On
    June 25 they have "largely stabilized" and warning signs have receded. The
    "increasing labor market slack" call is effectively withdrawn. HAWKISH-LEAN.
    Caveat retained: "albeit at low levels."

Own qualifier (inflation anchor maintained):
  "we are not seeing evidence that the labor market is adding to inflationary
   pressures." (stated in the inflation section)
  → Keeps the May 4 anchor that labor is NOT compounding the supply-shock
    inflation (the post-2021 lesson). Dovish-protective on this dimension.

Shift vs. prior speech (May 4):  HAWKISH LEAN — May 4's "increasing labor market
  slack" (soft data) is replaced by "largely stabilized." The dovish labor leg
  has weakened. Overall labor verdict moves from "conflicting signs" to "resilient
  … stable."
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (May 4):      HAWKISH LEAN — same.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains elevated relative to the Committee's 2 percent goal, in part
   reflecting supply shocks … including energy." [Jun 17 stmt]
  → Williams: "inflation is unquestionably elevated and well above the FOMC's
    longer-run goal of 2 percent."

Own language — KEY (explicit disinflation call, dovish DIRECTION):
  "In coming quarters, however, I expect inflation readings to edge down …"
  Three supports: (1) "the direct effects of existing tariffs on prices appear to
  have mostly played out. Any new tariffs would replace those that were curtailed
  or will soon expire, implying that we should not see a significant additional
  impulse on prices"; (2) energy stabilizing then falling later this year if
  Hormuz resolves; (3) shelter inflation slowing on modest market-rent increases;
  plus labor not adding pressure.
  → DOVISH directional read. Note the SHIFT on tariffs: on May 4 a "new round of
    tariffs in the coming months" was an upside RISK; here new tariffs are
    downplayed as net-neutral (replacing expiring ones). That is a softening.

Own language — NEW driver + the offsetting hawkish nuance (timeline slips):
  Adds a THIRD inflation driver new vs. May 4: "robust demand for certain
  categories of technology goods related to the AI investment boom." And the
  glide-path date SLIPS: "decline to 3-1/2 percent by year-end, then continue on
  a glide path toward our 2 percent goal in 2027 and land on target in 2028."
  → On May 4 he forecast "2% in 2027." Here 2% is not reached until 2028 — the
    return-to-target timeline is pushed out a full year. So even as DIRECTION is
    dovish, the DURATION is longer (more persistence acknowledged). AI demand is
    also flagged as an upside risk ("may push up prices more than expected").
  Expectations: "medium-term inflation expectations … remained well anchored …
   one-year-ahead up modestly and three- and five-year-ahead essentially
   unchanged." (maintains the May 4 anchored-expectations leg.)

Shift vs. prior speech (May 4):  MIXED, net mild dovish on direction / mild
  hawkish on duration — explicit "edge down" call + new tariffs downplayed
  (dovish), but 2%-target date slips 2027 → 2028 and AI demand added as a driver/
  upside risk (hawkish). Net: confident disinflation, but slower.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (May 4):      same.

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "The Committee decided to maintain the target range … 3-1/2 to 3-3/4 percent …
   in support of the Federal Reserve's dual mandate." [Jun 17 stmt]
  + the statement's commitment register ("The Committee will deliver price
   stability.")
  → Williams: "Given the elevated level of inflation, it is imperative that we
    restore it to our 2 percent longer-run goal on a sustained basis. The current
    stance of monetary policy is well positioned to do that." Explicitly endorses
    the June 17 hold ("at its meeting last week, the FOMC decided to maintain …").

Own language:
  "well positioned" = the committee's stable holding formula (boilerplate — flag
  only if it changes; it has not). NO cut signal. NO forward guidance (consistent
  with Warsh's June 17 removal). Closes on pure data-dependence: "my views, as
  always, will be based on the evolution of the totality of the data, the economic
  outlook, and the balance of risks."
  The "imperative that we restore it … on a sustained basis" phrasing is a touch
  firmer than May 4's symmetric "balance the risks," echoing the statement's
  price-stability commitment — mild hawkish framing, not a new signal.

  Cut signal status: ABSENT — unchanged from the April 16 retraction carried
  through May 4. He neither restores nor forecloses cuts; the disinflation glide
  path is described without attaching any rate-cut timing to it.

Shift vs. prior speech (May 4):  unchanged on stance — patient hold, no guidance,
  no cut signal. Commitment language marginally firmer (statement-aligned).
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (May 4):      unchanged.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
EMBEDDED OPERATIONAL SECTION (Type B — no rate-path signal)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
~Half the speech (Money Fund Symposium audience) covers implementation: ample
reserves framework; IORB sets the floor, ON RRP reinforces it, SRP sets the
ceiling at the top of the target range; reserve management purchases (RMPs)
maintain "ample." Concrete balance-sheet datapoint: after the FOMC judged in
December that reserves had returned to "ample," the Desk began RMPs at $40B/mo,
then tapered to $25B (mid-Apr–mid-May) and $10B (mid-May–mid-Jul); "stand ready
to adjust the pace of RMPs up or down as needed." Informational for money markets
and the balance-sheet trajectory; NOT a monetary-policy stance signal.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL with a mild HAWKISH lean vs. his May 4 baseline. The
               Troika holdline is intact (hold endorsed, no cut signal, no
               guidance). The directional moves: labor — the soft-data "increasing
               slack" concern from May has "largely stabilized" (hawkish);
               inflation — confident it will "edge down" and new tariffs downplayed
               (dovish), but the return-to-2% date slips 2027 → 2028 and AI demand
               is added as a new driver/upside risk (hawkish). Net, Williams is
               slightly firmer than May 4 while staying the patient-hold Troika
               anchor.

Positioning vs. the hawkish June 17 statement: Williams is ALIGNED (he is Troika
  and helped shape it) but is the CONSTRUCTIVE voice within it — he lays out a
  concrete disinflation glide path ("edge down," "land on target in 2028") rather
  than the statement's persistence-and-commitment register. He provides the "hold
  is well positioned, disinflation is coming, be patient" Troika framing — without
  signaling cuts.

Language watch:
  • "I expect inflation readings to edge down" — the clearest forward disinflation
     call from the Troika since the June meeting. If Jefferson/Warsh echo it, it
     signals the Troika is preparing the ground for an eventual cut bias (bottom-up
     language cycle; currently DISPERSED/Troika-only).
  • "largely stabilized, albeit at low levels" (job-availability / job-security
     gap) — withdrawal of his May "increasing labor market slack" call. Watch
     whether the dovish labor leg returns if the July payrolls (Jul 3) soften.
  • "land on target in 2028" — the return-to-2% horizon has slipped a year vs. his
     May "2% in 2027." A quiet acknowledgment of more persistence even amid a
     dovish direction.
  • "imperative that we restore it … on a sustained basis" — statement-aligned
     price-stability commitment language; top-down echo, not a personal signal.

Analyst flag: YES
  → Two things to assess. (1) Williams (Troika) on June 25 expects inflation to
    "edge down" and frames the hold as well positioned to deliver it — a more
    constructive read than the hawkish June 17 statement / SEP. Is the Troika
    quietly laying the groundwork for a later cut bias while the statement still
    reads hawkish (a bottom-up dots-vs-statement gap)? (2) Same-day CONTRAST with
    Goolsbee: the non-voter (Goolsbee) is MORE hawkish (AI-productivity overheating
    + persistence vigilance, cut signal dropped) than the Troika member (Williams,
    disinflation glide path). The committee's hawkish center may be the regionals,
    not the Troika — watch whether Jefferson/Warsh side with Williams' "edge down"
    or the regionals' persistence framing.
