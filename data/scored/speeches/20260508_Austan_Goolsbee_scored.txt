──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
No direct growth characterization in prepared remarks. Embedded in the
AI/productivity framework: the 1990s analogy — a productivity acceleration
either arrived unexpectedly (rate-lowering) or was widely expected
(rate-raising via wealth effect pulling demand forward).

Applied to 2026: Goolsbee's framework implies the current AI productivity
boom is predominantly expected, not yet delivered — i.e., the wealth
effect from anticipated future income is the operative force. He explicitly
names data center investment concentrated in Silicon Valley as a concrete
expression of this pull-forward.

Direction: neutral — no direct assessment of current growth conditions.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
No direct labor market assessment in the prepared remarks. In Q&A on
AI-driven labor displacement fears:
  "If 50% of consumers are hand-to-mouth and they can't borrow from the
   future then we can't overheat — that's the way it goes. But I think
   the my intuition was if you think there's a K-shaped economy and
   equity values are going through the ceiling and the top of the K are
   spending like crazy on houses in PaloAlto out of their equity wealth
   nobody has to borrow anything and it could still look very much like
   what I'm describing now."
  → K-shaped economy framing: the wealth effect driving demand is
    concentrated at the top; hand-to-mouth constraint at the bottom
    does not neutralize it. Consistent with his Feb 24 observation
    that rates may not be as restrictive as assumed.

Direction: neutral — labor not addressed as a policy topic.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
No direct current-cycle inflation assessment in prepared remarks.
The embedded framework has inflation implications:

  Scenario A (unexpected productivity): inflation falls, rates fall.
  Scenario B (expected productivity, widely anticipated AI boom): inflation
  falls initially, but wealth-effect pull-forward overheats economy, central
  bank must raise nominal rate even as inflation appears subdued.
  Scenario C (expected boom that disappoints): stagflation risk —
  overconsumption + recession when the boom fails to materialize.

Applied to current moment:
  "There's a whole list of other things... the things you talked about was
   data center investment and consumer spending at the higher end and do
   they keep blowing through that... The bigger the share of the total
   productivity boom that is still on the way, the more likely it is that
   rates are going to need to rise."
  → [Inference: implied hawkish lean on inflation via natural rate, not
    direct inflation assessment. The mechanism is expected AI productivity
    requiring higher r-star, not current inflation dynamics.]

Direction: hawkish lean (via natural rate / r-star implications) — inferred,
  not stated.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
No direct cut/hold statement. The prepared remarks build an analytical
framework with a directional implication:

  "If you have unexpected productivity, rates should probably fall. The more
   it's predicted to be coming soon, the more it's going to motivate shifting
   behavior by forward-looking agents, and the more likely it is to drive up
   the natural rate to prevent overheating. If the central bank reacts too
   slowly, it probably gets worse. The bigger the hype, the bigger the
   concern."
  → "The bigger the hype, the bigger the concern" is the operative line.
    Current AI hype is very large. The framework implies the natural rate
    is elevated — i.e., current policy may be less restrictive than it appears.

This is consistent with his Feb 24 NABE framing ("not obvious that our
interest rate policy is even restrictive") but approaches it from a
different angle: expected-productivity-driven r-star elevation, rather
than direct assessment of the level of rates.

Cut signal status: ABSENT in this appearance. The framework implicitly
  argues AGAINST cutting until the pull-forward demand effect is measured.

Shift vs. prior speech (Mar 23):   HAWKISH LEAN — Mar 23 was a direct
  Type A economic outlook interview. This is Type B. The AI framework adds
  a new channel (r-star elevation via expected productivity) reinforcing
  the existing "rates may not be restrictive" framing from Feb 24 / Mar 23.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 23):       As above. No direct comparison possible
  on labor/inflation but the natural rate framework is incrementally hawkish
  relative to his baseline conditional-cut stance.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH LEAN — via natural rate / r-star framework
               Goolsbee's AI productivity analysis implies the real neutral
               rate is elevated by anticipated future productivity gains. This
               adds an academic-level argument against cutting to his existing
               "inflation must show progress" framing.

Key analytical contribution:
  The unexpected vs. expected productivity distinction is the cleanest
  statement of this mechanism at a policy panel. Unexpected → lower rates.
  Expected (AI hype) → higher natural rate. The Fed is operating in the
  expected-productivity regime. This is not a cut argument.

Language watch:
  • "The bigger the hype, the bigger the concern" — compact, quotable, likely
    cited widely. Summarizes the r-star / pull-forward risk in one sentence.
  • K-shaped economy + equity wealth = demand pull-forward without borrowing
    — specific mechanism for why hand-to-mouth constraints don't immunize
    against overheating. Not yet in other officials' framing.
  • "If the central bank reacts too slowly it probably gets worse" — implicit
    commentary on current rate path; directional without being explicit.

Analyst flag: YES
  → Goolsbee's natural rate framework is analytically significant and likely
    the most-cited element of the Hoover panel. Analyst to assess whether
    the AI/r-star argument is consistent with his existing "not obvious rates
    are restrictive" view (Feb 24) or represents a new, more developed
    theoretical basis for that intuition. If other committee members adopt
    this framing, it would signal a committee-wide revision of the neutral
    rate estimate upward.
