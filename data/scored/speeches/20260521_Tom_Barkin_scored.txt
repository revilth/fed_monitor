PER-SPEECH ANALYSIS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
2026-05-21 | Thomas I. Barkin | Richmond Fed President | Non Voter
"Navigating Supply Shocks"
ULI Triangle Capital Markets, Dorothy and Roy Park Alumni Center,
Raleigh, NC
SOURCE: https://www.richmondfed.org/press_room/speeches/thomas_i_barkin/2026/barkin_speech_20260521

CLASSIFICATION: Type B with embedded outlook. Primary frame is a
  supply-shock framework speech (conventional look-through logic,
  anchoring mechanics, risk-of-recurrence argument). Embedded
  throughout: hard data on growth, labor market, and inflation;
  policy stance. Structure: supply-shock history → latest wave
  (Middle East) → resilience arguments → look-through convention
  → forward risk questions (businesses / consumers / expectations
  anchor) → policy conclusion. No standalone "outlook section" but
  sufficient coverage across all four topics for full scoring.

Reference FOMC:    April 29, 2026  (hold; FFR 4.25–4.50%)
Pre-FOMC baseline: March 27, 2026  "Driving Through Economic Fog (Still)"
                   Appalachian Highlands Economic Forum, Johnson City TN
Post-FOMC prior:   N/A — first post-April-29 Barkin speech.
                   March 27 serves as both prior speech and pre-FOMC baseline.
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "economic activity has been expanding at a solid pace" [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Barkin: "The U.S. economy remains remarkably resilient."
    Closely parallels the statement's framing; no softening.

Own language:
  "Consumer spending is up, and not just due to ever more expensive
   gasoline. Non-gas spending growth has remained solid, too.
   Corporate profits are high. Artificial intelligence (AI) investment
   continues at full steam. Manufacturing surveys have rebounded.
   The S&P 500 keeps setting records. Payroll growth has started to
   bounce back."
  → Unusually broad enumeration of positive indicators across spending,
    profits, investment, surveys, equities, and employment. More
    bullish than the March 27 "fog" framing.

  "I've heard two explanations [for resilience]. The first is that
   the U.S. economy has become somewhat immune to oil price shocks...
   The second is that larger tax refunds, along with supportive
   financial conditions and an AI-driven investment boom, have largely
   offset any adverse impact on demand — at least for now."
  → "At least for now" — a hedge on the durability of the offset.
    He does not adopt the resilience explanations uncritically.

  "Consumers and firms have become desensitized...Most people believe
   the United States won't tolerate sustained higher oil prices...
   so they work off the assumption that this shock will truly be
   transitory."
  → Behavioral argument: animal spirits are supporting demand, but
    the assumption of transitoriness is endogenous (not guaranteed).

Shift vs. prior speech:   HAWKISH LEAN (positive) — March characterized
  growth as "foggy" with limited visibility; May describes the economy
  as "remarkably resilient" with multiple supporting indicators. Higher
  growth reduces mechanical urgency for cuts.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:       same as above — HAWKISH LEAN (same baseline).

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, on average, and the unemployment
   rate has been little changed in recent months." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Barkin: "Payroll growth has started to bounce back."
    More optimistic than the statement — he describes a recovery
    trend rather than low-and-stable.

Own language — Hard data:
  "Payroll growth has started to bounce back." — uptick registered.
  [Note: March 27 described "Employment growth near zero" — this
   is a directional improvement.]

Own language — Forward risk (businesses):
  "They've largely gotten more productive through attrition, not
   layoffs. At what point will they more broadly gain conviction
   that it is time to reduce headcount more actively?"
  → Forward uncertainty: the efficiency gains have come without
    widespread layoffs. The risk is not current but prospective —
    if firms determine cuts are warranted, the transition could
    be abrupt.

Own language — Forward risk (consumers):
  "Consumer sentiment hit its lowest point on record as measured
   by the University of Michigan Survey of Consumers — but they've
   persisted."
  → Hard/soft divergence explicitly noted: consumers are unhappy
    but still spending. The forward risk: "As real wage growth
    slows, tax refunds fade, and trade-down options run their course,
    will consumers finally reduce their appetite for spending?"
  → Three specific triggers identified — real wages, fading fiscal
    support, exhausted coping mechanisms. Descriptive scenario (not
    explicit-named à la Waller).

Shift vs. prior speech:   MIXED — hard data improved (payrolls
  "bouncing back" vs. "near zero"); forward risk framing added.
  Net: slightly positive on current state, but forward risks now
  explicitly articulated in a way absent from March 27.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:       same baseline — same assessment.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase
   in global energy prices." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Barkin: provides the exact figures — "Headline PCE jumped up
    to 3.5 percent year–over–year in March 2026. Core inflation,
    which excludes food and energy, increased more modestly to
    3.2 percent." Energy attribution explicit (Middle East conflict).

Own language — Near-term expectations:
  "The recent increase in gas prices has understandably increased
   near-term inflation expectations."
  → Acknowledges near-term expectations lift. Not alarmed.

Own language — Long-term expectations:
  "So far, measures of forward inflation compensation beyond the
   next year and survey-based measures of long-term inflation
   expectations remain well anchored."
  → "Well anchored" language — unconditional, unlike Paulson's
    "currently in a good place" conditional hedge (May 19).
    Slightly more sanguine on expectations than Paulson.

Own language — KEY (duration risk):
  "With inflation above our 2 percent target for over five years
   now, it's worth asking whether the cumulative impact of so many
   waves risks loosening the anchor."
  → This is the signal. He uses the "five years above target"
    duration framing — the same phrase that appeared in Goolsbee
    (May 12, Non Voter) and Collins (May 13, Non Voter). Third
    official to deploy this language. Barkin does not resolve the
    question, but by asking it he is signaling personal unease
    with the duration of above-target inflation.

  "I'm hearing a renewed determination by businesses to pass these
   highly visible, conflict-driven cost increases on to their
   customers."
  → Note: this is the OPPOSITE of his March 27 signal, which was
    "More than nine in ten firms...feel they won't be able to do
    so to their desired levels" (consumers tired of high prices).
    The pricing power assessment has reversed — firms are now
    attempting pass-through. Material hawkish shift.
    [Note: March 27 described disinflationary pricing-power loss;
     May 21 describes a pass-through attempt — directional reversal.]

Shift vs. prior speech:   HAWKISH — PCE data higher (3.2% core vs.
  ~2.8% implied at March 27); pass-through behavior reversed (firms
  now attempting, vs. constrained in March); duration framing added.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:       same baseline — HAWKISH shift overall.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "remains prepared to adjust policy if risks emerge threatening
   its dual mandate objectives" [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Barkin: "the Fed is well positioned to respond as appropriate."
    Near-verbatim echo. This is boilerplate committee-consensus
    language.

Own language:
  "At our last meeting, we held rates steady; with little clarity
   on the duration and impact of this latest supply shock, it made
   sense to give ourselves some time before setting sail."
  → Endorses hold; frames it as temporal ("some time") rather than
    conditional on specific data thresholds. Patience, not a bar.

  "Going forward, I wouldn't be surprised if we continue to see
   rough seas that pressure the employment side of our mandate,
   the inflation side of our mandate, or conceivably both."
  → Two-sided risk framing: employment OR inflation OR both. This is
    a descriptive scenario — he names the risk categories but does
    not pre-commit to a policy response for each (contrast with
    Waller's Apr 17 explicit BENIGN/ADVERSE named scenario framing).

Cut signal status: ABSENT — no forward guidance; patience framing
  only; no timing language.
Hike signal status: ABSENT — "well positioned" is symmetric but
  no explicit hike scenario named.

Shift vs. prior speech:   UNCHANGED — March 27 was "await clarity,
  give ourselves some time"; May 21 is identical posture. Hold
  conviction unchanged.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:       same baseline — UNCHANGED on policy stance.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH LEAN
               Barkin's first post-FOMC speech is more hawkish than
               his March 27 pre-FOMC baseline on three dimensions:
               (1) growth resilience is higher, reducing urgency for
               cuts; (2) core PCE has risen from ~2.8% to 3.2%; and
               (3) he adds a duration concern — "five years above
               target" — that was absent in March, accompanied by
               a reversal in the pricing-power signal (firms now
               attempting pass-through vs. constrained in March).
               Policy stance is unchanged (hold/patience), but the
               conditions supporting that hold have become more
               inflation-weighted.

Language watch:
  • "Five years above our 2 percent target" — now in Goolsbee
    (May 12), Collins (May 13), and Barkin (May 21). Three Non
    Voters deploying the same duration framing in 10 days.
    Language cycle stage: EMERGING SHARED among Non Voters.
    Track for spread to Just Voters or Troika.
  • "Businesses...renewed determination to pass...cost increases"
    — reversal of Barkin's March 27 pricing-power signal.
    Barkin's ground-level business intelligence is a reliable
    early indicator. This directional shift is notable.
  • "Well positioned to respond as appropriate" — committee
    boilerplate echoed; no personal variation added. Barkin is
    not signaling personal asymmetry here.

Analyst flag:  YES
  → Barkin's pricing-power reversal is the most operationally
    significant signal in this speech. In March he reported
    firms unable to pass through costs ("consumers tired of
    high prices"). In May he reports "renewed determination" to
    pass through "highly visible" conflict-driven costs. If this
    reversal reflects actual business behavior rather than
    transient sentiment, it implies cost-push inflation from
    the energy shock is now feeding into broader price-setting.
    Combine with the "five years above target" duration framing
    and the cumulative anchor question — Barkin has moved from
    the committee's most diagnostically balanced Non Voter to a
    clear inflation-watching lean. His on-the-ground intelligence
    makes this harder to dismiss than standard macro language.
