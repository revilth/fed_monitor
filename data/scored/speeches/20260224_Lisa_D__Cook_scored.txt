ISSUE 1 — LABOR MARKET AND POLICY EFFICACY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"if AI continues to raise productivity, economic growth could remain strong,
 even as churn in the labor market leads to an increase in unemployment. In
 a productivity boom such as this, a rise in unemployment may not indicate
 increased slack. As such, our normal demand-side monetary policy may not
 be able to ameliorate an AI-caused unemployment spell without also increasing
 inflationary pressure. This means that monetary policymakers would face
 tradeoffs between unemployment and inflation."

→ Key implication: In an AI-driven labor market transition, a rise in
  unemployment may be structural (displacement) rather than cyclical
  (demand weakness). Standard rate-cut response to unemployment would be
  inappropriate — it would boost inflation without addressing the structural
  cause. This raises the bar for using rate cuts to respond to labor market
  softening if AI is the driver.

→ Policy signal: HAWKISH LEAN for the labor-inflation tradeoff. Cutting in
  response to AI-transition unemployment could be counterproductive.
  [Inference: Cook would need to distinguish cyclical from structural labor
  weakness before easing. This is an additional analytical filter on top of
  the standard dual-mandate framing.]

Current data reference:
  "the overall unemployment rate is still at a low 4.3 percent, and recent
   measures of layoffs remain subdued."
  → Cook characterizes the Feb 24 labor backdrop as benign (low unemployment,
    subdued layoffs). The AI-transition risk is a forward-looking concern, not
    a current diagnosis.

ISSUE 2 — NEUTRAL RATE ASSESSMENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"In anticipation of future productivity gains, we already see soaring
 AI-related business investment in data centers and chips, despite interest
 rates broadly being elevated relative to levels over the past 20 years.
 With investment contributing to strong aggregate demand, it is possible
 that the current neutral rate is higher than before the pandemic."

→ Cook explicitly raises the possibility that r* has risen due to AI-driven
  investment demand. If the neutral rate is higher than pre-AI estimates,
  then current rates (at the time: 3.50–3.75%) may be LESS restrictive than
  assumed. This is a hawkish implication — less room to cut before hitting
  neutral, and current stance may be only mildly restrictive.

  "This could reverse when the AI productivity gains are more fully realized
   or if the labor market transition leads to a rise in income inequality..."
  → Cook immediately qualifies: the r* elevation could be temporary. She
    holds this view with appropriate uncertainty.

[Inference: The r* observation is consistent with the committee-wide
concern (shared by Goolsbee's Feb 2026 framing that rates may "not even
be restrictive") but Cook applies an AI-specific mechanism. She is not
claiming rates are too low — she is flagging the analytical uncertainty
about how restrictive current policy actually is.]
