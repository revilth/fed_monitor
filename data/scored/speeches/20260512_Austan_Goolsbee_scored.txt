2026-05-12 | Austan D. Goolsbee | Chicago Fed President | Non Voter
"Fireside Chat — Greater Rockford Chamber of Commerce Luncheon"
Greater Rockford Chamber of Commerce, Rockford, Illinois
SOURCE: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=88dRnkmStR4

CLASSIFICATION: Type A (Economic Outlook) — fireside Q&A with moderator
  and audience questions. CPI released same morning (slight uptick);
  Goolsbee responds to live data in real time.

Reference FOMC:    April 29, 2026
Pre-FOMC baseline: March 23, 2026  "CNBC Interview" (Squawk Box)
Post-FOMC prior:   May 8, 2026  "Hoover Institution Panel" — Type B, no
                   directly comparable Type A content; effective prior for
                   Type A comparison is March 23.

GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  "For all the talk about AI data centers and the growth of technology
   and etc. The single strongest driver of growth of the US economy has
   been the relentless uptick of consumer spending. Just regular consumers
   with decent wage growth spending. That is what has driven the economy
   over the last year and a half."
  → Consumer spending as the growth engine — no compositional concern
    raised about quality of demand (unlike Barkin's observation about
    high-income concentration). He characterizes it as broad-based
    ("regular consumers with decent wage growth").

  Downside risk:
  "If people stop spending because they say, 'Hey, I need to build up a
   precautionary moat of savings in case something goes wrong.' I think
   the job market would stop being stable."
  → Consumer sentiment → precautionary saving → growth drag is the
    primary growth risk he names.

  Tax refund data point (Q&A): "50% of the tax refund going into savings
  [...] that feels higher than normal perhaps. And that wouldn't be a
  great sign."
  → Real-time behavioral flag. Not a forecast, but a leading indicator
    he notes with concern.

Direction: neutral lean dovish — growth is being driven by consumer
  spending but the precautionary saving risk is flagged explicitly.

Shift vs. prior (Mar 23):  DOVISH LEAN — Mar 23 was entirely focused on
  inflation and the energy shock with no growth characterization. Rockford
  introduces a specific growth risk (consumer sentiment / precautionary
  saving) that was absent in the prior appearance.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 23): Same as above.

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained slow." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Goolsbee version: "I have characterized the labor market overall as
    stable without being good."
  → Paraphrase of the committee's "slow" with a qualitative addition:
    "without being good." This is personal framing, not committee language.

Own verdict:
  "The four horsemen of the truth: the unemployment rate, the vacancy
   rate, the hiring rate, and the layoff rate. All four of those have been
   stable for almost a year and a half, but they say different things."
  → Novel framing. Not in any other official's language. Labels the
    four indicators he tracks as "the four horsemen of the truth" — implies
    other common metrics (payroll employment) are misleading.

Own qualifier — the paradox:
  "The hiring rate is extremely low, but has also been stable, but it's
   so low it would correspond in the past with the depths of a recession.
   Yet, the layoff rate is also extremely low. It's as low as it would be
   at the peak of a boom. So, everybody's scrambling — is that a sign that
   things are about to fall apart or is that a sign that we're measuring
   it wrong?"
  → Low-hire-low-fire paradox explicitly named. His interpretation: firms
    are in wait-and-see mode from uncertainty, not deterioration.

  "Businesses saying we just so much is happening. We're just gonna sit
   on our hand. We're not going to hire anybody. We're not going to let
   go of the people we have. We just want this to be resolved in the next
   six months."
  → "Next six months" resolution window — firms are implicitly pricing in
    a policy/tariff resolution by Q4 2026. If this fails to materialize,
    the stable labor market could deteriorate quickly.

  "I kind of think we're in a stable but not great labor market environment."
  → Overall verdict. Neutral, leaning cautious.

Shift vs. prior (Mar 23):  DOVISH LEAN — Mar 23 explicitly ranked
  "inflation's got to be a little ahead of the employment." Rockford
  does NOT re-rank inflation over labor. The four horsemen framing
  presents labor as genuinely stable (not deteriorating). This represents
  a softening of the explicit asymmetric risk statement from Mar 23.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 23): Same — the asymmetric ranking is absent.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Uncertainty about the economic outlook has increased further." [Apr 29]
  → Not directly echoed. Goolsbee focuses on data rather than
    committee language.

Own language — KEY (reacting to morning CPI):
  "Not only are we not making progress, it's going the wrong way. And
   not just in oil related things and not just in tariff related things.
   Now you've seen drifting upward of services inflation. And I keep
   highlighting for me that's the part that I'm nervous about. I want
   to see that at least stop growing and hopefully start going back down
   because that can't really be from oil prices. That's not really a
   big tariff component."
  → Services inflation drifting up — the critical signal. The key
    analytical point is that services cannot be attributed to oil or
    tariffs. If it's rising, it's a domestic demand / structural signal.
    This is the hardest inflation component to look through.

  "The unexpectedly disappointing part in my mind was on the services
   side. You continue to see this services going up."
  → Morning CPI real-time reaction. "Unexpectedly disappointing" suggests
    he did not anticipate this strength in services — it is new information
    even from his vantage point.

  "We've been above 2% for five years now... We believed that a lot of
   that was from tariffs... but then the inflation part would stop. Now,
   before that went away, we've added this oil price bit from the war.
   And not only are we not making progress, it's going the wrong way."
  → Cumulative shock framing: tariff residual + oil addition + services
    drift = three simultaneous inflation pressures. Consistent with Mar 23's
    "pile a new inflation shock on top of the old one" but adds services
    as a third dimension.

  Energy context: "Energy is way up. The [CPI headline was bad from it]."
  On core: "We mostly look at what we call core inflation, which means
   take the inflation and take out food and take out energy... if this
   conflict in the Middle East and the implications for oil prices do
   indeed prove to be passing, that should come down just as fast as it
   went up."
  → Conditional look-through on energy: IF the Middle East conflict proves
    transitory, energy-driven inflation reverses. This is not a commitment.
    The qualifier IF is doing heavy work.

Shift vs. prior (Mar 23):  HAWKISH LEAN — Mar 23 focused on gasoline/
  expectations transmission. Rockford adds services inflation as a NEW
  concern that cannot be attributed to oil or tariffs. This is a more
  difficult inflation signal than energy. The services concern is a
  meaningful escalation in his inflation assessment.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 23): Same — services concern is post-FOMC new
  information.

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "[The Committee is] attentive to the risks on both sides of its
   dual mandate." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Goolsbee does not echo this language directly. His framing in
    the closing statement is more conditionally optimistic.

Own language — CUT SIGNAL:
  "I remain optimistic and I was before the inflation started going up
   that rates can come down still a fair amount but we've got to see
   progress on inflation and if it were up to me we would kind of adopt
   that mindset."
  → Conditional cut signal. Two key elements:
    (1) "rates can come down still a fair amount" — this acknowledges
        a meaningful cutting path ahead; it is MORE optimistic than his
        Mar 23 "end-2026 possible but a wrench in plans."
    (2) "we've got to see progress on inflation" — the bar is inflation
        progress, not resolution. The conditional is firm.

  The "if it were up to me" framing is his personal view as a Non Voter
  in 2026 — he is not speaking for the committee.

On Fed independence (extended Q&A section):
  "I don't think Fed independence is truly under threat... The people
   who sit around the table take the job extremely seriously."
  "If we were to take away Fed independence of this narrow form... Inflation
   will come roaring back."
  → Strong institutional defense. No policy signals embedded.

Powell assessment:
  "I've been a big fan of Jay Powell's leadership. I consider him my first
   ballot hall of fame Fed chair."
  → Personal endorsement; analytically not significant for rate path.

Cut signal status: CONDITIONAL — "rates can come down still a fair amount"
  but conditional on inflation progress. The forward path is acknowledged
  as real; the bar is services and overall inflation showing progress.

  Notable: compared to Mar 23's "a wrench in the plans" formulation,
  the Rockford cut signal is LESS HAWKISH. Mar 23 suggested the energy
  shock threatened the cutting path. Rockford frames cuts as still available
  ("still a fair amount") pending progress. [Inference: the passage of time
  and additional data since Mar 23 may have stabilized his rate path view,
  even as inflation has not improved.]

Shift vs. prior (Mar 23):  DOVISH LEAN on cut signal — Mar 23 said the
  energy shock "throws a wrench into the plans." Rockford says "rates can
  come down still a fair amount" — implying the wrench has not permanently
  blocked the path, though the condition (progress on inflation) remains.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 23): Same — net softer on the cut path blockage,
  harder on services inflation concern.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    MIXED — hawkish on inflation (services new concern),
               dovish lean on cut signal (reinstates cutting path vs.
               Mar 23's "wrench in plans"), neutral-to-dovish on labor
               (drops Mar 23's explicit inflation-over-labor ranking).

Interpretation: Goolsbee is in a holding pattern. The services inflation
  data from the morning CPI is genuinely new and concerning to him, but
  he has not abandoned the conditional cutting path. The combination —
  acknowledging rates "can come down still a fair amount" while flagging
  services inflation as "the part I'm nervous about" — is consistent with a
  Non Voter who sees the path to cuts as available but blocked by the same
  services data he just received that morning.

Language watch:
  • "Stable without being good" — labor market verdict. Compact, quotable.
    Likely to be cited by media. Watch for spread to committee language.
  • "Four horsemen of the truth" (unemployment, vacancy, hiring, layoff
    rates) — novel analytical frame for labor assessment. If this framing
    spreads, it signals committee is relying on rates not on payroll counts.
  • "Services going the wrong way" / "that's the part I'm nervous about" —
    the services inflation concern is new and specific. If Paulson, Jefferson,
    or Williams adopt this language, it signals committee-wide hawkish
    recalibration on the hardest-to-look-through inflation component.
  • "Rates can come down still a fair amount" — softening of the cut
    blockage vs. Mar 23. This is the most dovish language from Goolsbee
    since the energy shock began. Conditional but notable.

Analyst flag: YES
  → Two competing signals in the same speech: services inflation is "going
    the wrong way" (hawkish new signal) AND "rates can come down still a
    fair amount" (dovish vs. prior). The analyst should assess: is the
    services concern strong enough to neutralize the dovish cut signal
    language, or is Goolsbee signaling that even with services inflation,
    the cutting path is intact? The CPI data is fresh (same morning) and his
    services concern may evolve as more data arrives.
