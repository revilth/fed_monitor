DALY — EMBEDDED POLICY METHODOLOGY (Non Voter, SF Fed President)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Daly's remarks present a framework for assessing whether to look through
supply shocks, using inflation dashboards. Her policy-relevant content:

On tariff look-through (pre-energy-shock baseline):
  "Only a small fraction of that dashboard was showing red or even
   showing pink. And it was just checking the work of is it really
   going to be a look through? And you do that and...this was the
   part that made it easier to have more confidence, not complete
   confidence, but more confidence that looking through was a
   reasonable strategy to have while you're being watchful."
  → Daly validated the tariff look-through as methodologically
    sound using her inflation dashboard as of the time of the speech.
    This is consistent with her May 8 (Hoover) stance: tariffs alone
    didn't flash the warning signals for persistence.

On the energy shock (current state, May 8):
  "Now we have the oil shock on top of that. And this is, you know,
   we only have data through April, so there's still more data to
   watch, but it's you're starting to see red in the areas that
   you'd expect. And one of the things I'm really keeping an eye on
   myself is not only the momentum index, but the New York Fed's
   global supply chain index."
  → The energy shock IS flagging persistence risks in her dashboard.
    She is not confident in look-through on the energy shock the way
    she was on tariffs alone. This is a hawkish lean from Daly on
    the inflation outlook for May 8.
  → Key watch variable she names: "if an oil price shock turns out
    to limit the supply chains...once they get clogged up, it takes
    a long time to bring them back...that's the things that could
    lead to more persistent pressure on inflation."

Net Daly signal from Hoover panel: NEUTRAL-TO-HAWKISH LEAN on
  inflation. She views tariff look-through as defensible but sees
  the stacked energy shock as starting to trigger persistence signals.
  No direct rate stance language. Consistent with her May 8 Daly

GOOLSBEE — ACADEMIC BACKGROUND (Non Voter, Chicago Fed)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Goolsbee's presentation: Expected vs. unexpected productivity booms
have opposite implications for interest rates.

Key theoretical result:
  — Unexpected productivity surge → rates should fall (lower
    marginal costs, inflation drops, Taylor rule: cut)
  — Expected/anticipated productivity surge → rates should rise
    (wealth effect pulls consumption forward before capacity
    expands; central bank must raise return on savings to prevent
    overheating)
  → Practical implication: "The bigger the share of the total
    productivity boom that is still on the way, the more likely
    it is that rates are going to need to rise."

Relevance to current cycle: If AI productivity gains are expected
  to be large and coming in the future (survey evidence suggests
  ~1pp/year for 10 years), the wealth-effect pull-forward argument
  supports higher rates, not lower. This is Goolsbee's academic
  case for why AI doesn't mechanically justify rate cuts. Note:
  this is a theoretical framework presentation, not a personal stance
  on current rates.

No direct current economic outlook signals. Consistent with
  Goolsbee's 2026 theme of skepticism about whether policy is even
  restrictive. No hawk-dove classification possible from this speech.
