"Labor Market Data: Signal or Noise?"
42nd Annual NABE Economic Policy Conference, Washington D.C.
SOURCE: https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/waller20260223a.htm

CLASSIFICATION: Type A — Economic Outlook (labor market focus)

Reference FOMC:    January 28, 2026
Pre-FOMC baseline: Jan 30, 2026  dissent statement (no policy speech scored

Post-FOMC prior:   N/A (first post-Jan28 economic outlook speech)

Dissent context: Waller dissented at Jan 28 preferring a 25bp cut.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  [No specific growth language in Jan 28 statement]
  → Waller: "Q4 GDP growth 1.4% at an annual rate. Private
    domestic final purchases increased by 2.4 percent."
    "I expect real GDP to grow above 2 percent for these
    six months [Q4 2025 + Q1 2026]."

Own language:
  "Business surveys indicate a pickup in activity in January.
   Manufacturing production increased 0.6 percent last month,
   the strongest reading in almost a year."
  → Solid growth backdrop. Not a concern.

Own addition — important nuance:
  "Business investment last year was largely due to data center
   construction and related investments — still a relatively
   narrow slice of the economy."
  → Flags concentration risk in the growth story. AI-driven
    investment is not broadly representative.

Consumer spending divergence:
  "Retailers continue to report a divergence between higher
   income shoppers, whose spending remains resilient, and
   lower- and middle-income customers, who are starting to
   spend less or switch to lower-cost goods and services."
  "I worry that the still-solid spending increases lately may
   be driven by stock-rich households and thus be masking
   weakness in the still substantial share of spending that
   relies on lower- and middle-income people."
  → Waller's key growth concern: aggregate spending looks fine
    but the composition is deteriorating. This is the seed of
    his demand destruction concern that becomes explicit in
    the Michigan survey reference on Apr 17.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC: N/A (baseline established)

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, and the unemployment rate
   has shown some signs of stabilization." [Jan 28 stmt]
  → Waller: "2025 was an extraordinarily weak one for job
    creation — the weakest outside of a recession since 2002."
    But January came in at +130,000 total / +172,000 private.

Own verdict — KEY:
  "I continue to believe that close to zero net job creation
   over 2025 indicates a weak, and fragile job market."
  → Despite the January surprise, his baseline is still
    fragile. The Jan reading is not yet a trend.

Own analytical framework:
  "One month of good news does not constitute a trend, but
   a year does."
  → His explicit standard: he needs sustained evidence of
    a labor market turn before updating his dissent view.

Own concern on January data quality:
  "I have some concerns that the report may contain more
   noise than signal." [Reasons: concentrated in healthcare
   + construction; ADP showed only 22K; Revelio showed 3K;
   Challenger counted 108K layoff announcements]
  → Deep skepticism about the January headline. He is
    not persuaded the labor market has turned a corner.

Own verdict on uncertainty:
  "I rate these two possible outcomes [signal vs. noise]
   as close to a coin flip."
  → Explicit 50/50 on whether the labor market has improved.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC: N/A (baseline established)

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Jan 28 stmt]
  → Waller: "PCE inflation...around 2.8 percent...core
    about 3 percent."

Own language — KEY FRAME:
  "Traditional central bank wisdom suggests that we should
   'look through' tariffs. I did this when they went up and
   will do so if they come down."
  → Explicit "look through" doctrine. No second-round
    effects = tariffs are one-time. This is Waller's
    foundational inflation premise at this date.

  "Underlying inflation — inflation without the effects of
   tariffs — is close to the FOMC's 2 percent goal."
  → His key assertion: strip out tariffs and inflation is
    already at target. This is the dovish anchor that makes
    his Jan 28 dissent coherent.

NOTE: No energy shock framing. No "sequence of transitory
  shocks." The "look through" doctrine is intact and
  unchallenged at this date. This is the critical baseline
  to compare against Apr 17.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC: N/A (baseline established)

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY — TWO SCENARIOS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Maintain target range" → Waller: held at Jan 28 but
  dissented. Explains his reasoning here.

Own framework — EXPLICIT TWO-SCENARIO ANALYSIS:

  SCENARIO 1 (Jan data is signal → labor turned a corner):
  "If the labor market data for February are consistent
   with the stronger job creation...it may be appropriate
   to hold the FOMC's policy rate at current levels."
  → Holds if labor has genuinely improved.

  SCENARIO 2 (Jan data is noise → labor still weak):
  "If the good labor market news of January is revised away
   or evaporates in February, it would support my position
   at the FOMC's last meeting, that a 25-basis-point
   reduction in the policy rate was appropriate."
  → Cuts if January is revised down or February disappoints.

  "I rate these two possible outcomes as close to a coin flip."
  → At this point, Waller is symmetrically uncertain.
    Labor is the deciding variable; inflation (ex-tariffs)
    he considers already near target.

Cut signal status: CONDITIONAL CUT SIGNAL — in the bad
  labor scenario, he would cut. In the good scenario, hold.
  His prior is dovish (dissented to cut) but he is updating
  on incoming data.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL-DOVISH — dissenter framing; "coin flip"
               on labor; explicit cut conditional on weak data

Directional:   At this date, Waller's framework is entirely
  labor-driven. Underlying inflation is "close to 2%" in his
  view (ex-tariffs), so inflation is not a constraint on cuts.
  The "look through" doctrine for tariffs is intact. The only
  question is whether the labor market has turned a corner.

CRITICAL CONTRAST WITH APR 17:
  By Apr 17, all of this has changed:
  • "Look through" tariffs → replaced by "sequence of
    transitory shocks / more vigilant"
  • "Coin flip on labor" → replaced by "hold even if labor
    weakens" in adverse scenario
  • Dovish dissenter → committee's most hawkish voter
  The Middle East energy shock (late Feb / early March) and
  a Feb jobs report that did NOT confirm the Jan turnaround
  are the two factors that drove the full reversal.

Language watch:
  • "Look through" tariffs — Waller's baseline doctrine.
    By Apr 17 he has qualified this significantly. Track
    the evolution as the key measure of how much the energy
    shock has changed his fundamental framework.
  • "Underlying inflation close to 2%" — his dovish inflation
    anchor. Still present in Apr 17 but now qualified by the
    "sequence of shocks" concern.

Analyst flag: YES
  → The Feb 23 → Apr 17 arc is the most dramatic individual
    speaker shift in 2026. Analyst to assess: what specifically
    happened between Feb 23 and Apr 17? The Feb jobs report
    came in weak (confirming Jan was partly noise), and the
    energy shock began in late February. Both factors together
    explain the full reversal from "coin flip, lean toward cut"
    to "hold even if labor weakens."
