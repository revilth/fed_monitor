INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Paulson: "Inflation has been above our 2-percent target
    for six years. While we've made significant progress,
    inflation is still 2.8 percent, not 2 percent."

Own language — KEY:
  "If inflation were at the 2-percent target, I would feel
   more comfortable being patient, keeping monetary policy
   on hold and waiting to see if a hypothetical growth surge
   puts upward pressure on inflation. But if inflation is
   above 2 percent and has been for some time, I would be
   more cautious."
  → MOST IMPORTANT SENTENCE. Paulson's explicit inflation-first
    bar: at 2.8% PCE, she is NOT in the patience zone. She
    needs 2% before she can comfortably hold through a growth
    surge without tightening. More restrictive than Jefferson's
    symmetric dual-risk framing.

  "Long-term inflation expectations are consistent with 2
   percent, but they may also be a little more fragile."
  → Unusual qualifier: "a little more fragile" on expectations.
    All other officials say expectations are "well anchored."
    This is Paulson's personal note of caution on the dovish
    anchor that other officials rely on most heavily.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC: ESTABLISHING BASELINE — key finding is
  that Paulson has a higher inflation bar than the committee
  median (Jefferson's symmetric framing).

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Well positioned" → Paulson implicitly endorses hold.

Own language — KEY:
  "We are better able to calibrate monetary policy when we
   start from a good place."
  → Her formulation: "a good place" = 2% inflation.
    Since we're not there, the implicit message is:
    get to 2% before becoming flexible.

  "Getting inflation to 2 percent and keeping the
   unemployment rate low creates...credibility and
   flexibility that help us navigate the future."
  → Credibility first, flexibility second. Hawkish ordering.

  "My current estimate of longer-run R* is close to the
   SEP median of 3.1 percent. But I could see that number
   being revised up, or possibly even down."
  → Open R* — balanced but leans toward upside revision
    in the high-productivity scenario. Implies policy may
    already be close to neutral at some R* estimates.

Cut signal status: ABSENT — explicit inflation-first bar
  prevents any forward guidance on cuts.
