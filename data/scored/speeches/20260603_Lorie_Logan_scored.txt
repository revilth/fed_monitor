GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Economic activity has been expanding at a solid pace." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Logan: "Economic activity remains strong." — slightly more emphatic
    characterization than the statement.

Own language:
  "Consumer spending is robust, partly supported by wealthy households'
   investment gains. Although higher energy prices have weighed on
   lower-income households, the U.S. economy as a whole has weathered
   the shock so far."
  → Acknowledges distributional split but characterizes aggregate
    as resilient. No demand-side growth risk flagged.

  "In the aggregate, corporate earnings are going gangbusters.
   S&P 500 companies' earnings grew more than 25 percent in the
   first quarter compared with a year earlier. While tech companies
   experienced some of the strongest growth, gains are widespread.
   The median S&P 500 company's earnings rose 14 percent year on year."
  → Broad-based earnings strength, not just tech-driven. Removes the
    compositional-growth-risk argument.

  "Financial conditions are accommodative. And AI investment continues
   to boom."
  → HAWKISH INFERENCE: "accommodative" financial conditions mirror
    Hammack's Jun 2 "supportive of growth rather than holding it back"
    — two voters independently characterizing conditions as permissive
    within 24 hours. [Note: Logan does not make the real-rate argument
    explicit, unlike Hammack; she uses "accommodative" as a descriptive
    judgment, not a calculation.]

  "Productivity improvements from AI could eventually reduce inflation.
   However, the potential size and timing of those gains are uncertain.
   The demand is here already."
  → Qualifies the AI-as-disinflationary argument: benefit uncertain
    and deferred; demand (inflationary channel) is immediate.

Shift vs. prior speech (May 1 dissent): N/A — no growth section in May 1.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 10): UNCHANGED — Feb 10: "economic activity has
  rebounded strongly... bolstered by strong consumer spending and business
  investment." Consistent resilient read; "accommodative" financial
  conditions is new language in 2026 for Logan.

Direction: neutral-to-hawkish — strong growth, accommodative conditions,
  no demand-side risk flagged.

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, on average, and the unemployment rate
   has been little changed in recent months." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Logan does NOT echo the low-job-gains framing. Her characterization
    diverges from the statement.

Own verdict:
  "The labor market appears stable and broadly balanced."
  → Identical read to May 1 dissent statement ("stable, with low
    unemployment and payroll job gains keeping pace with labor force
    growth") and May 10 commencement ("labor market is in balance").
    Continuity of characterization across three consecutive speeches.

Own quantification:
  "The unemployment rate has hovered around 4.3 percent for the past
   year. Employers are adding an average of about 50,000 jobs per month.
   That might sound low, but it's in line with the slow growth rate of
   the labor force."
  → Logan explicitly REFRAMES what the statement frames as weakness
    (low job gains) as consistency with full employment. This is a
    deliberate analytical divergence from the committee framing.

KEY INFERENCE:
  "These conditions indicate that monetary policy is not restraining
   the economy."
  → [Inference: Logan is drawing from labor + growth + financial
    conditions jointly to argue that current rates are not binding.
    This is not stated as an explicit theorem but is the logical
    conclusion of her characterization of both sides of the economy
    as resilient and conditions as "accommodative."] Functionally
    equivalent to Hammack's "policy may not be restrictive."

Shift vs. prior speech (May 1):  UNCHANGED — "stable" and "payroll
  gains keeping pace with labor force growth" = same as "50,000/month
  consistent with slow labor force growth." No shift.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 10):     UNCHANGED — Feb 10: "labor market
  now appears to be stabilizing, and the downside risks appear to have
  meaningfully dissipated." Consistent non-dovish labor view across
  all of 2026.

Direction: neutral-to-hawkish — stable/balanced framing removes the
  dovish leg of the dual-mandate argument; "not restraining" inference
  supports the case for tighter policy.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase in
   global energy prices." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Logan does NOT echo the energy attribution framing.

Opening framing — KEY:
  "Inflation is taking too long to return to the FOMC's 2 percent
   target."
  → Duration emphasis: not "elevated," but "taking too long." Same
    logic as May 1 ("increasingly concerned about how long it will
    take inflation to return all the way to the 2 percent target").
    The emphasis is on the timeline failure, not just the level.

Quantitative evidence:
  Headline PCE: "close to 4 percent over the past 12 months"
  Core PCE:     3.3% past year
  Dallas Fed trimmed mean: 2.3% past year
  Cleveland Fed median PCE: 2.8% past year
  NY Fed multivariate core trend: "moved above 3 percent this year"

TRIMMED MEAN CAVEAT — ANALYTICALLY IMPORTANT:
  "my staff's research cautions against putting too much stock in
   low readings of the trimmed mean. A change in the mix of price
   increases and decreases is causing the trimmed mean to drop too
   many price increases. That can pull the trimmed mean below the
   underlying trend in inflation."
  → Logan is explicitly setting aside HER OWN bank's trimmed mean
    (which reads 2.3%) in favor of higher measures. She is identifying
    a technical artifact that makes inflation look lower than it is.
    This is intellectually honest and significantly hawkish: even the
    metric most favorable to the dovish case (Dallas trimmed mean)
    should not be trusted, per Logan's own researchers.

Tariff attribution — KEY ANALYTICAL SEPARATION:
  "Dallas Fed researchers have also estimated the amount of inflation
   directly attributable to tariff increases. As tariff rates
   stabilize, they will remain a factor in the level of prices. But
   they should not contribute further to inflation, which is the rate
   of increase in prices."
  → Logan isolates tariff inflation to level effects, not ongoing
    rate effects. This is consistent with her May 1 argument that
    "underlying inflation was already a concern before the conflict."
    Both energy and tariffs are additive to, not the cause of, the
    underlying inflation problem.

VERDICT — KEY:
  "Putting together all these different analyses and ways of looking
   at the data, inflation appears to be trending toward the mid 2's
   — not all the way back to 2 percent."
  → EXPLICIT ABOVE-2% INFLATION FORECAST. This is the most direct
    statement from any FOMC voter that 2% will not be achieved under
    current conditions. Previous formulations were uncertainty-framing
    ("not yet fully confident," "taking too long") — Logan now
    provides a positive forecast: settling in the "mid 2's."
    This changes the analytical baseline: she is not arguing for
    patience while inflation returns to 2%; she is forecasting it
    will not return to 2% without policy action.

Inflation expectations watch:
  "I am closely watching movements in market prices for short-term
   and long-term inflation compensation, as well as surveys of
   inflation expectations."
  → Flags de-anchoring risk without explicitly saying expectations
    have moved. Consistent with Hammack's "sixth year of elevated
    inflation" de-anchoring concern (Jun 2).

Shift vs. prior speech (May 10 commencement): HAWKISH lean — May 10:
  "inflation is still too high." Jun 3 adds multi-measure quantitative
  analysis, trimmed-mean caveat, tariff level vs. rate separation,
  and explicit above-2% forecast. The qualitative direction is the
  same, but Jun 3 provides the full analytical architecture.
Shift vs. prior speech (May 1 dissent): HAWKISH lean — May 1:
  "increasingly concerned about how long it will take inflation to
  return all the way to 2 percent." Jun 3 upgrades to an explicit
  forecast that it will NOT return all the way to 2%: "trending
  toward the mid 2's — not all the way back to 2 percent." This is
  a meaningful escalation: uncertainty about the path → explicit
  pessimistic forecast.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 10): HAWKISH — Feb 10: "Not yet fully
  confident inflation is heading all the way back to 2 percent."
  Jun 3: explicit forecast of settling above 2%. The uncertainty
  framing has resolved to a bearish inflation forecast.

Direction: HAWKISH — explicit above-2% forecast; trimmed-mean
  caveat disables the dovish metric; tariff/energy treated as
  additive to a pre-existing inflation problem.

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "The Committee will continue to monitor... remains prepared to
   adjust policy if risks emerge threatening its dual mandate
   objectives." [Apr 29 stmt — no easing bias; Logan opposed it]
  → Logan does NOT echo this language.

Own language — KEY:
  "I am increasingly concerned that higher interest rates could be
   necessary later this year to fully restore price stability and
   appropriately balance both sides of the Fed's dual mandate."
  → CONDITIONAL HIKE SIGNAL — FIRST EXPLICIT FROM LOGAN.
    Component analysis:
    "increasingly concerned" — escalation language; not stable worry
    "higher interest rates" — hike, not hold-and-wait
    "could be necessary" — conditional (not imminent); calibrated
    "later this year" — temporal qualifier; not "soon" but within 2026
    "fully restore price stability" — implies current stance
      insufficient for the inflation side of the mandate
    "appropriately balance both sides" — dual mandate framing; she
      argues a hike serves BOTH goals given the labor/growth picture

  Note on scenario framing: Logan's hike signal is DESCRIPTIVE —
    she does not name explicit scenarios (cf. Waller Apr 17's
    "BENIGN/ADVERSE" labels or Hammack Jun 2's "Scenario 1/2"
    framework). Her framing is a general concern, not a pre-committed
    scenario response.

Cut signal status: ABSENT — hike signal present (conditional: "could
  be necessary later this year"). Symmetric framing from May 1 ("either
  an increase or a cut") has resolved hawkish.

Shift vs. prior speech (May 1 dissent): HAWKISH — SIGNIFICANT.
  May 1: "Depending on which scenario materializes, it could
  plausibly be appropriate for the FOMC's next rate change to be
  either an increase or a cut." Symmetric framing. Jun 3: "higher
  interest rates could be necessary later this year." The hike is
  no longer symmetrically possible — it is the primary concern.
  The shift from symmetric to asymmetric-hawkish is the single most
  important directional change in this speech.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 10): HAWKISH — Feb 10: "I am more worried
  about inflation remaining stubbornly high" — asymmetric worry but
  no explicit hike signal. Jun 3 adds the explicit hike signal. Full
  trajectory: Feb 10 hawkish worry → May 1 symmetric framing →
  Jun 3 explicit hike concern.

Direction: HAWKISH — first explicit hike signal from Logan; symmetric
  policy framing from May 1 resolved hawkish.
