GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed: not directly invoked
  → Bowman does not echo the "solid" activity characterization.
    She frames growth as resilient-but-moderating.

Own language:
  "The U.S. economy has been resilient, but the labor market
   remains vulnerable to adverse shocks and PCE inflation has
   moved up, due largely to higher energy prices."
  — Opening sentence of the current assessment; frames the situation
    as three concurrent challenges (resilience, labor vulnerability,
    energy-driven inflation) rather than the committee's more
    uniform "solid activity" framing.

  "real GDP growth has been rising at a moderate to solid pace,
   supported by strong AI-related business investment. Growth has
   slowed since last summer, as both softness in real personal
   income and the severe winter weather have weighed on consumer
   spending."
  — Moderate-to-solid; consumer spending is the drag, AI investment
    the offset. Consistent with other post-FOMC speakers.

  "Supportive supply-side policies, including less restrictive
   regulations and lower business taxes, will also likely favor
   these conditions."
  — Unique addition: Bowman explicitly credits deregulation and tax
    cuts as growth-supportive. No other scored speaker has made
    this framing. [Inference: she is more aligned with the
    administration's supply-side narrative than other officials.]

Shift vs. prior speech (Jan 30, 2026):
  NEUTRAL — Jan 30 flagged Q4 deceleration; now describes
  "moderate to solid" pace. The framing is broadly similar.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Jan 30, 2026):
  NEUTRAL — same.

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed: not directly invoked

Own verdict:
  "the labor market appears to have become more stable in recent
   months, there are still signs of fragility."
  — KEY SHIFT: In January 30, Bowman used "fragile" without
    qualification, calling it "increasingly fragile" and warning
    of rapid deterioration risk. Here she upgrades to "more stable"
    while retaining the "fragility" qualifier. This is her most
    constructive labor framing since joining the Board.

Own qualifier / additions:
  "The unemployment rate remained at 4.3 percent in April, with
   stronger payroll employment gains so far this year."
  — Acknowledges the stabilization in hard data.

  "The job-finding rate, which measures the percentage of
   unemployed workers that are able to find a job, has continued
   on a downward path in recent months."
  — Soft-data warning held: the flow into jobs from unemployment
    is still declining, pointing to deteriorating labor market
    dynamics beneath the stable U rate.

  "employment growth has been concentrated in less cyclically
   sensitive industries like health care and social assistance"
  — Compositional concern: not broad-based; narrow sectoral
    concentration is a fragility signal.

  "the share of long-term unemployed remains around 25 percent,
   reflecting a labor market with low dynamism"
  — Low dynamism framing echoes her Jan 30 "labor market can
    appear stable right up until it isn't" structural concern.
    [Inference: she still sees the labor market as fragile beneath
    the surface stabilization.]

Shift vs. prior speech (Jan 30, 2026):
  HAWKISH LEAN (less dovish) — "more stable" replaces "increasingly
  fragile and could continue to deteriorate." The alarm level has
  dropped. Stabilization in hard data acknowledged.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Jan 30, 2026):
  HAWKISH LEAN — same direction.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed: not directly invoked explicitly,
  but her framing is consistent with the committee's look-through
  stance on energy.

Own language — KEY:
  "Progress on lowering inflation appears to have stalled, with
   measures of PCE inflation moving up recently. On a 12-month
   basis, total PCE inflation picked up to 3.8 percent in April,
   mostly reflecting higher gasoline and fuel oil prices, and
   core PCE inflation rose further to 3.3 percent last month,
   up noticeably since September."
  — ACKNOWLEDGES deterioration. In January 30, she never saw these
    headline numbers (Jan PCE was reported in Feb). She now confronts
    3.8% total and 3.3% core directly. This is her most explicit
    acknowledgment of inflation worsening.

  "trimmed mean PCE measure, indicate that core inflation has
   moved down closer to 2 percent since last September."
  — MAINTAINS look-through anchor: underlying inflation (trimmed
    mean) is heading toward 2% even as headline deteriorates.

  "After removing these one-off factors [tariffs + software prices],
   core PCE inflation would have continued to hover only a bit
   above 2 percent, reflecting no pressures from the labor market
   and weakness in market rents."
  — CONSISTENT with her Jan 30 formulation ("After removing these
    [tariff] effects, core PCE inflation would have hovered close
    to 2 percent"). She is applying the SAME analytical methodology
    as January but arriving at "a bit above 2 percent" rather than
    "close to 2 percent" — a slight but meaningful upward revision
    to underlying inflation.

  "I am optimistic that, once the conflict is resolved, supply
   disruptions will ease, leaving a temporary imprint in PCE
   inflation and minimal impacts on domestic economic activity."
  — Conditional look-through on Iran energy shock: temporary,
    not structural. Most dovish interpretation of the shock on
    the committee.

  Risk qualifier:
  "But, should disruptions persist well into the second half of
   the year, we could start to see broader effects on inflation."
  — Escape valve: she is not fully committed to the look-through
    if the energy shock proves persistent.

Shift vs. prior speech (Jan 30, 2026):
  HAWKISH LEAN (less dovish) — Jan 30 described underlying
  inflation as "close to 2 percent" and "high confidence in
  disinflation." Now: "a bit above 2 percent" and a new risk
  qualifier on Iran energy persistence. The confidence level
  has declined; the underlying inflation estimate has nudged up.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Jan 30, 2026):
  HAWKISH LEAN — same.

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "additional policy rate adjustments" (Apr 29, 2026 statement
   — the easing bias language that Logan, Hammack, Kashkari
   dissented against on April 29)
  → Speaker version: "I appreciated and supported keeping the
    language from the March post-meeting statement about additional
    policy rate adjustments."
  — EXPLICIT EASING BIAS ENDORSEMENT. Bowman is the only scored
    voter who has explicitly stated she supported keeping the easing
    bias language after April 29. This is a significant signal:
    she is anchoring the committee's dovish minority against
    Waller/Cook's push for hike language and the hawkish
    dissenters' vote against the easing bias altogether.

Own language:
  "our moderately restrictive policy stance is intended to help
   maintain stable labor market conditions while allowing
   inflation to resume its downward trend toward 2 percent
   once the effects of tariffs and oil prices dissipate."
  — "Moderately restrictive" CARRIED FORWARD from January 30.
    This is Bowman's unique marker: every other scored voter
    uses "well positioned," "close to neutral," or equivalent
    holding language. Bowman continues to characterize policy
    as restrictive with room to ease.

  "It is appropriate to look through temporarily elevated
   inflation readings largely due to higher energy prices,
   provided that we remain credible in our commitment to achieve
   our inflation goal and one-off tariff effects wane, as I expect."
  — Look-through with a credibility proviso. Not unconditional.

  Conditional risk:
  "the more persistent higher oil prices are — or if we start
   to see broader effects of higher energy prices on PCE inflation
   — the more likely I will consider shifting my approach to
   thinking about the balance of risks."
  — This is her only hawkish warning: she can be moved if the
    energy shock broadens. But the threshold is persistence plus
    broader second-round effects — a high bar.

  "Before our next meeting in June, we will have received one
   additional month of employment and inflation data. In the
   meantime, I will continue to evaluate the incoming data..."
  — Standard data-dependent language; no forward commitment.

Cut signal status: RETRACTED — January 30 had an active cut
  advocacy signal ("ready to adjust... closer to neutral") and
  a 3-cut SEP disclosure. This speech mentions no cuts, no SEP,
  and no cut trajectory. The easing bias endorsement is NOT a
  cut signal; it signals she prefers the option to cut over the
  option to hike, but she makes no timing commitment.

[No hike signal — explicitly inconsistent with Waller/Cook framing]

Shift vs. prior speech (Jan 30, 2026):
  HAWKISH LEAN (less dovish) — cut signal retracted; SEP disclosure
  dropped; no "ready to adjust" language. She now waits for data
  rather than advocating action. The easing bias endorsement
  replaces active cut advocacy as her dovish marker.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Jan 30, 2026):
  HAWKISH LEAN — same.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH LEAN (vs. prior) / DOVISH LEAN (vs. committee)
               Bowman has moved hawkish relative to her January 30
               stance — labor is "more stable," inflation is "stalled"
               rather than "descending toward 2%," and her 3-cut SEP
               advocacy has been replaced by data-dependent waiting.
               However, she remains the most dovish voter on the
               current committee: the only voter explicitly endorsing
               the easing bias language, the only voter describing
               policy as "moderately restrictive," and the only voter
               applying an aggressive look-through to energy inflation.

               Her core analytical move: tariff-adjusted/trimmed-mean
               inflation remains the focal variable (≈2%), energy is
               temporary, labor has stabilized (though fragility
               remains beneath the surface). Under this framework,
               the conditions for cuts are not yet met but the
               conditions for hikes are not met either — she is
               genuinely in a wait-and-see position.

               The critical committee context: Waller and Cook have
               explicit hike signals; Logan, Jefferson, and Paulson
               are hawkish lean. Bowman is the only voter pushing
               in the opposite direction. Her endorsement of the
               easing bias provides the committee's June 18 statement
               with at least one voter anchor for the existing language —
               but she is in a minority position relative to the vocal
               hike camp.

Language watch:
  • "moderately restrictive" — UNIQUE to Bowman; all other voters
    use neutral/hold language. If this spreads, it signals a dovish
    committee shift. Currently isolated.
  • "I appreciated and supported keeping [easing bias] language"
    — UNIQUE; first explicit post-FOMC easing bias endorsement from
    any voter. Analytically important as the dovish counterweight
    to Waller/Cook hike advocacy. Not a cut signal.
  • "a bit above 2 percent" (underlying inflation) — slight upward
    revision from her Jan 30 "close to 2 percent." Consistent
    direction of travel with the rest of the committee, but she
    remains at the most dovish end of the inflation assessment.
  • "low dynamism" (labor market) — new phrasing for structural
    fragility; more nuanced than prior "fragile" language.
