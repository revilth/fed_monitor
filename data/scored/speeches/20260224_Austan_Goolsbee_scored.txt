LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, and the unemployment rate has
   shown some signs of stabilization." [Jan 28 stmt]
  → Goolsbee: "unemployment rate...4.3%...basically been
    steady for a year...rate-based measures have shown
    steadiness at levels comparable to normal expansions."

Own verdict — KEY:
  "The job market has been steady."
  "I don't think that says much about the business cycle or
   labor market slack."
  → Confident characterization: labor is fine. No downside
    risk language. Very different from Bowman's fragility frame.

Own analytical contribution:
  "Low hiring with low firing is not a normal business cycle
   combination. But it is exactly the combination you would
   expect if businesses are dealing with heightened uncertainty."
  → Uncertainty explanation for low-hire/low-fire: it's not
    weakness, it's caution. Removes the labor case for cuts.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Jan 28 stmt]
  → Goolsbee: "Core PCE inflation for the year was around 3%.
    We made progress in 2023 and 2024, but that progress
    stalled in 2025."

Own language — KEY:
  "With inflation at 3%, it is not obvious that our interest
   rate policy is even restrictive. The real federal funds
   rate measured that way is pretty close or even a bit below
   the consensus view of long-run r*."
  → Joins Logan in questioning whether rates are restrictive.
    This is the single most important sentence from a voter
    in 2026 on the neutral-rate debate.

  "Core PCE services excluding housing inflation has been
   running stubbornly high, at 3.3%, over the past year.
   That is very unlikely to have come from tariffs, and it's
   harder to make an optimistic case that high services
   inflation is just transitory. So we need to be vigilant."
  → Hawkish on services inflation specifically. He is NOT
    comfortably looking through non-tariff inflation.

  "Since we have been burned by assuming transitory inflation
   before and since there are plausible scenarios where
   inflation again proves more persistent than we forecasted,
   we should be careful not to put ourselves in a difficult
   position."
  → Explicit 2021 lesson: do not reflexively look through.
    This is the same intellectual frame as Waller's "sequence
    of transitory shocks" (Apr 17), emerging two months earlier.

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed: none

Own language — KEY:
  "I feel that front-loading too many rate cuts is not
   prudent in that circumstance."
  "Before we cut rates more to stimulate the economy, let's
   be sure inflation is heading back to 2%."
  → Explicit conditions for further cuts: confirmed disinflation
    progress first. No forward guidance on timing.

  "I remain optimistic that there can be more rate cuts this
   year. But that hinges on seeing actual progress on inflation
   that shows we are on a path back to 2%."
  → Conditional dovishness: cuts possible, but conditional
    on data. Not the unconditional dovish framing of his 2024
    reputation.

Cut signal status: CONDITIONAL ONLY — cuts possible but
  require confirmed inflation progress. "Not prudent" to
  front-load.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL-HAWKISH — significantly more cautious
               than his public reputation; joins Logan on
               neutral-rate question from a voter

Directional:   The "Goolsbee is dovish, watch him for cut
  signals" heuristic is outdated for 2026. He joins Logan and
  Barkin in questioning whether rates are even restrictive,
  explicitly warns against front-loading cuts, and flags
  services inflation as genuinely sticky. His conditional
  openness to cuts requires confirmed disinflation — a
  higher bar than committee boilerplate.

Language watch:
  • "Not obvious our interest rate policy is even restrictive"
    — a voter using Logan-style language. This is the
    committee's emerging hawkish fault line.
  • "Front-loading too many rate cuts is not prudent" — his
    explicit policy constraint. Track for spread.
  • Services inflation "harder to make an optimistic case"
    — anticipates the "sequence of transitory shocks" frame.
