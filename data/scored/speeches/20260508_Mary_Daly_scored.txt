──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is the core of Daly's speech. She presents an inflation dashboard
methodology and applies it to the current tariff and oil shocks.

FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Uncertainty about the economic outlook has increased further."
  [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Daly version: "How do you resolve the models are our benchmarks... but
    then history has to be disciplined with the incoming information."
  → Her methodology IS the committee's uncertainty response made operational.

Own language — TARIFF SHOCK assessment:
  "Looking at this measure, this dashboard and many others... only a small
   fraction of that dashboard was showing red or even showing pink. And it
   was just checking the work of is it really going to be a look through?
   And you do that and you know we go out and talk to businesses too and
   ask them about how much they're passing through. But this was the part
   that made it easier to have more confidence, not complete confidence, but
   more confidence that looking through was a reasonable strategy to have
   while you're being watchful that something else might change."
  → KEY: "more confidence, not complete confidence, that looking through
    was a reasonable strategy" — qualified endorsement of look-through on
    tariffs, with explicit watchfulness caveat.

Own language — OIL SHOCK addition (the material shift):
  "Now we have the oil shock on top of that. And this is, we only have
   data through April, so there's still more data to watch, but you're
   starting to see red in the areas that you'd expect."
  → KEY: "starting to see red in the areas you'd expect" — the dashboard
    is now flagging concern. The look-through confidence has eroded with
    the addition of the oil shock.

  "One of the things I'm really keeping an eye on myself is not only the
   momentum index, but the New York Fed's global supply chain index...
   if an oil price shock turns out to limit the supply chains and then it
   takes, we've learned about supply chains is once they get clogged up, it
   takes a long time to bring them back. And so that's the things that could
   lead to more persistent pressure on inflation."
  → Supply chain transmission channel is her key monitoring variable.
    Explicit mechanism for why the oil shock could become persistent.

  "And this doesn't say you're going to have more persistent inflation. It
   says you're going to have more persistent inflation pressure."
  → Careful hedge: pressure ≠ realized persistence. She's not declaring
    a second inflation episode, but flagging the risk channel.

Scenario framing type: DESCRIPTIVE (not explicit/named). Daly describes
  the dashboard turning red and names the supply chain transmission channel
  as a mechanism for persistence, but does not pre-commit to a named policy
  response in either scenario. Contrast with Waller (Apr 17): BENIGN/ADVERSE
  with named policy responses = explicit. Daly = descriptive risk language.

Direction: HAWKISH LEAN — tariff look-through endorsed but the oil shock

What makes this speech analytically significant:
  Daly makes the committee's "watchfulness" language operational. She
  describes the exact mechanism by which she would shift from "look through"
  to "respond": the dashboard. The tariff shock passed the look-through test.
  The oil shock is now marginal — "starting to see red." The supply chain
  index is her tripwire.

This is the clearest articulation by any official of what would cause the
committee to abandon the look-through stance: not inflation itself, but the
supply chain transmission index turning red persistently.

Language watch:
  • "Starting to see red in areas you'd expect" — dashboard language applied
    to the oil shock. If this spreads to other officials, it signals the
    committee's look-through tolerance is eroding.
  • "More persistent inflation pressure" (vs. "more persistent inflation") —
    precise distinction that other officials are not drawing explicitly.
  • NY Fed global supply chain index — her named tripwire. Worth tracking.

  → Daly articulates the look-through tripwire more precisely than any other
