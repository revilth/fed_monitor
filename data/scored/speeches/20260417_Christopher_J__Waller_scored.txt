LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, and the unemployment rate has
   been little changed in recent months." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Waller: "Payroll gains have alternated between positive
    and negative numbers for the past 10 months."

Own verdict:
  "I am going to have to get used to payroll numbers that
   are lower than I am accustomed to seeing in a growing
   economy."
  → Recalibrates the break-even: lower immigration means
    lower job growth is consistent with a stable labor market.
    This is a dovish framing of low payrolls — rejects the
    idea that low job growth signals recession risk.

Own qualifier (hawkish):
  "The University of Michigan Surveys of Consumers last week
   reported its lowest-ever reading for consumer confidence...
   there may be a threshold crossed at which point they start
   to economize."
  → Acknowledges demand destruction risk from prolonged energy
    shock; this is the labor downside scenario.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 23):   UNABLE TO ASSESS — Feb 23
  unscored; establishing post-FOMC baseline.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Waller: echoes committee framing on tariff pass-through
    and energy price increases.

Own language — KEY (hawkish framework):
  "Learning from that experience [2021-22], I will be cautious
   when faced with a sequence of transitory shocks. While
   intellectually it makes sense to look through each shock,
   with a sequence of shocks, policymakers need to be more
   vigilant."
  → MOST IMPORTANT SENTENCE. "Sequence of transitory shocks"
    is a new analytical framework: don't automatically look
    through inflation when shocks stack on top of each other.
    This is explicitly drawing the 2021-22 lesson and raising
    his personal bar for "looking through" inflation.

  "There is a risk that prolonged high energy prices and
   secondary effects...eventually do change the expectations
   of firms and consumers, who I note have seen inflation
   above 2 percent now for five years."
  → Five-year framing + expectations vulnerability = hawkish.
    Higher bar for dismissing energy shock as transitory.

Own dovish anchor:
  "Using research by the Federal Reserve staff...underlying
   inflation — by which I mean excluding tariff effects —
   is running close to 2 percent."
  → Strips out tariffs and finds structural disinflation
    story intact. Preserves a dovish floor even while
    raising his vigilance framework.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC: ESTABLISHING POST-FOMC BASELINE —
  "sequence of transitory shocks" framework is new and
  hawkish vs. his earlier stance.

MONETARY POLICY — TWO EXPLICIT SCENARIOS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  → Waller: references the committee's "hold" decision.

Own language — SCENARIO ANALYSIS (unique in 2026):

  BENIGN SCENARIO (energy shock resolves):
  "If the Strait of Hormuz opens...I was more inclined toward
   cuts to support the labor market later this year when the
   outlook is more steady."
  → Conditional dovish signal: cuts possible in 2026 IF
    energy resolves. No other voter has given this explicit
    conditional.

  ADVERSE SCENARIO (energy shock persists):
  "In this case [prolonged conflict]...that may mean
   maintaining the policy rate at the current target range
   if the risks to inflation outweigh those to the labor
   market."
  → MOST HAWKISH STATEMENT from any voter in 2026: explicit
    willingness to hold even if labor market weakens, if
    inflation risks dominate. This goes beyond the committee's
    symmetric "dual risk" framing — Waller has an asymmetric
    adverse scenario where inflation wins over labor.

Cut signal status: CONDITIONAL ONLY (benign scenario only).
  In the adverse scenario, no cuts even with labor weakness.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC: HAWKISH — his Jan 28 dissent (preferred
  cut) has been replaced by explicit "hold even if labor
  weakens" in the adverse scenario. The energy shock changed
  his calculus significantly.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL-HAWKISH — most hawkish voter speech in
               2026; new "sequence of shocks" framework raises
               vigilance bar significantly

Directional:   Waller is noticeably more hawkish than Jefferson
  and Williams on the dual-risk framing. His "sequence of
  transitory shocks" lesson from 2021-22 and explicit "hold
  even if labor weakens" in the adverse scenario represent
  the furthest hawkish reach of any voter since the energy
  shock began.

  • "Hold even if labor weakens" — explicit; no other voter

  • Underlying inflation "close to 2%" ex-tariffs — the
    dovish anchor that prevents this from becoming fully
    hawkish.
