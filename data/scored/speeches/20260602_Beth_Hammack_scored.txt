GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed: not directly invoked.

Own language:
  "Consumer spending has continued to rise at a moderate pace."
  "Businesses have been ramping up their spending on AI-related
   investments in data centers, computing equipment, and software."
  "SORCE indices... show strengthening business conditions,
   employment, capital expenditures, and cost pressures."
  "In spite of elevated uncertainty, most conversations that I've
   had with business leaders indicate expectations for solid
   activity in coming months."
  → Overall growth characterization: RESILIENT. No weakness signal.

FINANCIAL CONDITIONS — KEY HAWKISH ADDITION:
  "measures of financial conditions are supportive of growth rather
   than holding it back."
  "I mainly hear about plans for business investment, not that
   credit markets or interest rates are holding companies back,
   and this is telling me that policy may not be restrictive."
  "rising inflation means the real federal funds rate has actually
   declined over the last few months."
  → HAWKISH FRAMING: Hammack argues that financial conditions are
    accommodative and that the real FFR has declined as inflation
    rose — the functional equivalent of saying the Fed has eased
    even while holding the nominal rate steady. This framing
    directly supports the case for tighter policy.

Shift vs. prior speech (May 1):   HAWKISH — May 1 statement noted
  "resilient thus far in 2026" without elaboration. June 2 adds
  explicit financial conditions analysis arguing policy is NOT
  restrictive (real rate falling).
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 6):       HAWKISH — Mar 6 had no financial
  conditions analysis. "Policy may not be restrictive" is new to
  Hammack's framework post-March. Mar 6 said "around neutral";
  June 2 implies it may now be below neutral in real terms.

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, on average, and the unemployment
   rate has been little changed" [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Hammack does NOT echo this language. She omits any mention of
    low job gains. Her characterization diverges from the statement.

Own verdict:
  "The unemployment rate was 4.3 percent in April, similar to
   where it was last summer. This is right around my definition of
   full employment in the long term, when labor demand and labor
   supply are in balance."
  → FULL EMPLOYMENT FRAMING: Identical to May 1 dissent statement.
    No deterioration, no softening signal, no downside risk.

Own qualifier:
  "Claims for unemployment insurance and notices of mass layoffs
   remain low, providing no signal of an impending slowdown."
  → Explicitly rules out forward-looking labor deterioration.
    No "soft data diverging from hard data" framing (contrast
    with Williams, who distinguishes hard vs. soft data).

Shift vs. prior speech (May 1):   UNCHANGED — "near full employment"
  persists. May 1 used same characterization. No shift.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 6):       HAWKISH — Mar 6 acknowledged
  "labor market softening we've seen over the last year" as a
  dual-mandate consideration. June 2 has no such acknowledgment;
  4.3% is simply "full employment." Hammack has dropped the
  dovish labor qualifier from her pre-FOMC framework.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase
   in global energy prices." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Hammack does NOT echo the energy attribution framing.

Own language — KEY:
  "Inflation is too high and is moving higher."
  → UPGRADED CHARACTERIZATION. May 1: "inflation pressures continue
    to be broad based." Mar 6: "inflation is too high." June 2 adds
    the directional component: "moving higher" — not just elevated
    but actively accelerating.

  Headline PCE: 3.8% in April, "well above our 2 percent objective."
  Core PCE: 3.3% in April, "also well above our objective and well
    above levels from six months or a year ago."
  → Year-over-year comparison: core inflation worse than six months
    and one year ago. The trajectory is adverse, not merely elevated.

  "Looking within the components of core inflation, I see relatively
   broad-based price pressures across goods and nonhousing services."
  "Inflation among services excluding housing has been stuck at an
   elevated rate and has shown little improvement over the last two
   years, even though these prices should be less impacted by tariffs
   and energy prices than other components."
  → STRUCTURAL INFLATION ARGUMENT: Services ex-housing — the
    category most reflective of domestic demand and wages — has
    been elevated for two years and is NOT attributable to energy
    or tariffs. This directly undermines the look-through case.

  Inflation expectations risk:
  "With the economy now in its sixth year of elevated inflation,
   consumers, businesses, and financial markets may start to build
   in expectations for higher future inflation."
  "Preventing an inflationary mindset is critical to delivering on
   our 2 percent objective. Increases in inflation expectations that
   threaten our goal warrant taking decisive action."
  → ESCALATION LANGUAGE: "decisive action" in response to
    expectations de-anchoring. This is stronger urgency than any
    prior Hammack statement.

Shift vs. prior speech (May 1):   HAWKISH — May 1: "broad based"
  inflation. June 2 adds: "moving higher," year-over-year
  deterioration, two-year services stickiness, and "decisive action"
  on expectations. Substantially more alarmed tone.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 6):       HAWKISH — Mar 6: "too high,"
  "broad based." June 2 adds active acceleration, quantified data
  (3.8% headline, 3.3% core), services persistence argument, and
  inflation expectations urgency. The energy shock has deepened the
  inflation concern substantially since March.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "additional adjustments" [easing bias, Apr 29 stmt]
  → Hammack does NOT echo. She opposed this language in her dissent.

Own language — KEY:
  "For today, it's reasonable to keep rates steady given the
   uncertainties around the economic outlook."
  → Base case: HOLD. Same as May 1. She still supports the hold.

  "But if recent data trends continue, it may soon be appropriate
   for policy to act to address the growing risks of persistently
   elevated inflation."
  → CONDITIONAL HIKE SIGNAL — FIRST FROM HAMMACK. "Act" in this
    context means tighten: she cites "growing risks of persistently
    elevated inflation" as the trigger, not labor market weakness.
    "May soon" adds temporal urgency.

  "Based on the data, I'm more concerned about the growing risks
   of persistently elevated inflation than the risks to full
   employment and also that monetary policy may not be sufficiently
   restrictive to bring inflation down to 2 percent in a timely
   fashion."
  → EXPLICIT POLICY DIAGNOSIS: rates may be below the level needed
    to achieve the 2% target. "Not sufficiently restrictive" is a
    direct statement that the current FFR is too low. This is the
    most direct case for a rate increase of any voter.

  Urgency logic:
  "If we wait for definitive evidence that high inflation has become
   embedded in the economy, it may require larger policy adjustments,
   at greater cost."
  → PROACTIVE RATIONALE FOR HIKING: she invokes the Volcker cost
    argument — waiting too long requires a larger, more painful
    adjustment. The Volcker episode is referenced explicitly earlier
    in the speech. This is not boilerplate; it is a policy argument.

Cut signal status: ABSENT — easing bias explicitly rejected at Apr 29;
  hike signal now present (conditional: "may soon be appropriate
  to act").

Scenario framing:
  Hammack presents two-scenario analysis:
  Scenario 1 (explicit): Extended oil + supply chain pressures
    → hold ("balance weaker labor prospects with elevated inflation").
    Less likely: "sharper downturn" → more accommodation.
  Scenario 2 (explicit): Inflation remains elevated if energy costs
    don't fall, businesses raise prices → "more restrictive monetary
    policy could well be needed."
  → Framing is EXPLICIT AND NAMED (type: explicit scenario with named
    outcomes), per CLAUDE.md definition. The hike scenario is
    presented as a plausible baseline, not a tail risk.

Shift vs. prior speech (May 1):   HAWKISH — May 1: "I supported
  holding the federal funds rate steady" (no hike signal). June 2:
  "may soon be appropriate for policy to act" on inflation. First
  forward-leaning hike language from Hammack.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 6):       HAWKISH — Mar 6: "hold for quite
  some time," "two-sided risks to rates" (symmetric hike/cut). June 2:
  upside-biased with conditional hike signal. The symmetric two-sided
  framing from March has resolved hawkish.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH — significant escalation from May 1.
               Five structural shifts in this speech vs. the prior:
               (1) Real rate argued to have FALLEN as inflation
                   rose — removes the "policy is restrictive" premise;
               (2) Inflation "moving higher," not just elevated;
               (3) Services ex-housing persistence for two years —
                   structural argument against look-through;
               (4) Conditional hike signal: "may soon be appropriate
                   for policy to act";
               (5) Explicit urgency logic invoking Volcker costs of
                   waiting too long.
               This is the most hawkish Type A speech from any voter
               since the April 29 FOMC meeting.

Position relative to committee:
  Hammack now joins Waller (May 22: "I am prepared to raise rates")
  and Cook (May 27) as the third voter to deploy explicit hike-ready
  language in the post-April-29 period. The coalition of explicit
  hike signalers now stands at 3 voters (Waller, Cook, Hammack) plus
  two implicit hawkish holders (Logan May 10, Kashkari's dissent
  record). Bowman (May 29) remains the explicit dovish outlier
  defending the easing bias. Median voter Paulson has not updated
  her stance since May 19.

Language watch:
  • "may soon be appropriate for policy to act" — FIRST from Hammack.
    Temporal ("may soon") + directional ("act" = tighten). If echoed
    by Logan or Kashkari before Jun 6 blackout, the hike coalition
    has a strong pre-FOMC signal.
  • "monetary policy may not be sufficiently restrictive" — the
    cleanest articulation of the hike argument's logic: not just
    that rates could go higher, but that current rates are insufficient
    to achieve the target. Watch whether Waller or Logan adopt this
    specific framing in any remaining pre-blackout communications.
  • "inflation is too high and is moving higher" — directional
    acceleration language; more urgent than "elevated." [Note: not
    yet in the FOMC statement, which uses "elevated in part
    reflecting energy prices."]
  • "decisive action" (on expectations) — escalation phrase; Hammack
    is the first voter to use this term in 2026.
  • "sixth year of elevated inflation" — duration framing (similar
    to "five years" used by Barkin, Logan, and Hammack herself in
    prior speeches). Inflation expectations de-anchoring is now
    explicitly named as a tail risk.

Analyst flag:  YES
  → HIKE COALITION QUESTION: Waller (May 22), Cook (May 27), and
    Hammack (Jun 2) have all deployed explicit hike-ready language
    in the span of 11 days. If Logan (no post-May-10 speech
    recovered) and Kashkari (all post-Apr-29 stubs) share this view,
    the committee could have 5 hike-ready voters at June 18. Does
    Warsh hold this position? His silence through 35+ days is the
    critical gap. The June 18 outcome is ambiguous between hold-with-
    removal-of-easing-bias and an actual rate increase, depending
    on Warsh's undisclosed stance.
  → REAL RATE ARGUMENT: Hammack's claim that the real FFR has
    declined as inflation rose is worth quantifying. If headline PCE
    was ~3.8% in April and the nominal FFR is 4.25%, the real rate
    is approximately 0.45% — plausibly below neutral. This is a
    materially different picture from the "moderately restrictive"
    characterization used by Bowman (May 29). Analyst should verify
    whether this calculation holds across different inflation measures.
