LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Own verdict:
  "The labor market appears to be largely stable. The unemployment rate,
   4.3 percent in April, has remained unchanged, on net, since last summer.
   The rate is in line with estimates of the natural rate of unemployment,
   suggesting that the supply and demand of labor are roughly balanced."
  → Explicitly balanced framing: supply/demand equilibrium as of May 2026.

Own qualifier:
  "I view the downside risks to the labor market as being elevated. One
   factor is heightened uncertainty about output due to the Middle East
   conflict. A softening in demand could lead to a softening in the labor
   market."
  → Named risk vectors: (1) Middle East conflict demand uncertainty,
    (2) low-hire environment, (3) AI-driven structural labor displacement
    ("most significant reorganization of work in generations").

  "AI-related job loss could precede job gains. Although we do not have
   conclusive evidence of this occurring yet, it may still be on the
   horizon, and increased churn in the labor market could be anticipated."
  → Forward-looking AI displacement risk — not a current diagnosis
    but a named monitoring item. Cook explicitly watches for this.

Notable ABSENCE vs. Feb 4: Cook does not repeat the distributional
  deterioration flags from Feb 4 (part-time workers, youth/Black
  unemployment). Current labor assessment is less granularly alarming.

Shift vs. prior speech (Feb 4):   HAWKISH LEAN — labor described as
  "largely stable" and "roughly balanced"; distributional deterioration
  flags dropped; downside risk flag elevated but offset by stable headline.
  Feb 4 framing was more attentive to visible softening signals.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 4):       same — see above.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[Cook leads the Economic Outlook section with inflation — unusual sequence
 (she opened with labor in Feb 4). The reordering itself signals priority.]

Own language — KEY:
  "Inflation is clearly moving in the wrong direction."
  → Strongest negative verdict in Cook's scored corpus.
    Compare to Feb 4: "Progress on inflation essentially stalled in 2025."
    May 27 is a directional regression claim — not just stalled but worsening.

Current readings:
  "PCE price index rose 3.8 percent over the 12 months ending in April.
   That reading is well above our 2 percent target."
  "core PCE inflation is estimated to have risen by 3.3 percent over the
   12 months ending in April — its highest reading since 2023."
  [Compare to Feb 4: PCE 2.9%, core 3.0%. Headline PCE up 90bp and
   core PCE up 30bp since her last economic outlook speech.]

Shock stacking:
  Cook identifies three concurrent inflation pressures:
  (1) Tariffs — "should result in only a one-time shift upwards in the
      price level, and the effects of tariffs on inflation should begin
      to abate soon."
  (2) Energy/Iran conflict — "path of energy prices is tied to the
      ongoing conflict, the results of which are highly uncertain."
  (3) AI investment demand — "companies have announced more than $1.5
      trillion in data-center plans... Effects of this demand on prices
      are apparent: prices have risen significantly for chips, other high-
      tech equipment, and software. Wages in specialty trades in
      construction have picked up notably. Electricity and water prices
      have each increased by about 5 percent over the past year."
  → Cook identifies AI capital expenditure as a third inflation vector
    distinct from tariffs and energy — a novel analytical frame unique
    to her position as Financial Stability Committee chair with direct
    data-center investment research access.

Credibility/entrenchment risk:
  "After five years of above-target inflation, I am particularly attuned
   to the risk that elevated inflation will become embedded in price- and
   wage-setting behavior."
  → "Five years above target" language now explicit (Feb 4: "nearly five
    years"). Duration framing now matches Waller (May 22) and the broader
    committee hawkish coalition.

  "Firms may embed these shocks into their pricing decisions, and workers
   may incorporate them into wage negotiations."
  → Wage-price feedback risk named as a medium-term transmission mechanism.

Shift vs. prior speech (Feb 4):   STRONGLY HAWKISH — "clearly moving in
  wrong direction" vs. "progress stalled"; PCE 3.8% vs. 2.9%; "five years"
  vs. "nearly five years"; AI investment demand as new third shock named.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 4):       same — see above.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Own language:
  "I see elevated risks to both sides of our mandate, and from a
   risk-management perspective, I currently believe that the right
   course of action is to hold rates steady."
  → Hold is the baseline.

  "I want to be clear about my risk assessment: The risks remain tilted
   toward higher inflation."
  → Asymmetric framing: dual mandate acknowledged, but inflation side
    dominates.

  "In my baseline forecast, disinflation should resume in upcoming months
   without having to raise rates."
  → Baseline holds: no rate action needed IF disinflation materializes.

  "I am prepared to raise rates, if the expected disinflation does not
   appear in a timely manner."
  → EXPLICIT CONDITIONAL HIKE SIGNAL. Condition: disinflation fails
    to appear "in a timely manner." Framing note: this is descriptive/
    conditional (not a named scenario with pre-specified trigger), but
    Cook pre-commits to a response. Stronger than risk language; slightly
    less explicit than Waller's Apr 17 "BENIGN/ADVERSE" named scenarios.

  "I will continue to monitor labor-market developments, as well, and
    would be prepared to adjust my policy stance downward should the
    labor market deteriorate."
  → Cut signal preserved as a conditional backstop — but subordinate to
    the inflation framing. She addresses labor first only after stating
    the hike scenario. Ordering signals priorities.

Cut signal status: CONDITIONAL ONLY (labor backstop), SUBORDINATE to hike scenario.
Hike signal status: PRESENT — conditioned on disinflation not appearing
  "in a timely manner." Cook is the SECOND Just Voter (after Waller,
  May 22) to explicitly name rate hike as a potential outcome in 2026.

Easing bias stance: Cook does not reference or address the committee's
  current statement easing bias. [Inference: she may be aligned with the
  easing-bias-removal position given her hike framing, but she does not
  explicitly call for its removal as Waller did on May 22.]

Shift vs. prior speech (Feb 4):   HAWKISH — from "no cut signal / hold
  with inflation-first framing" to "prepared to raise rates." This is
  the clearest directional shift in Cook's 2026 scored corpus.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 4):       same — major hawkish shift.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH — explicit hike signal; inflation "clearly moving
               in wrong direction"; duration framing now explicit; AI demand
               as novel third inflation vector.

  Cook's May 27 speech represents the most hawkish policy stance she has
  articulated in the 2026 scored corpus. Three changes from Feb 4 are
  policy-consequential: (1) inflation described as "clearly moving in the
  wrong direction" (vs. "stalled progress"); (2) an explicit conditional
  hike signal, compared to Feb 4's "no cut signal / patient hold"; and
  (3) AI investment demand named as a third inflationary pressure — a
  framing unique to Cook and potentially the leading edge of how the
  committee narrativizes the AI-driven inflation contribution. The "five
  years" duration framing now matches the hawkish voter coalition (Waller,
  Goolsbee, Collins, Barkin — all in the 2026 corpus). Cook is the second
  Just Voter to explicitly name hike possibility (after Waller May 22).

    Cook's corpus; stronger than Waller's "five years above target" because
    it asserts direction of change, not just duration. Language cycle stage:
    DISPERSED (Cook only so far; watch for adoption by other voters).
  • "I am prepared to raise rates" — EMERGING SHARED (now two Just Voters:
    Waller May 22, Cook May 27). Language cycle stage advances from DISPERSED.
    Critical threshold: if Troika member adopts this language, June 18
    statement shift to explicit symmetric risk becomes likely.
  • AI investment demand as inflation vector ("$1.5 trillion in data-center
    plans... electricity and water prices up 5%") — novel framing specific
    to Cook's data access. Not yet echoed by other officials; could spread
    as the committee's second-order inflation narrative.
  • "Five years of above-target inflation" — EMERGING SHARED (now Waller,
    Goolsbee, Collins, Barkin, Cook). Approaching COMMITTEE-WIDE status
    among non-Troika voters and non-voters.
