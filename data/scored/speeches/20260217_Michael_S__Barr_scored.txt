EMBEDDED ECONOMIC OUTLOOK (opening section)

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Own verdict:
  "While the labor market slowed through last summer, it is now
   stabilizing. This stabilization is occurring with an unemployment
   rate that is broadly consistent with what many estimate is its
   long-run level, when the economy is in balance." — Neutral and
   constructive: stabilizing at or near maximum employment level.
  "Job creation has been near zero over the course of last year,
   as has labor force growth." — Acknowledges near-zero net job
   creation, similar to Waller's Jan 30 characterization, but
   frames it as matched by supply deceleration (neutral net effect).
  "Very low levels of job creation and also a low firing rate...
   a tentative balance in labor supply and demand. But it is a
   delicate balance, and that means that the labor market could be
   especially vulnerable to negative shocks." — DOVISH QUALIFIER:
   "delicate balance," "especially vulnerable." More concerned than
   Williams/Jefferson but less alarmed than Bowman/Waller.

Direction: NEUTRAL with dovish lean — stabilizing but vulnerable.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC: [N/A — no prior 2026 scored Barr speech]

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Own language — KEY:
  "PCE remains elevated at 3 percent, about where it was a year
   ago. Disinflation... slowed last year, as goods price inflation
   picked up, in large part due to tariffs." — Acknowledges stalled
   disinflation since 2024.
  "That pattern appeared to continue in the inflation data released
   last week." — Recent data confirms continued stall.
  "I see the risk of persistent inflation above our 2 percent target
   as significant, which means we need to remain vigilant." —
   HAWKISH QUALIFIER: "significant" risk of persistence. This is
   more hawkish than Barr's Mar 26 speech and substantially more
   hawkish than his Jan 2026 characterization as implied by his
   vote (he voted with the majority to hold). Contrast with
   Paulson/Bowman's "cautious optimism" on inflation.
  "It is reasonable to forecast that tariff effects on inflation
   will begin to abate later this year, but there are many reasons
   to be concerned that inflation will remain elevated." — Forward-
   looking but with explicit uncertainty flagged. Not a clean
   look-through endorsement.

Direction: NEUTRAL-to-HAWKISH — acknowledges stalled disinflation
  and flags persistent inflation risk as "significant."

Shift vs. pre-FOMC: [N/A]

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Own language — KEY:
  "The prudent course for monetary policy right now is to take
   the time necessary to assess conditions as they evolve." —
   Data-dependent patience; no cut signal.
  "I would like to see evidence that goods price inflation is
   sustainably retreating before considering reducing the policy
   rate further, provided labor market conditions remain stable." —
   CONDITIONAL HOLD: two explicit conditions for a cut: (1) goods
   inflation sustainably retreating AND (2) labor stable. This is
   more conditional than the committee's standard "data-dependent"
   language.
  "Based on current conditions and the data in hand, it will likely
   be appropriate to hold rates steady for some time as we assess
   incoming data, the evolving outlook, and the balance of risks." —
   Extended hold bias; "for some time" is more explicit than the
   committee's open-ended language.

Cut signal status: ABSENT — explicit hold bias with two conditions
  required before considering cuts.

Direction: NEUTRAL-to-HAWKISH — hold for some time; two conditions
  required for cuts; "significant" inflation risk.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL with hawkish lean — Barr's Feb 17 embedded
               outlook is meaningfully more hawkish than Bowman's
               or Paulson's January speeches. His "significant" risk
               of persistent inflation, two conditions for cuts, and
               "hold for some time" language places him in the
               neutral-to-hawkish bloc, consistent with his
               subsequent Mar 26 scored speech.

Language watch:
  • "Risk of persistent inflation above 2%... significant" —
    Barr's inflation concern is more explicit than Bowman or Paulson.
    This framing moves toward what Logan/Hammack/Waller adopt
    post-Iran conflict.
  • "Two conditions before considering cuts" — goods inflation
    retreating AND labor stable. This is a more conditional
    threshold than the committee's statement language.

Analyst flag:  NO — consistent with the hold position the committee
  maintained in January 2026.
