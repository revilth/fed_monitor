PER-SPEECH ANALYSIS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
2026-05-22 | Christopher J. Waller | Governor | Just Voter
"Policy Risks Have Changed"
The Centre for Central Banking Guest Lecture, Frankfurt, Germany
SOURCE: https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/waller20260522a.htm

CLASSIFICATION: Type A — Full Economic Outlook. Four-section
  structure: Growth / Labor Market / Inflation / Monetary Policy.
  Title signals an explicit assessment of how the risk balance has
  shifted since the April FOMC. Waller's first post-FOMC economic
  outlook speech; first post-energy-shock update since Apr 17.

Reference FOMC:    April 29, 2026 (hold; FFR 4.25–4.50%)
Pre-FOMC baseline: April 17, 2026  "One Transitory Shock After Another"
                   (David Kaserman Memorial Lecture, Auburn University — scored)
Post-FOMC prior:   April 21, 2026  "Modernizing Federal Reserve Operations"
                   (Brookings Institution — Type B / operational, NOT scored
                   for monetary policy; not a valid comparison baseline)
  [Note: This speech is therefore Waller's FIRST post-Apr-29 economic
   outlook speech. All shifts are vs. the Apr 17 pre-FOMC baseline.]
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Economic activity has continued to expand at a solid pace." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Waller version: "gross domestic product has continued to grow at a
    solid pace, boosted by torrid business investment related to
    artificial intelligence (AI) and resilient consumer spending."

Own language:
  "Real GDP grew 2.1 percent in 2025, at a 2 percent rate in the
   first quarter, and my estimate is that this is close to the pace
   in the second quarter."
  → Solid growth picture echoing committee boilerplate; no personal
    departure from consensus.

  "I see no signs of this investment slowing down." (re: AI buildout)
  → Affirmative on one key demand driver; no compositional concern raised.

Notable qualification:
  "These refunds are concentrated among those of higher income, so it
   isn't helping the lower-income households who have been feeling the
   burden of higher gasoline prices over the past few months."
  → Income bifurcation noted — upper-income spending supports aggregate
    data; lower-income squeeze from gas prices. Watcher flag on whether
    this broadens into aggregate demand pullback.

  "Spending did fall in April at auto dealerships as well as furniture
   and clothing outlets and department stores, and I will be watching
   carefully to see whether this persists."
  → Targeted softness in discretionary categories; not yet a verdict
    but a forward monitoring flag.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Apr 17):  unchanged overall — both speeches affirm
  solid aggregate growth; Apr 17 flagged consumer confidence at "lowest-
  ever reading"; May 22 adds specific April softness in autos/furniture/
  apparel. Growth assessment broadly stable.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, and the unemployment rate has been
   little changed in recent months." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Waller version: "The unemployment rate in April held steady at
    4.3 percent...The economy created a net 115,000 jobs in April
    and averaged 48,000 new jobs over the past three months."

Own verdict — KEY:
  "After worrying about a deterioration in the labor market through
   most of 2025, recent data has indicated to me that labor supply
   and demand have finally come into a rough balance."
  → Explicit declaration of stabilization. "Finally" signals this is
    a new assessment, not continuation. "Rough balance" preserves
    some uncertainty but the directionality is clear.

Own framing on low payrolls:
  "That is historically a low level and, in the past, has been a
   sign of weakness, but with labor force growth also near zero due
   to an aging population and low net immigration, little or no job
   creation is now consistent with a stable labor market."
  → Reiterates the recalibrated break-even from Apr 17; low payrolls
    are not a recession signal when labor force growth is itself near zero.

Own policy implication — KEY:
  "I don't see the prospect of a weakening labor market as the dominant
   force that should be guiding monetary policy in the months ahead."
  → REMOVES the labor-market-downside leg from his prior policy
    framework. At Apr 17, labor weakness was one of the two conditions
    that could trigger cuts (the BENIGN scenario). That conditional
    is now explicitly deprioritized.

Caveat retained:
  "the United States hasn't often experienced little or no growth in
   labor supply, and in the absence of historical experience, it is
   hard to judge how employers may react now to a downturn in demand."
  → Appropriate uncertainty hedge; does not reinstate the dovish
    conditional but acknowledges limits of current calibration.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Apr 17):  HAWKISH — Apr 17 framed payrolls
  as "alternating between positive and negative," with consumer
  confidence at "lowest-ever" raising the specter of "threshold
  crossed" demand destruction. May 22 replaces this with "finally
  come into rough balance" and explicitly removes labor as the
  dominant policy driver. The labor-market downside risk has been
  materially downgraded.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Waller goes considerably beyond this: PCE ~3.8% YoY ("highest
    in three years"), core PCE ~3.3% YoY ("most in two and a half
    years"). The "somewhat elevated" formulation understates his
    current read.

Own language — KEY (HAWKISH ESCALATION):
  "Inflation is not headed in the right direction."
  → Direct, unambiguous verdict. Deliberate departure from committee
    boilerplate. Most explicit negative inflation assessment from any
    voter in 2026.

Own breadth signal:
  "About half of the categories of goods and services prices tracked
   in consumer inflation have been up 3 percent or more this year, a
   historically large share."
  → BREADTH of inflation is the new concern beyond headline/core levels.
    This is distinct from the energy shock narrative (which could be
    dismissed as transitory) — breadth implies diffusion into non-energy
    components that cannot be looked through.

Specific April data (hawkish):
  "CPI inflation was up 0.6 percent last month, with a 3.8 percent
   jump in energy prices. More concerning was that grocery prices in
   April were up 0.7 percent, apparel 0.6 percent, and services
   excluding energy 0.5 percent."
  → Services ex-energy at 0.5% monthly is the most concerning data
    point — this category is the one the Fed most directly controls
    and the one least explicable by the energy shock.

Producer prices as leading indicator:
  "The input price index for manufacturing firms has jumped from 59
   to 84.6 over the past three months, matching the level reached in
   April 2022, the same reading as during the thick of the pandemic-
   related supply chain crisis."
  → Explicit historical comparison to 2022 supply chain peak — a
    hawkish framing that elevates the energy shock's severity.

Five-year duration framing (joins Non Voter chorus):
  "the FOMC has been unable to achieve our stated inflation target
   for more than five years."
  → Waller is the FIRST Just Voter to use this exact duration framing
    explicitly. Previously appeared only from Non Voters (Goolsbee
    May 12, Collins May 13, Barkin May 21). Language cycle stage now:
    EMERGING SHARED — crossing from Non Voter to Just Voter tier.

Bayesian framework (new analytical addition):
  "If people do not know the true inflation generating process and
   see a sequence of positive price shocks, they may infer that the
   next price shock is more likely to be positive than negative."
  → Formal theoretical justification for why transitory shocks can
    still unanchor expectations. This is the analytical elaboration
    of his Apr 17 "sequence of transitory shocks" language — now
    equipped with a named theoretical mechanism (Bayesian updating)
    that makes the concern more precise and more difficult to dismiss.

Expectations nuance (partial anchor retained):
  "measures of longer-term inflation expectations are still relatively
   low and appear well anchored, some expectations from one to five
   years ahead have moved up since the beginning of 2026."
  → 1-5 year expectations elevated but longer-term anchored. Waller
    explicitly says he is watching for contagion to longer-term
    measures. This is a conditional concern, not a verdict of
    unanchoring — but the direction of monitoring is hawkish.

Own dovish anchor from Apr 17 — NOW REMOVED:
  April 17 contained: "underlying inflation...excluding tariff
  effects — is running close to 2 percent." That ex-tariff floor
  is ABSENT from May 22. The data now preclude it: PCE 3.8%,
  core 3.3% — there is no credible underlying "close to 2%" story.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Apr 17):  MATERIAL HAWKISH — Apr 17 retained
  the ex-tariff dovish anchor ("underlying close to 2%"). May 22
  drops it entirely as data have deteriorated further. PCE 3.8%
  vs. implied ~2.5% ex-tariffs in April. Breadth signal added.
  Bayesian framework formalizes the "sequence of shocks" concern.
  April data worse than expected across groceries, apparel, services.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  The Apr 29 statement retained "easing bias" language indicating
  the FOMC would assess "the extent and timing of additional
  adjustments to the target range."
  → Waller explicitly advocates REMOVING that language: "I would
    support removing the 'easing bias' language in our policy
    statement to make it clear that a rate cut is no more likely
    in the future than a rate increase."

Own verdict on near-term policy:
  "on net, my current policy position is to hold rates steady for
   the near term."
  → Unambiguous hold. No nuance on timing.

Own language — cut conditions (hawkish bar):
  "I am going to need to see improvement on inflation or a significant
   deterioration in the labor market before I would consider reducing
   the policy rate."
  → Double bar for cuts: EITHER meaningful disinflation OR dramatic
    labor deterioration. In Apr 17, the BENIGN scenario would have
    allowed cuts "to support the labor market later this year." That
    conditional is now closed — "significant deterioration" is a much
    higher bar than "labor market needing support."

Own language — hike risk (NEW; first voter to go explicit):
  "I can no longer rule out rate hikes further down the road if
   inflation does not abate soon."
  → First Just Voter to explicitly articulate hike possibility in 2026.
    "Can no longer rule out" is a deliberate escalation from the
    Apr 17 adverse scenario which only said "maintain the policy rate."
    The word "no longer" signals this is a change in his assessment.

  "If I believe inflation expectations start to become unanchored,
   I would not hesitate to support an increase in the target range."
  → Conditional hike trigger named: unanchored expectations. Clearer
    than the Apr 17 adverse scenario's "inflation risks outweigh
    employment risks." A specific mechanism for what would activate hikes.

"Next move" framing:
  "The next move, whether it is a hike or cut, will depend on the data."
  → Removes asymmetric dovish bias entirely. Symmetric framing on the
    direction of the next move is itself a hawkish statement given
    where markets are pricing (cuts, not hikes).

Time preference:
  "I am prepared to be patient in holding policy at its current
   restrictive setting as we watch how the conflict evolves."
  → Patience framing. No timeline commitment. Removes any guidance
    that would imply cuts are imminent.

Cut signal status: ABSENT. The Apr 17 BENIGN conditional cut signal
  (cuts possible if energy shock resolves) has been removed. No cut
  scenario remains in the May 22 framework.

Hike signal status: CONDITIONAL — explicitly in play if inflation
  does not abate or if expectations become unanchored. Not a default
  scenario but no longer ruled out.

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Apr 17):  MATERIAL HAWKISH —
  Apr 17: BENIGN scenario (cuts possible); ADVERSE scenario (hold
    even if labor weakens).
  May 22: NO benign scenario; hike explicitly possible; easing bias
    removal explicitly advocated. Full reversal of the Apr 17 dovish
    conditional. The two-scenario framework has collapsed into a
    single track: hold until inflation improves, with hike risk
    rising if it does not.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH — clear and material escalation from the
               already-hawkish Apr 17 baseline

This speech represents the most hawkish signal from any Just Voter
in 2026. Waller's Apr 17 speech was already the most hawkish voter
speech of the cycle; May 22 moves further in the same direction:

(1) Labor stabilization removes the conditional dovish leg. The Apr 17
    BENIGN scenario's cut trigger (labor market support) is gone because
    the labor market no longer needs support.
(2) Inflation has materially worsened: PCE 3.8% (Apr 17 baseline implied
    ~2% ex-tariffs). The ex-tariff dovish anchor cannot survive current
    data. Breadth of price increases (half of categories above 3%) adds
    a diffusion concern beyond the energy shock.
(3) Easing bias removal: First voter to explicitly advocate removing the
    Apr 29 statement's easing bias language. If this view is shared by
    other voters, a statement change at the June 17-18 FOMC becomes likely.
(4) Hike risk named: First voter to explicitly say hikes are possible. The
    Bayesian framework provides an analytical structure for this — multiple
    transitory shocks compound expectations risk even if individually
    dismissible.

Language watch:
  • "I would support removing the 'easing bias' language" — HIGHEST
    PRIORITY. If Paulson, Jefferson, or Williams echo this call, the
    Jun 17-18 FOMC outcome changes materially. Language cycle stage:
    DISPERSED (one voter); watch for spread.
  • "I can no longer rule out rate hikes" — HAWKISH NEW FRAME. First
    voter to go explicit. If this language migrates to Paulson or
    Logan, hike scenarios become the committee's mainstream discussion,
    not an outlier view.
  • Bayesian framework for expectations — "sequence of price shocks
    raises inferred probability of future inflation" — sophisticated
    analytical cover for hawkish action. May appear in other officials'
    speeches as second-order framing.
  • "Five years above 2% target" — now crossing from Non Voter to
    Just Voter tier. One step from Troika adoption. Language cycle
    stage: EMERGING SHARED.
  • "Policy Risks Have Changed" (speech title = policy signal) —
    the risk balance has shifted from symmetric to inflation-dominant.

Analyst flag:  YES
  → Two questions for analyst:
    (1) Is Waller's easing-bias-removal call a personal outlier or
        does it reflect pre-June-18 coalition building? This is the
        most consequential near-term policy question. If even one
        other voter echoes it before June 18, easing bias removal at
        that meeting becomes likely.
    (2) The hike signal: is Waller signaling a genuine shift in the
        reaction function, or is this designed to move market pricing
        rather than reflect a true base case? The "no longer rule out"
        phrasing vs. "would not hesitate to support" (conditional on
        expectations unanchoring) — is the latter language calibrated
        to a specific trigger (expectations unanchoring, a relatively
        high bar) or is it setting up a hair trigger?
