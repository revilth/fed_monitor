GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed: None — Kovner does not echo FOMC statement language
  (as a non-policymaker, this is expected).

Own language:
  "Without gas, year-over-year growth in total card spending remained well above 3
   percent in March."
  → Headline demand resilient ex-energy. No compositional breakdown at the
    aggregate level.

COMPOSITIONAL CONCERN (analytically important):
  High-income: "Asset prices have skyrocketed alongside costs in recent years —
   whether via the stock market or real estate. This has allowed high-income
   consumers to maintain their spending... continue to splurge in other areas
   like luxury goods or travel. We hear from businesses offering luxury products
   and services that demand remains robust."
  Middle/low-income: "Middle- and low-income consumers... haven't gotten the
   e-bike boost... running out of trade-down options after years of high price
   growth and now high gas prices. Increasingly, they are making choices about
   what to buy and what to skip, trading off between spending categories and not
   just within them."
  → Spending bifurcation by income tier, with low-income demand increasingly
    constrained. Aligns with Barkin's prior framing of income-tier differentiation.

Forward risk:
  "The longer oil prices are high, the more demand destruction becomes a risk.
   Low-income Americans, with less wiggle room in their budget, are worth watching."
  → Conditional downside. Not modal; not imminent. Tax refunds (+10% through April)
    identified as temporary buffer.

Manufacturing: "We've seen the ISM index turn positive... signs that more
  manufacturers see better times ahead." Small positive; "mixed" on Richmond Fed
  surveys. Net: cautious green shoot, not yet sustained signal.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed: None directly.
  Kovner uses her own framing, which is analytically consistent with committee's
  "job gains have remained slow" and "unemployment rate has been little changed."

Own verdict:
  "People still have jobs; businesses haven't been laying off workers... and that
   has made a difference."
  → Low-hire-low-fire framing echoed in accessible language. Consistent with
    committee consensus; no personal addition beyond the data.

KEY QUANTITATIVE SIGNAL — job-finding rate deterioration:
  "In 2022, the share of unemployed workers who found a job in the next month was
   30 percent. That's now down to 24 percent."
  → Six-percentage-point drop in job-finding rate. This is a more granular labor
    deterioration signal than unemployment rate alone captures. Consistent with
    the "fragility below the headline" framing also noted by Collins (May 13).

Hiring outlook (CFO Survey):
  "Less than 40 percent of firms are actively hiring for new positions... CFOs
   reported that even with healthy revenue growth expectations for the year,
   employment is expected to grow at a tepid pace in 2026 and 2027, well below
   forecasts for 2025."
  → Forward-looking signal: weak hiring sustained into next year, not just
    current period.

Structural vs. cyclical:
  "Some of the slowdown in hiring is structural... Economists at the Richmond
   Fed found that unlike what we've typically seen in historical data, some of
   the slowdown has come from workers who are considered 'more attached' to the
   workforce. That's a fairly new phenomenon."
  "Only about a third of the difference in job finding rates is explained by AI
   exposure."
  → Richmond Fed staff research: structural component confirmed, but AI explains
    only a minority. The remainder (two-thirds) is not explained — could be
    immigration-driven supply, sector mismatch, or demand-side caution.

Headline summary: "Resilience if you have a job, but fragility if you don't."
  → Compact bifurcation framing; more candid than typical committee language
    about labor stress below the headline unemployment rate.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed: None directly.
  Her framing is analytically consistent with committee language but uses
  her own formulations throughout.

Own language — cumulative shock framing:
  "Inflation has been above the Fed's 2 percent target for five years now."
  → Duration stacking consistent with Waller (Apr 17), Collins (May 13), Powell
    (Apr 29 Q&A). Becoming a widely shared staff/official analytical framing.

  "No one single thing has prevented inflation from returning to 2 percent; instead,
   it's been a rolling sequence of different forces, all of which I might have said
   should prove temporary."
  → Direct articulation of the "sequence of transitory shocks" thesis. Richmond Fed
    research staff is using the same analytical frame as Waller/Barkin/Powell.
    Not yet in the FOMC statement, but now confirmed at the staff level as well.

Housing:
  "The disinflationary process for housing is slow, but there are positive signals.
   Rent prices are falling, and that's a leading indicator."
  → Moderately dovish housing note; rent as leading indicator cited by several
    officials. Months supply of housing building (favorable).

Tariffs:
  "While it has been over a year since the initial tariff announcements, the tariff
   impact on prices is not yet wrapped up. On the contrary, we still hear plenty
   of businesses hoping to pass through tariff costs."
  → Hawkish: pass-through is NOT complete. Multiple businesses queued up to raise
    prices when the political window opens. Consistent with Barkin's Beige Book
    contacts framing.

Energy/Oil rule of thumb:
  "For every 10 percent increase in oil prices, one can expect to see an associated
   increase of 0.2 percentage points in headline PCE inflation and 0.04 percent
   points increase in core PCE inflation."
  → Quantitative contribution of energy to core is small (0.04pp per 10% oil
    increase); the risk channel is via expectations, not direct contribution.

KEY SIGNAL — expectations risk as the primary concern:
  "As I see it, the key risk for price stability is not the short-term
   inflationary pressure from gas prices, but their cumulative effect on
   inflation expectations."
  "The concern this time around is what I mentioned earlier: the cumulative effect
   of back-to-back supply shocks. Do consumers start to expect higher inflation to
   stick around longer and become the norm? There's no sign of that today, but were
   it to occur, the Fed's job of price stability would become much harder."
  → Short-term expectations moved with gas (consistent with historical pattern);
    five-year expectations "thus far have remained steadier." Current assessment:
    stable but at risk. The concern is that repeated shocks erode the "transitory"
    credibility that allows look-through.

Net signal:    HAWKISH LEAN (analytically) — no direct policy signal
               (non-policymaker). The analytical framing is more hawkish than
               neutral: five-year inflation duration, ongoing tariff pass-through,
               and expectations risk as the primary concern all point in a
               restrictive direction. Labor is stable but fragile below the
               headline. Growth is resilient but bifurcated.

Informational value: HIGH for understanding Richmond Fed staff analytical

  (1) Cumulative shock / expectations risk framing as the DOMINANT inflation

      more granular than unemployment rate or payroll growth.

      explains ~1/3 of the flow deterioration. Remaining 2/3 unexplained.
