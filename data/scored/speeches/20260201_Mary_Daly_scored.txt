MONETARY POLICY SECTION — HISTORICAL ANALOGY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Daly structures her monetary policy argument entirely as a historical parallel
between the 1990s computer revolution (Greenspan era) and today's AI moment.
This is a methodological/analytical speech, not a current stance speech.

Core historical precedent:
  "At the same time, FOMC policymakers were concerned about elevated
   inflation and a tightening labor market. Standard macro models and
   monetary policy thinking suggested interest rates should rise to ward
   off overheating in the labor market and a more significant run-up in
   inflation. Then-Chair Greenspan offered a different view... posited that
   the burgeoning computer revolution would spur sustained productivity
   growth, allowing the economy to grow faster without putting upward
   pressure on prices. In this scenario, the economy would not need to be
   bridled, and interest rates would not need to increase."
  → The historical narrative: Greenspan's productivity-optimism justified
    patient/dovish policy despite apparent overheating signals. He was right.

Key outcome statement:
  "In the end, the FOMC remained patient on policy and the roaring '90s
   followed, with a strong labor market and sustained growth."
  → Patience = good outcome. This is the implicit lesson Daly is drawing.

Lessons extracted:
  1. "we won't find all the answers in the aggregate data on productivity,
     the labor market, or inflation. Seeing developments before they fully
     emerge requires digging deeper, relying on disaggregated information
     that foreshadows transformation."
  2. "There is no matrix that tells us exactly what data to follow at any
     moment in time. Greenspan's innovation wasn't looking at more data; it
     was looking at the right data."
  3. "Talking to businesses matters. Businesses invest, experiment, and learn
     long before we see it in aggregate productivity data."

Policy implication (implicit):
  → If AI is genuinely boosting productivity (as computers did in the 1990s),
    then apparent inflationary or labor-tightening signals may overstate the
    need for restrictive policy. The 1990s lesson is: be open to being wrong
    about the inflation/growth tradeoff in the presence of a transformative
    technology. Patience was rewarded.

Explicit disclaimer:
  "none of this is a playbook for managing the economy and AI, but it is a
   foundation."
  → Daly explicitly hedges: the 1990s analogy is not a prescription.

Direction: NEUTRAL with mild dovish tilt from historical analogy.
  The 1990s precedent implies openness to patient/accommodative policy when
  productivity transformation is underway. But Daly explicitly says this is
  not a playbook — the speech offers no current policy direction.
