GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Economic activity has been expanding at a solid pace." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Williams: "The US economy has so far remained remarkably resilient.
    Real GDP grew 2 percent in 2025 and is poised to maintain that pace."

Own language: Forecasts 2-2.25% real GDP growth in 2026 and 2027.
  Consumer spending resilient, business investment robust (AI-driven).
  Declines in residential construction and federal government spending
  are being offset. Supply-chain disruptions emerging (PMI flash surveys
  showed "abrupt, sharp increases in delivery times and input prices").
  Notes "new round of tariffs in the coming months" as additional upside
  risk to import prices.

Direction: neutral — solid baseline; AI investment and consumer strength
  offsetting drags; new tariff round flagged as additional risk.

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, on average, and the unemployment rate
  has been little changed in recent months." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Williams echoes the hard data: 4.3% unemployment unchanged 9 months;
    payroll growth "low but at levels consistent with underlying labor
    force growth." UI claims and layoff measures "low and stable."

Own language — KEY (soft data):
  "Some soft data suggest a less sanguine view. Perceptions of job
  availability from the Conference Board survey have continued to trend
  downward. Similarly, we have yet to see signs of sustained stabilization
  in the 'job security gap'... Together, these indicators suggest
  increasing labor market slack."
  → This is Williams' verdict on the soft data indicators in this paragraph
    specifically — not his overall labor market characterization. His
    overall framing is the speech's opening labor sentence: "conflicting
    signs — Much of the hard data points to stabilization, while some of
    the soft data suggest continued gradual slowing."

Own language — HAWKISH ANCHOR:
  "Unlike then [post-pandemic], the labor market is not adding to inflation
   pressures... This is reassuring, since one lesson from the post-pandemic
   period was that a perfect storm of higher input and labor costs led to
   larger-than-normal pass-through to prices."
  → Explicitly distinguishes the current supply shock from 2021-22.
    Labor is NOT the problem — it cannot compound the inflation shock.

Shift vs. Apr 16:   MILD DOVISH on soft data — Apr 16 noted hard-soft
  divergence; May 4 adds explicit conclusion that soft data indicators
  "suggest increasing labor market slack." Overall framing unchanged:
  "conflicting signs."
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 30): MILD DOVISH on soft data — labor concern
  more explicit; overall framing still "conflicting signs."
Direction: neutral — overall characterization is "conflicting signs"
  (hard data stable, soft data gradually slowing). "Increasing labor
  market slack" is the conclusion of the soft data subsection only,
  not Williams' verdict on the labor market as a whole.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase in
   global energy prices." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Williams: "Overall inflation... rose to 3-1/2 percent in March.
    The combination of higher tariffs and energy prices has contributed
    about a percentage point to that figure."

Own language — KEY (look-through anchors):
  "I anticipate the pass-through of current tariffs to prices to be
   mostly completed in the next few months and therefore their effects
   on the inflation rate to fade."
  "Underlying inflation outside of imported goods and energy has so far
   remained stable, and there are still no signs of significant second-
   round effects from tariffs spilling over to the rest of the economy."
  "Inflation expectations have remained well anchored despite the deluge
   of shocks." NY Fed SCE: 1-year ahead picked up, but "medium- and
   longer-term expectations have remained solidly anchored."
  → Three-layer look-through case: (1) tariff pass-through is temporary;
    (2) underlying inflation stable — no second-round effects; (3) medium/
    long-run expectations solidly anchored. This directly contradicts
    Hammack's "broad based" framing and Logan's "pre-existing above-2%"
    concern.

Own complication:
  "I also expect there will be a new round of tariffs in the coming
   months, which would put additional upward pressure on import prices."
  → Acknowledges the look-through case is complicated by further tariff
    rounds. Still frames it as temporary, but it's a meaningful caveat.

Forecast: 3% inflation in 2026, then 2% in 2027 as tariff and energy
  effects "move into the rearview mirror."

Shift vs. Apr 16:   UNCHANGED in direction. Similar look-through
  framing. "New round of tariffs" is an addition.
Direction: neutral with dovish lean — look-through case fully articulated;
  "stable underlying" directly counters the dissenters' narrative.

Own language: No cut signal. Symmetric risk framing. "The risks to both

  Repeats commitment to data-dependence.

Cut signal status: ABSENT — no change from Apr 16 retraction.
Shift vs. Apr 16:   UNCHANGED on policy stance.
Direction: neutral — patient hold confirmed; no new guidance.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL — Troika holdline maintained; soft data on labor
               points to some slowing, but Williams' overall verdict is
               "conflicting signs," not a directional dovish call.

Directional:   [Inference: Williams' framing functions as the Troika
  response to the hawkish dissenters — Williams does not reference
  the dissenters or frame his speech as a rebuttal.] Three factual
  departures from the dissenter narrative:
  (1) Underlying inflation "stable" with "no second-round effects" —
      directly contradicts Hammack's "broad based" framing
  (2) "Increasing labor market slack" per soft data — maintains the
      dovish leg of the dual-risk argument the dissenters want to remove
  (3) Expectations "solidly anchored" — counters Kashkari's unanchoring
      risk framing in the adverse scenario

  The committee fault line is now clearly drawn: dissenters see
  pre-existing/broad inflation with anchored labor; Williams sees
  energy/tariff inflation with mixed labor signals (hard data stable,
  soft data showing some slowing). These two narratives
  cannot coexist in the statement language indefinitely — June will
  force a resolution.

Language watch:
  • "Increasing labor market slack" (soft data) — new and explicit.
    If Jefferson echoes this, it represents Troika consensus that labor
    is softening and the dovish leg remains valid.
  • "No significant second-round effects" — Williams' anchor against the
    "broad based" framing. This is the key factual dispute with the
    dissenters. Watch core services ex-housing data.
  • "New round of tariffs in the coming months" — first Troika mention
    of additional tariff rounds as an inflation upside risk.

  → Williams' framing directly contradicts the three dissenters on
