LABOR MARKET

Own verdict (verbatim repeat from May 12):
  "the job market in my mind is stable without being good."
  → Identical characterization to May 12 ("stable without being good").
    UNCHANGED.

Own framework (repeat):
  "the four horsemen of the truth … the unemployment rate, the vacancy rate,
   the layoff rate, and the hiring rate … The labor market has been remarkably

  → Same "four horsemen" frame as May 12. Same low-hire/low-fire paradox

  → Distributional point (recent grads bear the low-hiring environment);
    color, not a directional shift.

Shift vs. prior speech (May 12):  UNCHANGED — verdict, framework, and causal

Shift vs. pre-FOMC (May 12):      UNCHANGED.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "supply shocks that have driven price increases in certain sectors,
   including energy" [Jun 17 stmt]
  → Speaker version: tracks the same supply-shock framing in his own words —
    tariffs ("supposed to be temporary … one-time increase") plus the energy/
    war shock, layered on a pre-existing above-target run. Consistent with
    the committee's "sequence of supply shocks" language now in the statement.

Own language — KEY (services persistence, maintained from May 12):
  "there's been a kind of a disturbingly high component of services inflation
   which isn't really from oil prices … isn't really from tariffs … it's worth
   keeping an eye on that."
  → SAME core concern as May 12 ("services going the wrong way … the part I'm
    nervous about"). The services-persistence worry is maintained, framed as
    the hard-to-look-through component. Tone slightly less alarmed than the
    fresh-CPI reaction of May 12, but the substance is unchanged.

Energy — symmetric caution (NEW emphasis):
  "I cautioned them on the way up … I'm cautioning you on the way down. Don't
   overreact either … the most important thing is trying to get the through
   line of inflation."
  → Explicit symmetry: just as he looked through the energy spike upward, he
    will look through a war-de-escalation energy decline. A potential oil
    decline is "a nice tailwind … headline inflation could come way down,"
    but he refuses to let it pull policy dovish. NET HAWKISH-NEUTRAL: declines
    to bank disinflation he views as noise.

Tariffs (persistence):
  "more persistent than people anticipated … runup of prices was delayed more
   than we thought … now it's been more persistent than we thought."
  → Reinforces the persistence narrative; recent goods CPI showed "a little
    bit of progress … back to mild deflation," but he explicitly discounts one
    month ("one month is no months … certainly doesn't make a trend").

Shift vs. prior speech (May 12):  hawkish lean — services concern maintained;
  ADDS explicit symmetric refusal to look dovishly through an energy decline,
  and discounts the one favorable goods-CPI print. Net: holds the hawkish line
  rather than easing it as energy headlines improve.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (May 12):      same.

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed / committee process:
  Extended, sympathetic commentary on Chair Warsh removing forward guidance —
  the single most distinctive Jun 17 statement change:
  "the statement this time was dramatically shorter and did not involve … forward
   guidance … Chairman Warsh has never been a public fan of forward guidance …
   I have always been uneasy with the use of forward guidance on a routine basis."
  Likens it to "a caffeine cleanse." Names the cost of guidance: it can make the
  Committee "slow-footed … move too late," and a missed prediction dings credibility.
  → This is process/governance endorsement of the new communications regime, NOT
    a personal rate-path signal. Treat as committee-consensus alignment (he is
    ratifying the Warsh statement architecture), per the top-down language rule.
    He also flags Warsh as a "foxhole buddy" from the financial crisis — collegial
    signal, not analytical.

Own language — NEW hawkish-capable framing (productivity overheating):
  "this is the most expected increase in productivity growth … of our lifetimes …
   expected productivity growth is … an increase in your wealth today … you start
   spending now … IPOs worth a trillion dollars … data centers have massive
   investments … you can easily overheat the economy in the short run before the
   magic of the productivity has arrived."
  Greenspan/late-1990s parallel, carried to its hawkish conclusion:
  "by 1999 … the Fed actually starts raising the interest rates very rapidly …
   on the grounds that … this is overheating the economy and there are dangers of
   inflation."
  → This is the speech's most consequential new content. Goolsbee frames the AI
    productivity boom as a potential SHORT-RUN OVERHEATING / inflation risk
    (demand pulled forward via wealth effects and capex), explicitly invoking a
    historical episode that ended in rapid hikes. Hawkish-capable. Aligns with
    the June 17 SEP's hawkish turn and the statement's strong-investment language.

Own language — primary worry is PERSISTENCE, not labor downside:
  "the worrisome stuff … in the Fed's sense … is that inflation proves
   substantially more persistent than we are currently forecasting … the Fed made
   a mistake thinking inflation is just going to go away … people already got
   burned once … if people go down … 'I think inflation's going to be 7% next
   year, so I'm going to need 8% wage' … that kind of a spiral … is really
   difficult to get out of."
  → Names inflation persistence + expectations de-anchoring as his top policy
    worry, invoking the 2021–22 transitory error as the cautionary template.
    Vigilance framing, hawkish.

Cut signal status: ABSENT this appearance. The May 12 conditional dovish signal
  — "rates can come down still a fair amount" pending inflation progress — was
  NOT repeated. [Inference: partly topic-driven — the moderator never asked his
  preferred rate path directly, so the absence is not by itself a deliberate
  retraction. Do not over-read it as a withdrawn cut call.] However, the
  affirmative content he DID volunteer (productivity-overheating risk + inflation-
  persistence vigilance + refusal to look dovishly through energy) all leans
  hawkish, with zero offsetting cut-path language. Net effect: his stance reads
  meaningfully less dovish than May 12.

Shift vs. prior speech (May 12):  hawkish lean — drops the explicit "rates can
  come down still a fair amount" cut signal; adds the productivity-overheating
  risk and the inflation-persistence vigilance frame.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (May 12):      same.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH LEAN (vs. his own May 12 baseline).
               Labor is unchanged ("stable without being good," four horsemen).
               Inflation concern is maintained on services and HARDENED by an
               explicit refusal to look dovishly through a potential energy
               decline. Monetary policy is the real move: the May 12 conditional
               cut signal is gone, replaced by (a) a new AI-productivity SHORT-RUN
               OVERHEATING risk drawn to a hike-ending historical parallel, and
               (b) inflation-persistence/expectations vigilance anchored to the
               2021–22 error. He also ratifies Warsh's forward-guidance removal.
               This is the first concrete evidence that the hawkish June 17
               consensus has penetrated to a non-voting regional president — the
               key question the June 22 report flagged for this event.

Language watch:
  • "you can easily overheat the economy in the short run before the magic of
     the productivity has arrived" — AI/productivity reframed as an OVERHEATING/
     inflation risk rather than a clean disinflationary tailwind. Novel among the
     regionals; watch whether Williams/Paulson/Jefferson pick up the
     productivity-overheating frame. Language cycle: DISPERSED (single official).
  • "stable without being good" — labor verdict, now used in ≥2 consecutive
     Goolsbee appearances. Stable personal idiom; not spreading committee-wide.
  • "I'm cautioning you on the way down. Don't overreact" — symmetric look-through
     on energy; a marker that the committee will not treat oil-driven headline
     relief as policy-relevant disinflation. Watch for echo in Troika language.
  • "caffeine cleanse" (forward-guidance removal) — colorful ratification of the
     Warsh communications regime; quotable, but governance commentary, not a rate
     signal.

Analyst flag: YES
  → Goolsbee — a Non Voter who as recently as May 12 still volunteered "rates can

    introduced a productivity-driven OVERHEATING risk plus persistence vigilance.
    Assess: is this a genuine hawkish migration of a previously dovish-leaning

    preference directly? His Feb 24 "not obvious rates are restrictive" line was
    NOT revisited here — worth probing whether he still holds it given the SEP's
    hawkish turn. Confirm against his next directly-policy-focused appearance.
