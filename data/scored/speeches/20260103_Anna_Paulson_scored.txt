GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Own language:
  "GDP growth has been very strong, with Q3 real GDP coming in at an
   above-trend 4.3 percent." — Strong on the headline.
  "Income growth has not kept up with consumption growth, and the
   savings rate fell in Q3." — Compositional warning: consumption
   is debt/wealth-financed, not income-driven. Downside risk.
  "Contacts consistently report that low-income households are struggling
   pressured by high prices, and they are increasingly concerned about
   job security. High-income households are driving elevated consumption
   growth." — Same narrow-demand framing as Barkin Jan 6.
  "When GDP and labor market signals are in conflict, the labor market
   signal turns out to be more accurate." — Explicitly endorses labor
   data over GDP as the more reliable leading indicator.

Direction: DOVISH lean — growth strength discounted as narrow and
  savings-funded; labor market treated as more reliable signal.

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Own verdict:
  "The labor market is clearly bending, it is not breaking." —
  The key phrase. Sets a dovish framing: acknowledges weakness
  without alarm.
  Unemployment at 4.6% (November) — "about a half a percentage
  point above the range that prevailed through July of last year."
  "Payroll growth has fallen substantially and the base for job
   creation has narrowed. Nearly 90 percent of net private job
   creation occurred in a single sector: health care."

Own qualifier / addition:
  "Labor market risks have risen and that has been an important
   factor in my support for the 75 basis points of cuts last year.
   I will be monitoring labor market developments closely."
  → Explicitly links cut support to labor risk. Dovish framing
    on future monitoring.
  "Slowdown in demand appears to have outpaced the slowdown in
   supply." — Demand weakness, not just supply normalization.
   Slightly more bearish on labor than some Troika language.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Own language — KEY:
  "If I had to pick a theme for inflation for 2026 it would be
   'cautious optimism.'" — Personal framing that is dovish relative
   to the committee's "remains elevated" language.
  "Tariff-induced price adjustments are largely confined to goods...
   a shift in the price level but do not create sustained inflation." —
   Same look-through framing as Williams/Jefferson. Transitory.
  "I see a decent chance that we will end the year with inflation
   close to 2 percent on a run-rate basis; that is, 12-month inflation
   may still be a little elevated, but three-month inflation will be
   2 percent by the end of the year." — Specific and constructive.
   Trimmed-mean / run-rate framing rather than headline 12-month.
  Shelter: "Unambiguously good" — falling from 5.1% to 3.7% year-over-year.
  Services ex-shelter: Declining from 3.5% to 3.3% — "moving in the
  right direction."

Direction: DOVISH — "cautious optimism" vs. committee's "remains
  elevated"; run-rate framing shifts the metric dovishly.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    DOVISH lean — Paulson is meaningfully more dovish than
               the Troika at the January baseline: "still a little
               restrictive" (vs. Troika's "at/near neutral"), an
               explicit conditional cut signal for later 2026, and
               endorsement of the labor market signal over GDP data.
               The inflation framing is constructive ("cautious
               optimism"), broadly consistent with but slightly more
               optimistic than Williams/Jefferson.

Language watch:
  • "Still a little restrictive" — rate characterization; more
    dovish than Troika's "at/near neutral." Paulson's cut bar is
    lower than the Troika's.
  • "Some modest further adjustments later in the year" — conditional
    cut signal; watch whether this erodes or strengthens post-Iran
    conflict.
  • "Labor market signal turns out to be more accurate than GDP" —
    Paulson's analytical weighting. Relevant when GDP and labor
    diverge again (as they do in May 2026 with the energy shock).
  • 1990s AI productivity parallel — argues for patience rather
    than reflexive tightening. Watch for this to appear across
    other speakers.

Analyst flag:  NO — clean baseline establishment. Key calibration
  note: Paulson at the Jan 3 baseline is positioned as the committee's
  median voter with a dovish lean on rates. The subsequent question
  is whether the Iran energy shock reverses this.
