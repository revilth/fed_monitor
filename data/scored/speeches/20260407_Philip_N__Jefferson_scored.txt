──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, and the unemployment rate has
   been little changed in recent months." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Jefferson: "three-month moving average of job growth...
    averaged about 70,000 jobs per month...somewhat subdued."
    Also echoes the stabilization theme.

Own verdict:
  "I see the labor market as roughly in balance."
  → Identical to Mar 26 characterization. Stable.

Own qualifier (⚠ carried forward from Mar 26):
  "...but susceptible to adverse shocks."
  → Present again in Apr 7. This is now a durable addition
    to his labor verdict, not a one-time observation.

Own addition (new in Apr 7):
  "While the labor market appears to be stabilizing, I remain
   cautious about that assessment."
  → Explicit caution qualifier on the stabilization read.
    Slightly more cautious than Mar 26.

  Despite better March jobs number (+178K), he emphasizes:
  "Through the first quarter of the year, employers added an
   average of about 70,000 jobs per month...somewhat subdued."
  → Consciously de-emphasizes the strong March headline and
    focuses on the soft 3-month average. Downside risk signal.

Shift vs. Mar 26 (post-FOMC prior):   MINIMAL — same verdict;
  caution carried forward; one good month doesn't move him.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 6):   DOVISH — Feb 6 did not flag
  downside risks explicitly; Apr 7 does ("susceptible to
  adverse shocks," "cautious about" stabilization assessment).

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Jefferson: "Inflation remains above the Federal Reserve's
    2 percent target...progress has stalled over the past
    year mainly due to tariffs."

Own language — KEY:
  "It has been my expectation that the disinflationary process
   would resume once higher tariffs are no longer pushing up
   consumer prices. [...] The recent increase in energy prices,
   however, will apply some upward pressure on headline
   inflation, at least in the near term. The ongoing trade
   policy uncertainty and geopolitical tensions pose upside
   risk to my inflation forecast."
  → Structure matters: he states his prior expectation, then
    concedes the energy shock complicates it. "Upside risk
    to my inflation forecast" — personal acknowledgment that
    his baseline has deteriorated. This phrase was first used
    in Mar 26 and is now repeated, making it a durable
    characterization, not a one-time flag.

Shift vs. Mar 26 (post-FOMC prior):   SLIGHT HAWKISH — same
  language as Mar 26 but "upside risk to my inflation forecast"
  is now more embedded rather than new.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 6):   HAWKISH — Feb 6 was confident
  disinflation would resume; Apr 7 now has energy and
  geopolitical complications layered on.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  → Jefferson: "I am confident that our current policy stance
    is well-positioned to respond to a range of outcomes."
    (Near-verbatim committee holding formula.)

Own language:
  "The current stance should continue to support the labor
   market while allowing inflation to resume its decline
   toward our 2 percent target as the effects of tariff
   pass-through are completed."
  → Slightly dovish framing: "allowing inflation to resume
    its decline" implies confidence that the policy already
    in place is sufficient. No new tightening needed.

  "I confront an outlook in which there is downside risk to
   the labor market and upside risk to inflation."
  → Dual-risk formula: identical to Mar 26. Now clearly his
    stable characterization, repeated verbatim.

Cut signal status: ABSENT — "well positioned" formula,
  unchanged from Mar 26.

Shift vs. Mar 26 (post-FOMC prior):   NONE — identical formula.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Feb 6):   NONE — "well positioned" stable.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL — locked into patient hold; no movement
               in either direction despite better March data

Directional:   Jefferson's Apr 7 speech is nearly identical
  to Mar 26 in framing. The one genuine new signal: "upside
  risk to my inflation forecast" is now durable (appeared in
  both Mar 26 and Apr 7), and his caution on the labor
  stabilization read is maintained despite the better March
  jobs number. He is not moving toward cuts on one month of
  good data.

    statement or Williams' speech.
  • "Upside risk to my inflation forecast" — durable inflation
    qualifier. Personal; not yet in statement. If Williams

  • Dual-risk formula ("downside to labor, upside to
    inflation, well positioned") — now Williams' language too
    (Mar 30, Apr 16). Likely becoming committee-wide.

Analyst flag: NO — trajectory clear and stable.
