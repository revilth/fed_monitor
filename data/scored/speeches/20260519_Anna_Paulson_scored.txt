"Navigating Uncertainty: Inflation, Labor Markets, and the Stance of Monetary Policy"
Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta's 2026 Financial Markets Conference, Amelia Island, FL
SOURCE: https://www.philadelphiafed.org/the-economy/monetary-policy/260519-navigating-uncertainty-inflation-labor-markets-and-the-stance-of-monetary-policy

GROWTH
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed: not directly invoked
  → Paulson characterizes overall activity favorably.

Own language:
  "GDP grew 2 percent [last year] and it is on track to do
   the same this year. Headline growth in the first quarter
   was solid, and business investment was a bright spot."
  → No growth concern. AI-driven investment supporting
    conditions broadly.

  "Growth in consumer spending...is more modest. In the first
   quarter, consumption grew at an annual real rate of 1.6
   percent, down from 2.1 percent last year."
  → Consumption deceleration noted, but not alarming — consumer
    still spending, just at a slower pace.

  "both households and businesses are cautious...Some projects
   are being put on hold until there's more clarity."
  → Forward guidance is softer — uncertainty drag, not hard data
    weakness. "Few signs that consumers are pulling back sharply
    in the aggregate."

Shift vs. prior speech (Mar 27):   neutral — AI buildout as growth
  driver consistent with March framing; consumer caution more explicit.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 27):       neutral — same baseline.

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  Apr 29 stmt: unemployment "has moved up but remains low"
  → Paulson: "the unemployment rate has been remarkably steady
    and near what I consider to be full employment for many
    months...a still low 4.3 percent last month."
  Consistent with committee framing; she adds "near full
  employment" characterization.

Own verdict:
  "The hard data suggest a labor market that is stable and
   consistent with full employment. But what we are hearing
   from people — both directly and through surveys — suggests
   somewhat softer labor market conditions."
  → Hard/soft data divergence acknowledged explicitly. Labor
    market is stable but not complacent — she is watching.

  "My base case is that the labor market will remain stable
   and that we will end the year with an unemployment rate in
   the range that has prevailed over the past few years."
  → Central case: no deterioration, roughly 4.3% at year-end.

Own qualifier:
  "This is the only time the unemployment rate has risen by
   one percentage point or more outside of a recession."
  → Historical anomaly framing — notes how unusual this
    softening cycle is. Implicitly acknowledges downside risk
    without endorsing it as base case.

  NY Fed Consumer Expectations survey: "mean probability that
   the unemployment rate will be higher a year from now was
   44 percent in April, up from 34 percent in January of last
   year."
  → Soft data showing elevated labor pessimism — she flags
    this but does not treat it as her own verdict.

Shift vs. prior speech (Mar 27):   slight hawkish lean — March framing
  "I see no signs that the labor market is contributing to
  inflation today" is absent. May framing is neutral: stable hard
  data, softer soft data, monitoring.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 27):       same observation — labor assessment
  has moved from explicitly dovish (no labor-inflation link) to
  neutral (stable but watching soft signals).

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  Apr 29 stmt: "Inflation remains somewhat elevated"
  → Paulson: "Inflation is too high." — More emphatic.
    She leads both opening and inflation section with this verdict.

Own language — KEY:
  "Inflation is too high. Even before the conflict in the
   Middle East and the recent spike in oil and gas prices,
   inflation was elevated."
  → Establishes pre-shock baseline as already elevated, not
    just pushed there by the shock.

  "Headline PCE inflation was 3.5 percent in March, and core
   was 3.2 percent. Last week's data on consumer and wholesale
   prices for April point to continuing pressures."
  → Current readings cited explicitly: headline 3.5%, core
    3.2%. March 27 baseline was 2.8% core PCE. Significant
    deterioration.

  "Gas prices, which are up more than 50 percent since the
   beginning of the year."
  → Energy shock quantified. Strong.

  "Both tariffs and the disruptions to production and shipping
   associated with hostilities in the Middle East are classic
   supply shocks. Directionally, they increase prices and
   reduce output."
  → Stagflationary characterization — does not label it
    transitory. Uses "classic supply shocks" framing, then
    asks the key question: should policy look through?

Three-factor assessment (her framework for deciding on look-through):
  1. Strength of activity: "the pace of economic activity does
     not appear to be adding materially to inflationary
     pressures." → not amplifying
  2. Inflation expectations: long-term "stable" (2–2.5%);
     short-term moved up — "an area I'm watching closely, but
     currently long run expectations are in a good place."
  → IMPORTANT: long-term expectations framed as contingent
    ("currently"), not unconditional. This is less confident
    than the committee's standard "well anchored" boilerplate.
    March 27: "may be a little more fragile" — same personal
    caution maintained.
  3. Policy stance: "policy is mildly restrictive" — providing
     the buffer against amplification.

Shift vs. prior speech (Mar 27):   hawkish lean — core PCE moved from
  2.8% to 3.2%; headline from ~2.6% to 3.5%; new supply shocks
  (energy, tariffs, Middle East). March baseline was "above 2%
  for six years but declining"; May baseline is "too high" and
  rising on new shocks.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 27):       hawkish lean — same observation.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  Apr 29: "well positioned to respond to a range of outcomes"
  → Paulson: "I believe monetary policy is currently
    well-positioned to balance the risks." — Near-verbatim.

Own language — KEY:
  "The bottom line: I believe monetary policy is appropriately
   positioned to navigate current challenges. Policy is mildly
   restrictive and that restrictiveness is helping to keep
   inflation pressures in check while the labor market remains
   stable."
  → Policy holding verdict: "mildly restrictive" as adequate
    given current conditions. Emphasis on restrictiveness as
    the active inflation-control mechanism.

  "Keeping rates steady allows us to assess how the economy
   is evolving and the risks to both price stability and the
   labor market."
  → Hold stance explicitly stated. "Both" mandate legs invoked.

  "Assuming the labor market remains in balance, rate cuts
   would only become appropriate once we have seen sustained
   progress on inflation."
  → CUT CONDITION: dual requirement — (1) labor market
    stability maintained, AND (2) "sustained progress on
    inflation." High bar. "Sustained" implies not one data
    point; implies a trajectory, not a level.

  "I think it is healthy that market participants have taken
   on board scenarios where the funds rate remains unchanged
   for an extended period, as well as scenarios where further
   tightening becomes necessary."
  → MOST IMPORTANT SENTENCE. Paulson explicitly validates
    market pricing for both extended hold AND further
    tightening. This is the clearest forward guidance of
    the speech — she is not ruling out hikes.

  Markets: January → "three cuts this year"; post-conflict
  → "perhaps one rate cut"; more recently → "steady rates
  or even a modest tightening." "The way the market has
  moved...largely aligns with my own thinking."
  → Self-endorses market's hawkward repricing.

Cut signal status: ABSENT — explicit cut conditionality
  ("sustained progress on inflation") and validation of
  tightening scenarios both work against near-term cuts.

Shift vs. prior speech (Mar 27):   hawkish lean — March: "more
  cautious" at 2.8% PCE, cut signal absent; May: "mildly
  restrictive" holding appropriate, validates further
  tightening scenarios. Explicit move from "patient hold"
  framing toward "watch for tightening" territory.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 27):       same — hawkish lean.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH LEAN — holds at "mildly restrictive,"
               raises the bar for cuts to "sustained progress
               on inflation," and explicitly validates both
               extended hold and further tightening scenarios.

This speech represents a meaningful hawkish shift from the
March baseline. Paulson in March was cautious-hawkish at
2.8% core PCE; in May, with core at 3.2% and headline at
3.5%, driven by supply shocks she explicitly declines to
dismiss as "transitory," her bar for cuts has effectively
risen. Her personal deviation from the committee's "well
anchored" expectations language continues (now "in a good
place" vs. unconditional "well anchored"). She self-endorses
market repricing toward extended hold / modest tightening.

As the assessed committee median voter, this framing is
significant: it suggests the committee's cut threshold
currently requires sustained disinflation from elevated
levels — not just absence of acceleration.

Language watch:
  • "Sustained progress on inflation" — cut condition. Note
    whether she softens or hardens this formulation.
  • "Extended hold" and "further tightening" — market scenario
    endorsement. Adoption by other members would cement a
    regime shift in committee language.
  • "Currently long run expectations are in a good place" —
    conditional phrasing; more cautious than standard boilerplate.

Analyst flag: YES
  → Paulson endorses the market's hawkward repricing (from 3
    cuts to hold/tighten) as "largely aligning" with her own
    thinking. Combined with her explicit "sustained progress"
    cut bar and tightening scenario validation, this raises
    the key question: at what inflation trajectory does the
    committee median (Paulson) become willing to cut?
    Analyst to assess: does core PCE at 3.2% rule out cuts
    for the rest of 2026, or only for the next 1-2 meetings?
