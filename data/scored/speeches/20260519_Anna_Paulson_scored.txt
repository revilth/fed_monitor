GROWTH

[Treatment warranted — speaker flags AI investment as growth support
and notes consumption moderation. Compositional concern present.]

FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Recent indicators suggest that economic activity has been expanding
   at a solid pace." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Paulson: "Last year, real GDP grew 2 percent, and it is on track
    to do the same this year. Headline growth in the first quarter was
    solid, and business investment was a bright spot."

Own language:

   the AI buildout and that is supporting business conditions more

  "the dynamics supporting the economy that were at play in 2025 could

Shift vs. prior speech:    DOVISH LEAN — growth framing is similar but
  she explicitly acknowledges consumer moderation that was not a theme

Shift vs. pre-FOMC:        UNCHANGED — same characterization of solid
  GDP growth (~2%), consistent with prior baseline.

LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, on average, and the unemployment rate
   has been little changed in recent months." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Paulson: "the unemployment rate has been remarkably steady and near
    what I consider to be full employment for many months...a still low
    4.3 percent last month."

Own verdict:
  "The hard data suggest a labor market that is stable and consistent
   with full employment." — Benign characterization of hard data.

Own qualifier / addition:
  "But what we are hearing from people — both directly and through
   surveys — suggests somewhat softer labor market conditions."
  — Introduces soft data divergence. More cautious than March 27.

  "Going all the way back to 1948...this is the only time the
   unemployment rate has risen by one percentage point or more outside
   of a recession." — Historical anomaly framing. Acknowledges the
   unusual low-hiring/low-firing balance as inherently fragile.

  "AI and what it will mean for jobs. Some contacts tell us that there
   is growing pressure in corporate boardrooms...to translate AI
   efficiencies into cost savings, including through layoffs."
  — New risk flag: AI-driven labor demand reduction. Not in March 27.

Own policy link:
  "Assuming the labor market remains in balance, rate cuts would only
   become appropriate once we have seen sustained progress on
   inflation." — Labor is explicitly subordinated to inflation as the
   cut threshold. Hold labor in balance = necessary but not sufficient
   for cuts; inflation progress is the binding condition.

Shift vs. prior speech:    DOVISH LEAN ON ASSESSMENT — soft data
  divergence and AI downside risk are new additions vs. March 27's
  "I see no signs that the labor market is contributing to inflation."
  She's more watchful but base case remains stable.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:        DOVISH LEAN — March 27 had no soft data
  concern or AI layoff risk. May 19 introduces both.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase in
   global energy prices." [Apr 29 stmt]
  → Paulson: "Headline PCE inflation was 3.5 percent in March, and
    core was 3.2 percent." Explicitly attributes to "tariffs and the
    spike in energy and commodity prices associated with the conflict
    in the Middle East."

Own language — KEY:
  "Inflation is too high." — Direct. Not qualified.

  "After five years of elevated inflation and a series of supply
   shocks, this is a real concern." [on expectations anchoring]
  — Elevates inflation expectations fragility. Compare March 27:
    "Long-term inflation expectations are consistent with 2 percent,
    but they may also be a little more fragile." May 19 version is
    more explicit and contextualizes within the sequence of shocks.

  "I do want to emphasize that continued high inflation does not
   appear to be driven by labor market pressures." — Notable: she
   explicitly exculpates labor from the inflation problem. This
   removes the dual-shock worst case (demand + supply simultaneously).

Look-through framework (3 factors, assessed explicitly):
  1. Demand strength: "the pace of economic activity does not appear
     to be adding materially to inflationary pressures." — Low concern.
  2. Inflation expectations: "long run expectations are in a good
     place" but "This is an area I'm watching closely." — Conditional.
  3. Monetary policy stance: "monetary policy is mildly restrictive
     and that restrictiveness is helping to keep...effects in check."
     — Her conclusion: current stance is doing the work.
  → [Inference: her 3-factor framework is structured to justify
    holding (not tightening) given that all three conditions are
    currently manageable. But she flags expectations as the trip wire.]

Note on look-through precision: This is a DESCRIPTIVE framework —
  she reasons through three factors but does not name explicit
  scenarios with pre-committed policy responses (contrast Waller Apr 17
  "BENIGN / ADVERSE" with named outcomes). Descriptive, not named-scenario.

Shift vs. prior speech:    HAWKISH LEAN — core inflation has moved
  from 2.8% (March 27) to 3.2% (May 19). But her framework concludes
  current policy is still appropriate. Expectations fragility framing
  is more explicit. The shift is in the data inputs, not the framework.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:        HAWKISH LEAN — same framework, harder data.

MONETARY POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  April 29 statement included easing bias language (the language
  Hammack/Kashkari/Logan dissented against). Paulson VOTED FOR that
  language. In this speech, she does NOT echo any easing bias. The
  statement's easing posture is absent from her May 19 remarks.

Own language:
  "Policy is mildly restrictive and that restrictiveness is helping
   to keep inflation pressures in check." — "Mildly restrictive" is
   her stable rate characterization (same as March 6 and March 27).

  "I believe monetary policy is in a good place now. Keeping rates
   steady allows us to assess how the economy is evolving."
  — Clear hold signal. No forward guidance.

  "rate cuts would only become appropriate once we have seen sustained
   progress on inflation." — Explicit, high bar for cuts. Requires
   sustained disinflation, not just one good print.

  "I think it is healthy that market participants have taken on board
   scenarios where the funds rate remains unchanged for an extended
   period, as well as scenarios where further tightening becomes
   necessary." — KEY SENTENCE. She explicitly validates market pricing
   of (a) extended hold and (b) tightening. This is the most hawkish
   signal in the speech. It goes beyond the April 29 easing bias that
   she voted for.

  [Inference: Paulson voted for easing bias language on April 29 but
   is now implicitly walking it back. By endorsing tightening scenarios
   as "healthy" market pricing, she is signaling the May 19 energy
   shock context has shifted her posture relative to her April 29
   vote. This should be flagged for analyst review.]

Cut signal status: ABSENT — explicit cut bar is "sustained progress
  on inflation." Tightening scenarios explicitly endorsed as valid.

Shift vs. prior speech:    HAWKISH LEAN — March 27 had "Getting
  inflation to 2% and keeping unemployment low" as the framing.
  May 19 explicitly endorses tightening scenarios. This is a
  meaningful escalation even if the "mildly restrictive" label
  is unchanged.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC:        HAWKISH LEAN — pre-FOMC March 27 had no
  tightening scenario endorsement. May 19 introduces it explicitly.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH LEAN
  Paulson has moved modestly hawkish from her March 27 baseline
  and, more significantly, has moved hawkish relative to her April 29
  vote for easing bias language. The key escalation: she now explicitly
  validates market pricing of tightening scenarios. Her inflation-first
  bar remains unchanged ("sustained progress" required before cuts),
  but she has now added an explicit upper tail. This is the first
  time she endorses tightening scenarios in the 2026 corpus.

Language watch:
  • "rate cuts would only become appropriate once we have seen
    sustained progress on inflation" — inflation-first cut bar,
    clearer and more explicit than March 27's formulation.
    Watch: does this spread to Jefferson or Williams?
  • "scenarios where further tightening becomes necessary" — new
    language. Not an immediate signal, but she's no longer off-limits
    on hikes. Language cycle stage: DISPERSED (only Paulson so far;
    compare Logan's hawkish dissent framing).
  • "mildly restrictive" — stable label. Collins used this term too
    on May 13. Emerging shared language for current rate characterization
    among non-Troika officials. If Troika adopts it, it signals they
    agree cuts are not imminent.

Analyst flag:  YES
  → Paulson voted for easing bias language on April 29, 2026. Her
    May 19 speech does not echo that language and instead explicitly
    endorses tightening scenarios. Analyst to assess: (1) Does this
    represent a genuine hawkish shift from her April 29 vote posture,
    or is it conditional hedging that stops short of actually advocating
    for tightening? (2) Given that she is the estimated median voter,
    does this signal the committee's effective easing bias has already
    eroded within 3 weeks of the April 29 meeting?
