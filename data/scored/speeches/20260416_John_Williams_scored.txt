LABOR MARKET
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Job gains have remained low, and the unemployment rate has
   been little changed in recent months." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Williams: "much of the hard data point to a stabilization
    in the balance between supply and demand."
    Echoes the committee's stabilization read.

Own verdict — KEY PHRASE:
  "Lately, the labor market has been displaying conflicting
   signs."
  → Williams' personal framing layer on top of committee
    language. "Conflicting signs" is MORE cautious than
    the statement's "little changed" — adds uncertainty
    that the statement does not contain.

Own addition — NEW vs. Mar 30:
  "...while some of the soft data suggest a labor market
   that continues to gradually soften."
  → Hard/soft data split: hard data stabilizing, soft data
    softening. This is Williams' analytical contribution;
    the soft data pessimism (Conference Board, NY Fed
    consumer surveys) is NOT in the FOMC statement.
    First time Williams names a potential labor softening
    signal in 2026 — a dovish lean on labor.

  "What all of this means is that it's a reasonably good
   labor market if you have steady employment. But in a
   low-hire, low-fire labor market, it's not so good if
   you are looking for a job or worried you may need one soon."
  → Ground-level nuance distinguishing the employed from
    job-seekers. More analytical than standard committee
    language.

Shift vs. Mar 30 (post-FOMC prior):   MILD DOVISH — Mar 30
  said "no signs of deterioration"; Apr 16 adds soft-data
  softening as a live concern for the first time.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 3):   DOVISH — Mar 3 saw "promising
  signs of stabilization"; Apr 16 now flags "conflicting
  signs" with soft-data deterioration.

INFLATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FOMC statement language echoed:
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." [Mar 18 stmt]
  → Williams: "overall inflation...was 2.8 percent. Higher
    tariffs have contributed between one half and three
    quarters of a percentage point to that figure."
    Same quantification as Mar 30; consistent framing.

Committee consensus language (repeated from prior speeches):
  "There are still no signs of significant second-round
   effects from tariffs spilling over to the rest of the
   economy."
  → Dovish anchor. Appears in all Williams 2026 speeches.
    Its continued presence is the dovish floor.

Own language:
  "Not only are elevated energy prices showing up in the
   rising cost of fuel, but there are also pass-through
   costs in the form of higher airfares, groceries,
   fertilizer, and other consumer products."
  → Personalizes and concretizes the energy shock. No single
    killer phrase, but reinforces that the shock is broad.

Own (new — first time):
  "The conflict could also result in a large supply shock
   that simultaneously raises inflation — through a surge
   in intermediate costs and commodity prices — and dampens
   economic activity."
  → STAGFLATION SCENARIO explicitly named for the first
    time by Williams. "Supply shock that simultaneously
    raises inflation and dampens activity" = stagflation.
    This is the tail risk being formalized in language.

Own forecast (revised further):
  "I expect overall inflation to come in between 2-3/4
   and 3 percent this year." [up from Mar 30's "~2-3/4%"]
  → Upper end now 3.0%. Second consecutive upward revision.

Shift vs. Mar 30 (post-FOMC prior):   MILD HAWKISH on
  inflation forecast (upper end raised to 3.0%); new
  stagflation tail risk named.
Shift vs. pre-FOMC (Mar 3):   HAWKISH — forecast up from
  2.5% to 2.75-3.0%; cut signal absent; stagflation risk
  named.

SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    NEUTRAL — stable hold; slight dovish lean on
               labor (soft data) offset by hawkish lean on
               inflation (higher forecast, stagflation risk)

Directional:   Apr 16 introduces two genuinely new signals:
  (1) Soft data suggests labor is "gradually softening" —
  the first labor downside signal from a Troika member not
  yet captured by the FOMC statement. (2) Stagflation
  scenario named explicitly. Neither changes the policy
  stance, but both extend the analytical framework.
  The cut signal remains absent for the third consecutive
  speech, confirming the Mar 30 retraction was deliberate.

Language watch:
  • "Conflicting signs" — Williams' labor verdict. If this
    language appears in Jefferson's next speech or the April
    FOMC statement, it signals the committee is formalizing
    a more cautious labor read.
  • Soft data softening (Conference Board, NY Fed surveys
    trending down) — not yet in committee language. Potential
    leading indicator for a dovish shift.
  • "Stagflation scenario" — newly named by Williams and
    Waller (Apr 17) in the same week. Two Troika/Just Voter
    speeches naming this in the same week is notable.
  • Cut signal ABSENT — confirmed durable. Watch for any
    post-April FOMC speech that reintroduces it.

Analyst flag: YES
  → The labor soft-data observation ("gradually softening")
    is the one genuinely new signal in this speech. If the
    April FOMC statement softens labor language from "little
    changed" toward "some signs of softening," this Williams
    speech was the leading indicator. Analyst to assess: is
    the NY Fed survey deterioration in job-finding expectations
    historically predictive of labor market turns?
