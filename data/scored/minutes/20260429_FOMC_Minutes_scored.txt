April 28–29, 2026 FOMC Meeting
Minutes released: May 20, 2026 (3 weeks post-meeting)

Corresponding statement: April 29, 2026
Corresponding press conference: April 29, 2026
  → SOURCE: https://www.federalreserve.gov/mediacenter/files/FOMCpresconf20260429.pdf
Prior minutes: March 17–18, 2026
  → SOURCE: https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcminutes20260318.htm

FFR decision: Hold at 3-1/2 to 3-3/4%
Dissents: Miran (for cut); Hammack, Kashkari, Logan (against easing bias)

NOTE ON METHOD: The primary source is Participants' Views on Current
Conditions and the Economic Outlook and the Committee Policy Actions
sections. Staff review sections are background context only. Three
comparisons are made: (1) vs. the April 29 press conference opening
statement — the richest committee-approved benchmark; (2) vs. March 18
minutes — to track how the distribution of views shifted; (3) vs. the
April 29 statement — to identify what was intentionally omitted.

GROWTH — PARTICIPANTS' VIEWS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Participants:
  "Participants generally observed that economic activity appeared to be
   expanding at a solid pace." AI-related fixed investment described as
   "robust." Consumer spending "resilient." Several participants noted
   energy price strains on lower-income households. Several noted Middle
   East developments raised uncertainty and downside risks.

vs. April 29 press conference opening statement:
  Opening statement: "The U.S. economy has been expanding at a solid
  pace. Consumer spending has been resilient, and business fixed
  investment has continued to expand at a brisk pace."
  → Minutes and opening statement are fully aligned on growth —
    no divergence to flag.

vs. March 18 minutes:
  March: "Participants observed that economic activity appeared to be
  expanding at a solid pace... consumer spending had been resilient."
  → Language essentially unchanged. Growth remains solid and stable
    across both meetings; not a primary concern at either.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LABOR MARKET — PARTICIPANTS' VIEWS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Participants — hard data verdict:
  "Most participants judged that recent data, such as readings on the
   unemployment rate, layoffs, hiring, and labor force growth, suggested
   stabilization in the labor market."
  → Majority view: stable.

Participants — soft data and risks:
  "A few participants pointed to the possibility that the low rates of
   job growth were a sign of labor market fragility."
  "Some participants noted some signs of potential softness...the
   concentration of job growth in only a few sectors, declines in
   recent months in survey measures of job availability."
  "Several participants cited evidence...that firms were likely to
   delay or reduce hiring because of overall economic uncertainty or
   in anticipation of adopting AI technologies."
  "Several participants pointed to the possibility that a fall in labor
   demand could push the unemployment rate sharply higher."
  → Consistent minority: fragility risk, AI-driven reduction possible.

Risk balance:
  "Most participants judged...that risks to the employment side of the
   Committee's dual mandate were tilted to the downside."
  → Most see downside skew despite stable hard data.

vs. April 29 press conference opening statement:
  Opening statement added: "labor demand has clearly softened as well."
  This phrase — a direct demand-side admission — was the key labor
  signal in the opening statement and is NOT found in equivalent form
  in the minutes' participants section. The minutes describe committee
  views on hard data (stable) and risks (downside), but the opening
  statement went further by characterizing current demand as having
  "clearly softened." The opening statement language is Powell's
  committee-approved summary — more pointed than the minutes'
  distribution of views.

vs. March 18 minutes:
  March: "Most participants judged that...the labor market was broadly
  in balance, with the low rate of job growth roughly in line with
  slower labor force growth."
  March risk balance: "vast majority judged that risks to the
  employment side of the mandate were skewed to the downside."

  Changes March → April:
  — The majority still sees stabilization in hard data (unchanged).
  — The downside risk assessment is now "most" vs. "vast majority"
    in March. A very slight dovish softening on the risk framing —
    "most" is a smaller group than "vast majority."
  — The AI-driven hiring delay concern appears in both March and
    April, with similar language. Not a new theme.
  → Net: minimal change on labor. Slightly less extreme downside risk
    framing, slightly more emphasis on stabilization.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INFLATION — PARTICIPANTS' VIEWS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Participants:
  "Participants observed that overall inflation had moved up."
  "Several participants noted that the rate of increase in core goods
   prices remained elevated, at least in part reflecting the effects
   of tariffs."
  "A few of these participants remarked that, while price increases in
   the software category were contributing meaningfully to the increase
   in core inflation, price increases in that category may not be good
   predictors of future overall inflation." — partial look-through for
   software prices specifically.

  Expectations:
  "Most participants noted that measures of longer-term inflation
   expectations remained stable."
  "Several participants highlighted the possibility that, after several
   years of inflation above 2 percent, elevated inflation rates could
   begin to have an increased effect on wage- and price-setting
   decisions." — named fragility risk.

  Inflation timeline:
  "The vast majority of participants noted an increased risk that
   inflation would take longer to return to 2 percent than they had
   previously expected."

vs. April 29 press conference opening statement:
  Opening: "Inflation has moved up recently and is elevated...total PCE
  prices rose 3.5 percent...core PCE prices rose 3.2 percent. This
  relatively high rate largely reflects the effects of tariffs on prices
  in the goods sector."
  → The opening statement's attribution — tariffs driving core goods —
    matches the minutes' participants, though the minutes add the
    software price nuance. The opening statement does not mention the
    software look-through discussion.
  → Key Q&A addition (Powell unscripted): "we're several years above
    2 percent inflation, and that we're already looking through the
    tariff shock" — the stacking logic. This specific reasoning (two
    shocks stacked, making look-through harder) does not appear
    explicitly in the participants' section of the minutes. It appears
    to be Powell's personal framing, or at minimum goes beyond what
    was captured in the collective participants paragraph.

vs. March 18 minutes:
  March: "vast majority noted that progress toward the Committee's
  2 percent objective could be slower than previously expected."
  April: "vast majority noted an increased risk that inflation would
  take longer to return to the 2 percent objective."

  The language is semantically similar but the April framing uses
  "risk" and "take longer" — both signal a lengthening horizon and
  a heightened probability weighting. The direction of change is
  modestly hawkish.

  March also noted: "possibility that after several years of
  above-target inflation, longer-term inflation expectations could
  become more sensitive to energy price increases" — this concern
  appears in both March and April in nearly identical form. Not a new
  escalation in April.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MONETARY POLICY — PARTICIPANTS' VIEWS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
On the hold decision:
  "Almost all participants supported maintaining the current target
   range for the federal funds rate at this meeting."
  → Same result as March (Miran again preferred a cut).

On the rate stance characterization:
  "Participants generally judged that the current policy rate was within
   the range of plausible estimates of its neutral level."
  → This phrase requires careful interpretation: "within the range"
    explicitly accommodates multiple views — some may think policy is
    slightly above neutral (mildly restrictive), others at neutral,
    others perhaps slightly below. It does NOT resolve the question.
    Powell's Q&A gave a clearer personal read: "at the higher end of
    neutral or perhaps mildly restrictive" (at ~756, unscripted).
    The committee-approved framing in the minutes is intentionally
    vague enough to encompass this range.

  IMPORTANT — this language is NOT new in April. The March 18 minutes
  use the identical phrase: "these participants generally viewed the
  policy rate as within a range of plausible estimates of its neutral
  level." This language was already established in March and carried
  forward unchanged.

Easing bias discussion — HIGH SIGNAL:
  "Many participants indicated that they would have preferred removing
   the language from the postmeeting statement that suggested an easing
   bias regarding the likely direction of the Committee's future
   interest rate decisions."

  Powell's Q&A confirmation: "the number of people on the Committee
  who could support that language change changing to a more neutral
  stance...has increased over the intermeeting period." And: "the
  center is moving toward a more neutral place." He also confirmed:
  "conceivably could come as soon as the next meeting" (June 16–17).
  → The minutes + press conference together paint a clear picture:
    easing bias is a single-meeting away from removal.

  Why majority voted for it anyway: "we have so much to learn...
  what happens in the next 30, 60 days could really change the
  picture" (Powell Q&A). The majority's argument was timing, not
  substance — don't rush a symbolic language change under uncertainty.

Cut conditions — named by "several" participants:
  "Several participants highlighted that it would likely be appropriate
   to lower the target range...once there are clear indications that
   disinflation is firmly back on track or if solid signs emerge of
   greater weakness in the labor market."
  → High bar: "firmly back on track" (not just "making progress")
    AND "solid signs" of labor weakness.

Tightening conditions — named by "majority":
  "A majority of participants highlighted that some policy firming would
   likely become appropriate if inflation were to continue to run
   persistently above 2 percent."
  → The majority's stated reaction function: persistent inflation →
    hike. This is the operative framework, not the easing bias language.

vs. April 29 press conference opening statement:
  Opening statement: "We are well positioned to determine the extent and
  timing of additional adjustments to our policy rate based on the
  incoming data, the evolving outlook, and the balance of risks."
  → "Additional adjustments" preserves the easing bias in the approved
    committee language, but the minutes reveal this was a minority-
    preferred formulation on substance, preserved for timing reasons.
  → The opening statement does NOT mention: (a) the majority
    tightening view, (b) the "firmly back on track" cut threshold,
    or (c) the near-miss on easing bias removal. All three are
    significant committee views absent from the statement.
  → Key Q&A addition (Powell): The two explicit pre-cut conditions
    — "the back side of the energy shock AND progress on tariffs
    before we even thought about reducing rates" — are unscripted
    Powell additions not in the committee-approved language. These
    are personal signals, more specific and more hawkish than what
    the minutes capture collectively.

vs. March 18 minutes:
  This is the most important comparison. Key changes:

  A. EASING BIAS OPPOSITION ESCALATED
     March: "Some participants judged that there was a strong case for
     a two-sided description of the Committee's future interest rate
     decisions."
     April: "Many participants...would have preferred removing the
     language...suggested an easing bias."
     → "Some" → "Many": clear escalation. The easing bias is now
       opposed by a larger coalition.

  B. TIGHTENING SUPPORT ESCALATED
     March: "Some participants judged...a strong case for a two-sided
     description...reflecting the possibility that upward adjustments
     could be appropriate."
     April: "A majority of participants highlighted that some policy
     firming would likely become appropriate."
     → "Some" → "Majority": the tightening faction grew from a
       minority to a majority across the two meetings.

  C. CUT SUPPORT DECLINED
     March: "Many participants judged that, in time, it would likely
     become appropriate to lower the target range."
     April: "Several participants highlighted that it would likely be
     appropriate to lower the target range."
     → "Many" → "Several": cut-leaning participants declined.

  D. NEUTRAL RATE LANGUAGE — UNCHANGED
     Both March and April use identical language: "within a range of
     plausible estimates of its neutral level." Not a new signal in April.

  Summary of March → April shift in monetary policy views:
  Tightening faction: "some" → "majority" (significant escalation)
  Easing bias opposition: "some" → "many" (clear escalation)
  Cut support: "many" → "several" (notable decline)
  → The committee's center of gravity moved meaningfully hawkish
    across the two meetings.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net signal:    HAWKISH vs. March minutes; HAWKISH vs. press conference
               opening statement; HAWKISH vs. April 29 statement

The most analytically significant finding from the minutes is the
March → April shift in the distribution of monetary policy views:

  Tightening from minority ("some") to majority
  Cut support from majority ("many") to minority ("several")
  Easing bias opposition from minority to clear majority ("many")

These changes are larger in magnitude than the inflation or labor
language shifts, which were modest or unchanged. The committee's
effective policy bias has moved hawkish between the two meetings,
even though the formal statement language was preserved.

vs. press conference opening statement:
The minutes are more hawkish than the opening statement communicates.
The opening statement's "additional adjustments" language — the easing
bias — is explicitly characterized in the minutes as a minority-
preferred formulation on substance, preserved only for timing reasons.
Powell's unscripted Q&A was more informative than either the minutes or
the opening statement: his two-condition pre-cut framework ("back side
of energy shock AND progress on tariffs") and stacking logic ("already
looking through tariffs, so more caution on energy") go beyond what
appears in the collective committee characterization.

On the neutral rate:
The minutes language ("within the range of plausible estimates") is
vague by design and encompasses views from mildly restrictive to
neutral. This phrase was already in the March minutes; it is not new.
Powell's personal Q&A read — "higher end of neutral or perhaps mildly
restrictive" — is more specific but is an unscripted personal view, not
committee-approved language.

Language watch:
  • "firmly back on track" — the cut threshold for disinflation. Higher
    bar than "making progress." Named by "several," not the majority.
    Watch for Troika adoption — would confirm elevated cut bar.
  • "policy firming would likely become appropriate" — majority
    reaction function to persistent inflation. Not yet in any post-
    FOMC speech. If Warsh uses it, it becomes the new committee anchor.
  • Easing bias removal — Powell explicitly signaled June 16–17 as
    possible. The Paulson May 19 speech (scored separately) already
    validated tightening scenarios without echoing the easing bias,
    consistent with a June easing bias removal.

Analyst flag:  YES
  → The March → April shift from "some" to "majority" on tightening,
    and from "many" to "several" on cuts, is a significant change in
    the distribution of views. Analyst to assess: Is this shift
    primarily driven by the energy shock (a potentially transitory
    factor), or does it reflect a more durable reassessment of the
    inflation persistence risk? This distinction matters for how the
    committee would react to a cease-fire in the Middle East.
  → Also: Powell's unscripted two-condition framework ("back side of
    energy AND progress on tariffs") is more specific than anything in
    the minutes. Does this represent the committee's operative view, or
    just Powell's personal framing? If Warsh's first communication
    echoes or diverges from this framework, it will be the most
    important signal of his early tenure.
