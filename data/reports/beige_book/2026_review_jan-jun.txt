Ad-hoc one-time request: score all 2026 Beige Books released to date.
Released so far: Jan 14, Mar 4, Apr 15, Jun 3 (Jul 15 onward not yet out).
Method (Type H): national summary only — Overall Economic Activity / Labor
Markets / Prices; change vs. the prior report; track the qualitative
intensity words. District detail ignored.
Reference FOMC: April 29, 2026  |  Cycle: HOLDING  |  FFR: 4.25–4.50%
Sources: federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/files/BeigeBook_YYYYMMDD.pdf

NOTE ON THE JANUARY BASELINE: the Jan 14 comparison uses the report's own
"since last report" language (the late-2025 reports characterized activity as
"little change"); the Mar/Apr/Jun diffs are grounded in both reports verbatim.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
1.  JANUARY 14, 2026   (prepared at Richmond; data through Jan 5)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
OVERALL ECONOMIC ACTIVITY
  "increased at a slight to modest pace in eight of the twelve Districts" —
  and explicitly "an improvement over the last three report cycles where a
  majority of Districts reported little change." Holiday-led consumer spending;
  early bifurcation noted (high-income strong; low/moderate "increasingly
  price sensitive"). Outlooks "mildly optimistic."
  Direction: hawkish lean — activity firming out of stagnation.

LABOR MARKETS
  "Employment was mostly unchanged… eight of the twelve Districts reporting no
  changes in hiring." Hiring "mostly to backfill vacancies rather than create
  new positions"; "fewer workers were switching jobs." Wages "moderate," with
  contacts saying wage growth had "returned to 'normal' levels."
  Direction: neutral — flat, low-churn labor; wage normalization.

PRICES
  "Prices grew at a moderate rate across a large majority of Districts."
  Tariff cost pressures "a consistent theme across all Districts"; firms
  increasingly passing them through as pre-tariff inventories deplete.
  Outlook: "some moderation," but "prices to remain elevated."
  Direction: hawkish — broad, moderate, tariff-driven, sticky.

NET: mildly hawkish baseline — firming activity, flat labor, sticky tariff-led prices.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
2.  MARCH 4, 2026   (prepared at Cleveland; data through Feb 23)   vs. Jan 14
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
OVERALL ECONOMIC ACTIVITY
  KEY WORDS: "slight to modest" → "slight to MODERATE" (top end up a notch),
  BUT breadth narrowed: growing Districts 8 → 7, and flat/declining "increased
  from four in the prior period to five." Consumer "dampened by economic
  uncertainty, increased price sensitivity, and lower-income consumers pulling
  back." Manufacturing "improved overall… eight Districts reporting growth."
  Direction: neutral / slight softening — pace top-end firmer, breadth softer.

LABOR MARKETS
  "generally stable… seven of the twelve Districts reported no change" (Jan:
  eight). Flat/lower employment tied to "rising nonlabor input costs, softer
  demand, or uncertainty." Wages "modest or moderate."
  Direction: dovish lean — modest further cooling at the margin.

PRICES
  "increased moderately… eight Districts moderate, four slight or modest."
  Tariffs cited by nine Districts. KEY: "firms expected prices to rise at a
  somewhat slower pace in the near term." — first forward-looking softening.
  Direction: dovish lean — level still moderate, expectations easing.

NET: dovish lean vs. January — softer breadth, first hint of expected price moderation.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
3.  APRIL 15, 2026   (prepared at New York; data through Apr 6)   vs. Mar 4
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
OVERALL ECONOMIC ACTIVITY
  KEY WORDS: "slight to moderate" → back to "slight to MODEST" (top end down),
  "two Districts reported slight to modest declines." NEW SHOCK: "The conflict
  in the Middle East… a major source of uncertainty," firms "adopting a
  wait-and-see posture." Housing "softened"; higher-income spending "resilient."
  Direction: dovish lean on growth — softer pace + a fresh uncertainty shock.

LABOR MARKETS
  "steady to up slightly." Demand "stable, with low turnover, minimal layoffs,
  and hiring mostly for replacement"; "labor availability had improved." AI
  "had enabled many firms to delay or reduce hiring." Wages "modest to moderate."
  Direction: neutral — stable, slightly looser supply.

PRICES
  Headline still "mostly remained moderate," BUT the pivot is in costs:
  "Energy and fuel costs rose SHARPLY in ALL Districts, attributed to the
  Middle East conflict," plus metals-tariff increases (steel, copper,
  aluminum); "input cost increases outpaced selling price growth, compressing
  margins."
  Direction: hawkish developing — the energy shock enters the cost pipeline;
  selling prices lag for now.

NET: mixed — growth dovish, but the Middle East energy shock is the pivotal new
input, loading the price pipeline.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
4.  JUNE 3, 2026   (prepared at Kansas City; data through May 27)   vs. Apr 15
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
OVERALL ECONOMIC ACTIVITY
  KEY WORDS: growth BREADTH broadened — "slight to moderate pace for TEN of the
  twelve Districts" (Apr: eight, with two declines), only one decline / one no
  change. Manufacturing "modest to strong for nine." BUT consumer
  "increasingly bifurcated": middle-income "squeezing more life out of every
  dollar," low-income "greater financial strain"; "weakening consumer spending
  weighed on sentiment"; outlooks "little change in anticipated growth."
  Direction: mixed — firmer breadth (esp. manufacturing) vs. consumer-led caution.

LABOR MARKETS
  KEY: "Employment showed little to no change across eleven Districts."
  Crystallizes into a "low-hire, low-fire environment, with workers
  increasingly reluctant to change jobs." Hiring "selective… critical roles or
  attrition replacement." Wages "modest to moderate… largely in line with
  inflation," with more "cost-of-living increases to manage increasing fuel and
  other household cost pressures."
  Direction: neutral, frozen — note the fuel-driven cost-of-living wage
  adjustments (a mild second-round flag).

PRICES
  KEY WORDS: "moderate" → "MODERATE TO STRONG pace overall, with most Districts
  reporting HIGHER inflation than the previous report." Energy (Middle East)
  "the primary driver," with spillovers "into shipping, packaging, groceries,
  and fertilizer"; non-labor input costs "rising faster than selling prices."
  Direction: hawkish — clear re-acceleration; the energy shock is now broad-based.

NET: hawkish — activity broadened but consumer-cautious; labor frozen; PRICES
re-accelerating on the energy shock. Inflation is the dominant signal entering
the June 16–17 meeting.

2026 TRAJECTORY SYNTHESIS (Jan → Jun)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OVERALL ACTIVITY:  stagnant (late-2025 "little change") → firming (Jan) →
  brief wobble + Middle East uncertainty (Mar–Apr) → broadened breadth but
  consumer-bifurcated (Jun). Resilient on net, but growth increasingly
  concentrated in manufacturing / data centers / defense while the consumer
  splits by income. Not a growth scare; a composition story.

LABOR MARKETS:  flat the entire period, hardening into "low-hire, low-fire" and
  "reluctant to change jobs" by June. A frozen, gradually cooling market — no
  dynamism, but no break. Wages steady modest-to-moderate, increasingly framed
  as cost-of-living catch-up to fuel costs.

PRICES — THE SIGNAL:  moderate + tariff-driven (Jan) → a fleeting "somewhat
  slower pace" hope (Mar) → energy shock arrives in costs (Apr) → escalates to
  "moderate to strong / higher inflation than the previous report" (Jun).
  Two stacked supply shocks — tariffs THEN Middle-East energy — are compounding,
  compressing margins and feeding second-round into groceries, shipping, and
  fertilizer.

POLICY READ (entering Jun 16–17):  The Beige Book corroborates the hawkish
  committee tilt seen in the speeches. It supports the "hold" posture and the
  two stated pre-cut conditions — energy-shock dissipation and tariff progress —
  by showing the opposite of both: an intensifying energy shock and still-
  passing-through tariffs, against a frozen-but-not-breaking labor market and
  resilient (if bifurcating) activity. Net directional read of the 2026 arc:
  HAWKISH, and accelerating into June via the price channel.

  Cross-check vs. the speech side: this maps cleanly onto Waller/Cook/Hammack/
  Logan's explicit-hike framing and the Troika's "sequence of shocks / look-
  through requires more caution" language — the Beige Book is the on-the-ground
  evidence base for that committee hawkishness.

WATCH (next Beige Book, Jul 15, vs. Jun 3):
  • Whether "moderate to strong" prices escalate further or the energy shock
    begins to fade (the key pre-cut condition).
  • Whether "low-hire, low-fire" tips toward actual job cuts (a frozen market
    can thaw downward quickly).
  • Whether consumer bifurcation broadens into an outright spending downturn.
