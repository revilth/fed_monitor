1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The dominant signal across 2026 FOMC communications is a stable, patient hold — but with a meaningful internal fault line. The committee broadly agrees that: (1) tariff effects on inflation are transitory one-time price-level shifts, (2) no second-round inflation effects are detectable, and (3) the labor market is fragile but not broken. Where officials diverge is on whether current rates are still restrictive (Jefferson/Williams: yes, well-positioned) or already near neutral (Logan, Barkin: rates at the "higher end of neutral"). This second view, if it spreads, removes the mechanical justification for near-term cuts even if inflation falls gradually.

The Middle East energy shock (since late Feb 2026) has caused a visible, coordinated shift: all Tier 1 officials retracted or softened any cut signals after the shock began, with Williams explicitly walking back his Mar 3 "further cuts eventually warranted" language by Mar 30. The committee is now in a genuine wait-and-see posture with no clear catalyst for either direction.

Powell's complete absence from economic outlook speeches (both 2026 appearances were about Fed independence, not policy) is itself a signal — likely a deliberate strategy to avoid giving the administration more material for political pressure while Jefferson and Williams carry the policy signaling function.

Only Bowman and Miran remain clearly in the "rates are restricting the economy, cut more" camp.

IMPLICATION: The "Goolsbee is dovish, watch him for cut signals" trading heuristic appears outdated for 2026. His Feb 24 speech suggests he has moved significantly toward the middle of the committee. Whether the Middle East energy shock (which began after this speech) has pushed him further in either direction is unknown — his Apr appearances have no transcripts.

8. PORTFOLIO IMPLICATIONS

Current committee posture: Extended hold with no imminent catalyst for movement. The Middle East energy shock has pushed the next-cut timeline out. The dominant internal debate is not "when to cut" but "are rates even restrictive?" — and that debate argues against cuts.

For rate path:
— Near-term (May-June FOMC): Near-zero probability of any rate move. The energy shock + dual risks framing makes both cuts and hikes implausible until oil markets settle.
— H2 2026 probability of cuts: Highly conditional on (1) Middle East ceasefire, (2) energy prices retreating toward pre-conflict levels, (3) core inflation declining toward 2.5% or below, AND (4) no change in inflation expectations. Williams' explicit 2027 timeline for reaching 2% implies no cuts until late 2026 at the earliest in the base case.
— Hawkish tail risk: If energy prices remain elevated through Q2 and "sequence of shocks" language spreads from Waller/Barkin to voters (especially Cook/Barr), the committee could discuss whether the hold needs to extend indefinitely — or whether a hike is on the table if expectations begin to drift. This is not the base case but the risk that most distinguishes the current situation from a simple "inflation transitory, cuts coming" narrative.
