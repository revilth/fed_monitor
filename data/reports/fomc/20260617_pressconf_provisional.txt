LABOR MARKET
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prior (Apr 29 Powell opening): "While job gains have remained low, the
  unemployment rate has been little changed in recent months."
June 17 Warsh opening: "Job gains have kept pace with the workforce, and
  the unemployment rate has changed little."

"Remained low" → "kept pace with the workforce" is GENUINELY NEW language,
absent from Powell's April 29 opening. This reframe is the most consequential
labor change: it recasts soft payrolls as ADEQUATE given slower labor-supply

directly inverts Powell's Apr 29 Q&A admission that "labor demand has clearly
softened" — the new Chair's first statement declines to endorse that framing.

Direction: HAWKISH — removes the labor-market-softening rationale for easing.

(4) productivity and jobs, (5) causes of inflation. Each with the charge to

Consider alternatives. And ultimately propose next steps for policymaker
consideration." Timeline: most to conclude by fall/year-end.

Direction: HAWKISH (structure) + INSTITUTIONAL (scope). Removing forward
guidance eliminates all signaling of a cutting bias. The task-force scope

itself may be restructured or the format revised before the next meeting.

Q&A — CHAIR'S PERSONAL SIGNALS (unscripted; Warsh's personal baseline)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This is Warsh's first press conference as Chair. Per CLAUDE.md instructions,
the Q&A establishes his personal baseline — not a shift from a prior Warsh Q&A.
Compare to what his pre-confirmation speeches implied; flag divergences.

KEY Q&A SIGNALS (4 bullets where Q&A went beyond or diverged from the opening):

1. ON THE 2% TARGET — more specific than the statement:
   Q&A: "That is the Federal Reserve's long-held objective of 2%. The 'two' is
   the left of the decimal point. For now, 'zero' is to the right."
   The statement says "2 percent goal." The Q&A adds explicit zero-tolerance
   framing — no rounding, no 2.5% de facto ceiling, no symmetric AIT tolerance.
   This goes materially beyond the opening statement's "deliver price stability."
   SIGNAL: Warsh's personal bar for "done" on inflation is exactly 2.0%, not
   "near 2%" or "approaching 2%." The zero-tolerance framing is harder than any
   Powell Q&A in 2025–2026.

2. ON THE DOT PLOT / SEP — signals imminent reform:
   Q&A: "I, however, have refrained from offering any projections of my own,
   consistent with my long-held views on the SEP, at least as currently
   structured." / "I did not submit a dot for me. It's not helpful in the
   conduct of policy."
   The opening statement makes no reference to the SEP or Warsh's abstention.
   The Q&A reveals: (a) Warsh is the missing 19th → 18th dot, confirmed;
   (b) "at least as currently structured" is the key phrase — signals the SEP
   format is under active review, consistent with the communications task force;
   (c) the Chair explicitly characterizing a core communication tool as "not
   helpful in the conduct of policy" is a strong signal it will be reformed or
   eliminated, likely by the September meeting.

3. ON THE HIKE SIGNAL IN THE DOTS — quantifies what the opening only implied:
   Q&A: "Despite the recent pullback in oil, half of the members of the FOMC
   expect rate hikes as soon as this year, reflecting strong labor market and
   inflation data."
   The opening statement carries no tightening bias — it is neutral in language.
   The Q&A quantifies the latent hike risk explicitly ("half," "as soon as this
   year") and attributes it to BOTH the labor market AND inflation, despite the
   SEP's actual unemployment projection going DOWN. This suggests Warsh reads
   the labor market as no longer a dovish offset — consistent with the "kept
   pace with the workforce" reframe in the statement.

4. ON THE FIVE-YEAR COMMUNICATIONS FAILURE — frank admission beyond the statement:
   Q&A: "The commitment to deliver is strong, unanimous, and unambiguous, and
   that's I think an important message we've missed for five years, and we're
   going to fix that."
   The opening statement pledges to "deliver price stability." The Q&A adds
   the candid judgment that the Fed has been sending the WRONG message for five
   years — a direct (if implicit) critique of Powell-era forward guidance and
   the 2020 AIT framework that tolerated above-2% inflation. This is Warsh's
   personal signal, not committee consensus language.

5. ON FORWARD GUIDANCE PHILOSOPHY — more definitive than "not well suited":
   Q&A: Forward guidance dropped as it was "not well suited to the current
   policy conjuncture." The phrase "current policy conjuncture" is narrow —
   it applies to now, not necessarily permanently. But read with the task forces
   and the SEP abstention, Warsh's personal signal is that he views forward
   guidance as structurally problematic, not merely temporarily mismatched.
   [Inference: the conjuncture framing may be diplomatic; Warsh's long-held
   academic critique of forward guidance predates this conjuncture.]
