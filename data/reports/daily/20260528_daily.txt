1.  LISA D. COOK  |  Governor  |  Just Voter
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  Cook treats growth last and briefly — a structural positive with no
  policy implications. "GDP growth has remained robust" and "labor
  productivity growth has exceeded its pre-pandemic average." AI is
  expected to "further boost productivity growth" in the near to medium
  term. No compositional concerns or demand risks flagged.
  Direction: neutral

LABOR MARKET
  Cook characterizes the labor market as "largely stable" with the 4.3%
  unemployment rate "in line with estimates of the natural rate." Supply
  and demand of labor are "roughly balanced." She flags elevated downside
  risks — Middle East conflict uncertainty, low-hire environment, and
  AI-driven structural displacement ("most significant reorganization of
  work in generations") — but none of these is a current condition; all
  are forward-looking risks. Notably, she does not repeat the distributional
  deterioration flags (part-time workers, youth/Black unemployment) from
  her Feb 4 speech — suggesting current labor is more benign on balance.
  Direction: neutral (stable present, elevated downside risks watch-listed)

INFLATION
  Cook opens her economic outlook with inflation — an unusual ordering that
  itself signals priority. "Inflation is clearly moving in the wrong
  direction." PCE at 3.8% (12 months ending April); core PCE at 3.3%,
  "highest since 2023." She identifies three concurrent upward pressures:
  (1) tariffs — expected one-time, "should begin to abate soon"; (2) energy
  from the Iran conflict — uncertain path; (3) AI capital expenditure — "$1.5
  trillion in data-center plans" driving up chip, construction wage, electricity
  and water prices. The AI investment demand vector is analytically novel —
  Cook's first explicit treatment of AI as an inflation contributor rather
  than just a productivity booster. She closes with the credibility/
  entrenchment frame: "After five years of above-target inflation, I am
  particularly attuned to the risk that elevated inflation will become
  embedded in price- and wage-setting behavior."
  Direction: hawkish

MONETARY POLICY
  Cook holds. "I currently believe that the right course of action is to
  hold rates steady." But the hold is asymmetric: "The risks remain tilted
  toward higher inflation." She then pre-commits to both sides: baseline
  case → disinflation resumes without a hike; adverse case → "I am prepared
  to raise rates, if the expected disinflation does not appear in a timely
  manner." The hike signal is conditional and descriptive (not a named
  scenario with a specific trigger), but she pre-commits to a policy
  response — making it stronger than mere risk language. A subordinate
  cut signal is also offered ("would be prepared to adjust my policy stance
  downward should the labor market deteriorate"), but it comes second and
  is clearly subsidiary.
  Direction: hawkish — explicit conditional hike signal

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH — inflation deterioration + explicit hike scenario

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Logan's IMES panel remarks cover oil market supply, Treasury market
resilience, and global demographics. No policy stance expressed, no
current conditions assessment. However, her oil supply analysis is
directly relevant to the "energy shock dissipation" pre-cut condition
(Powell Apr 29 Q&A). Key data: Strait of Hormuz closure has reduced
global oil supply "by nearly 13 million barrels per day, or more than
10 percent." Inventory drawdowns have filled much of the shortfall but
"inventories are finite." U.S. production response is limited: Dallas
Fed energy survey expects output to rise by at most 250,000 bpd by
end-2026 and 500,000 bpd in 2027 — constrained by capital discipline,
labor, and Permian Basin pipeline capacity. Logan's conclusion: "One
way or another, I expect energy markets to come into rough balance
before too long. If the molecules aren't available, the world can't
consume them." This is a supply-constraint framing for the energy shock:
adjustment comes through demand curtailment, not supply restoration.
[Inference: Logan's data suggests the energy shock timeline is
measured in months, not weeks — which delays the first pre-cut
condition from Powell's list. No explicit policy implication stated.]

Note: Logan's most recent scored economic outlook speech is May 10, 2026
(hawkish — "inflation still too high," Apr 29 dissenter on easing bias).
This IMES speech does not change or update that prior assessment.
