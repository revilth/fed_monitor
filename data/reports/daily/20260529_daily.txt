1.  PHILIP N. JEFFERSON  |  Vice Chair  |  Troika
    "Global Economic Developments and the U.S. Economy"
    BoJ-IMES Conference, Tokyo, Japan  (May 27, 2026)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[First post-April-29 economic outlook speech from Jefferson.
 Williams (May 4) was the only prior post-FOMC Troika Type A speech.
 Warsh has not spoken since confirmation. This is therefore the most
 significant data point from the Troika since the May 4 Williams speech.]

GROWTH
  Jefferson echoes the FOMC statement characterization directly ("recent
  economic growth in the U.S. has been solid"), then adds a deceleration
  expectation: he "expects a more modest pace of growth this year as
  households face high energy costs." He flags that he is "watching
  whether higher energy prices will start to weigh on consumer spending."
  This is a new addition vs. his April 7 speech, which carried no explicit
  growth deceleration view. Growth is a secondary concern behind inflation,
  but the energy-demand channel is now actively monitored at the Troika level.
  Direction: neutral (solid but moderating; energy drag flagged)

LABOR MARKET
  Jefferson characterizes the labor market as "broadly stable, with both
  hiring and firing at relatively low levels." This is slightly more
  constructive phrasing than his Apr 7 "roughly in balance" — "broadly
  stable" implies wider confirmation rather than a knife-edge balance.
  He maintains downside risks: "I see risks to the labor market as
  somewhat skewed to the downside." This mirrors his Apr 7 language
  ("susceptible to adverse shocks," downside risks flagged). No change
  in fundamental assessment.
  Direction: neutral — stable with maintained downside tail; unchanged
  from prior speech.

INFLATION
  Jefferson upgrades his inflation description materially: "In recent
  months, inflation moved notably higher because of higher energy costs."
  This is a confirmed-deterioration framing vs. his April 7 language,
  which described only prospective "upward pressure" from energy costs.
  He names two concurrent shocks: tariffs (stalling disinflation for
  "the preceding year") and energy (driving inflation "notably higher"
  in recent months). His forward view: "I expect inflation to decline
  later this year as the effects of tariffs and the energy shock wane" —
  a two-shock conditional for resumed disinflation, directly mirroring
  the framework Powell articulated in the April 29 press conference Q&A.
  Upside risk language carried forward: "I view risks around my inflation
  outlook as tilted to the upside." New emphasis: "I am firmly committed
  to returning inflation to the FOMC's 2 percent target" — placed as the
  opening sentence of his policy paragraph, a positioning that signals
  priority.
  Direction: hawkish — "moved notably higher" is a meaningful upgrade
  from prior speeches; two-shock conditional is more restrictive than
  prior tariff-only framing.

MONETARY POLICY
  Jefferson echoes the committee holding formula: "this policy stance
  leaves us well positioned to respond to economic developments based on
  the incoming data, the evolving outlook, and the balance of risks."
  New addition: "I have not prejudged the next meeting and look forward
  to engaging with my colleagues about the policy necessary to best
  achieve our dual-mandate goals." The "not prejudged" formulation is
  more deliberately open-ended than his prior speeches' confident "well
  positioned" language alone. It does NOT echo Waller's and Cook's
  explicit hike signals ("prepared to raise rates") — Jefferson leaves
  both directions formally open without endorsing tightening. No cut
  signal. No hike signal. Explicit inflation commitment new to this speech.
  Note: Jefferson cites the FFR as "3-1/2 to 3-3/4 percent" — this
  conflicts with the established 4.25–4.50% rate; flagged as a data
  quality issue pending analyst verification against official transcript.
  Direction: neutral with slight hawkish lean — "not prejudged" is
  structurally more open than prior hold-formula language; inflation
  commitment emphasis is new.

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH LEAN — first Troika speech since Williams May 4
  confirms inflation has deteriorated ("moved notably higher"), adopts
  the two-shock conditional structure from Powell Apr 29, and opens
  policy optionality with "not prejudged." Jefferson stops short of
  Waller/Cook's explicit hike signals but does not contradict them.

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (Apr 7, 2026):
  HAWKISH LEAN — inflation moved from "upward pressure" to "moved
  notably higher"; growth deceleration expectation added; two-shock
  conditional introduced; "not prejudged" replaces unqualified hold formula.

vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (Apr 7, 2026 = same):
  HAWKISH LEAN — same as above; Apr 7 is both prior and pre-FOMC baseline.

vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:
  Jefferson sits between the explicit hike signalers (Waller, Cook) and
  the patient-hold Paulson median-voter position. He is hawkish lean but
  has NOT joined the hike camp. His "not prejudged" language is softer
  than Waller's "prepared to raise rates" and Cook's pre-commitment to
  hike "if expected disinflation does not appear in a timely manner."
  With Williams' May 4 neutral and Jefferson's May 27 hawkish lean,
  the Troika has now signaled through two speeches — both lean hawkish
  relative to prior stances, but neither endorses tightening explicitly.
  Warsh remains the unknown variable: no post-confirmation public speech.

WILLIAMS (May 28) — RESEARCH CONTEXT NOTE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Williams' Reykjavík speech on "Productivity Growth and the Challenge
of Real-Time Policymaking" is an academic research speech — no current
economic assessment, no labor/inflation/rate language. It addresses
the historical difficulty of identifying productivity trend shifts in
real time (1970s slowdown, 1990s boom). The analytical implication is
a "robust policy" argument for gradual adjustment to structural changes
rather than sharp reactions to data that might be noise. This is
consistent with Williams' existing patient-hold framing but is not
an update to his current policy stance. Prior scored speech (May 4,
2026 — "There Is No Try") remains operative.

Note of potential indirect relevance: The AI productivity theme is
present in Williams' speech ("AI and its potential to spur a
productivity boom"). His historical analysis suggests that even if
AI drives a real productivity acceleration, the policy-relevant
variable is the recognition lag — central banks should adjust
gradually. This is dovish on margin in an AI-boom scenario but
Jefferson's May 27 speech and the current cycle context are not
AI-productivity dominated.
