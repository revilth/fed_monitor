1.  SUSAN M. COLLINS  |  Boston Fed President  |  Non Voter
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  Real GDP grew 2% in Q1 2026 (annualized), consistent with last year's pace.
  Collins describes growth as "resilient" and holds a modal outlook of
  "continued solid economic growth over the remainder of this year" — but
  explicitly conditioned on Middle East conflict resolving relatively soon.
  Structural demand supports (high firm cash holdings, household net worth)
  and productivity-driven investment are cited as buffers against the energy
  shock. However, productivity is also a two-sided story: demand effects
  sustain activity but also sustain inflation, as firms capture efficiency
  gains as margin rather than passing them through as lower prices.
  Direction: neutral — solid modal, downside risk conditioned on conflict duration.

LABOR MARKET
  Collins notes the unemployment rate is 4.3% through April, "relatively
  stable since mid-2025," and echoes the committee-wide "low hire, low fire"
  read. Her personal language goes further than the April 29 statement:
  "The labor market has clearly softened from the overheated conditions of
  2023 and 2024." NEW compositional concern: unemployment has "increased
  notably for college graduates," especially those aged 25–34, with longer
  unemployment spells — an atypical pattern she attributes to "a shift in
  employers' desired skill mix" from AI adoption. She flags this as a risk
  that "could become more widespread" but characterizes the current
  mismatch as "manageable." Labor not currently pressing for policy action.
  Direction: neutral lean dovish — headline stable, but compositional
  concern is new and forward-looking.

INFLATION
  Core PCE has been above 2% for over five years with "no progress since
  2024" — Collins' own language, the starkest characterization of the
  disinflation stall in the current Non Voter corpus. Services inflation
  excluding housing "remains above levels consistent with 2 percent
  inflation." Her structural explanation: productivity gains in services
  are being captured as firm profit margin given resilient demand, not
  passed to consumers — this is a demand-pricing-power argument for
  services stickiness, harder to address via rate policy. Tariff pass-through
  is approximately 70% complete (staff estimate); remaining effects should
  wane, but near-term upside risk if tariff rates revert to pre-Supreme
  Court levels. Short-term inflation expectations are elevated; longer-term
  expectations still anchored, but the correlation between near- and
  longer-term expectations "has recently increased somewhat."
  Direction: hawkish lean — core stall, services structural stickiness,
  patience-for-look-through explicitly exhausted.

MONETARY POLICY
  "It will likely be necessary to maintain the current, slightly restrictive
  monetary policy stance for some time." Hold is the modal. "More than five
  years of above-target inflation has reduced my patience for 'looking
  through' another supply shock." HEADLINE SIGNAL: "I can also envision a
  scenario that requires some policy tightening to ensure that inflation
  returns durably to 2 percent." This is the first explicit rate hike scenario
  from a Non Voter in the 2026 post-FOMC corpus. She frames it as non-modal
  ("while I hope policy normalization can resume late this year") and restores
  standard optionality language at the close ("well positioned to adjust as
  needed"). Cut signal is absent. Hike tail risk is now on the record.
  Direction: hawkish lean — first hike scenario from a Non Voter; hold
  framing upgraded from "no urgency" to "necessary for some time."

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH LEAN — material shift from March 6 baseline.
  Three concrete movements vs. baseline: (1) disinflation timeline
  downgraded from "second half of year" to "little to no reduction this
  year"; (2) explicit hike scenario added; (3) "reduced patience for
  looking through" adopted (third official after Waller Apr 17, Powell
  Apr 29). Services stickiness is her headline analytical concern.

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (March 6, 2026):         HAWKISH — Mar 6 said "no urgency"
  and "second half of year" for disinflation. May 13 says "necessary for
  some time" and "little to no reduction this year" plus explicit hike scenario.
vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (March 6, 2026):      Same — Mar 6 is the pre-FOMC baseline.
vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:                 Collins sits slightly to the hawkish
  side of the Non Voter group (more hawkish than Goolsbee's conditional cut
  signal, broadly aligned with Daly's May 8 "well positioned" hold framing).
  Her explicit hike mention is the most hawkish statement from any Non Voter
  since the April 29 FOMC meeting.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "Reduced my patience for 'looking through' another supply shock" —
    third adoption (Waller Apr 17, Powell Apr 29, Collins May 13).
    Language cycle stage: EMERGING SHARED — not yet in a statement but
    appearing across Troika + a Just Voter (Waller) + a Non Voter.
  • "No progress since 2024" on core inflation — explicit stall framing.
    If this migrates to a voter (Paulson or Jefferson), it signals committee
    has given up on near-term disinflation and will hold longer.
  • "Envision a scenario that requires some policy tightening" — tail risk
    hike language from Non Voter. Watch for Just Voter adoption; if Paulson
    or Hammack use this language, it would be a material signal.
  • "Firms retain pricing power and have less incentive to fully pass
    efficiency gains on to consumers" — structural services stickiness
    explanation. Novel in the 2026 corpus; could reframe committee
    discussion of why services inflation is not responding to productivity.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → Explicit tightening scenario from Collins warrants close attention.
    Questions for analyst: (a) Is the 70% tariff-pass-through estimate
    robust, and does it adequately explain the remaining services stickiness?
    (b) Collins' "slightly restrictive" vs. prior "mildly restrictive" — is
    this a meaningful semantic shift or stylistic variation? (c) Is the
    college-graduate unemployment pattern a leading indicator of broader
    AI-driven labor market disruption, or limited to a specific sector?

CROSS-SPEAKER NOTE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Collins (May 13) and Goolsbee (May 12, Rockford) both note services
inflation as the critical non-attributable inflation component — Goolsbee
called it "the part I'm nervous about," Collins describes it as structural
given firms' pricing power. Both officials reaching the same conclusion
from different directions (Goolsbee from real-time CPI reaction, Collins
from productivity-margin analysis) suggests this is the committee's emerging
consensus view on why inflation is not responding as expected. Neither,
however, translates the services concern into the same policy conclusion:
Goolsbee maintains a conditional cut signal; Collins names a hike scenario.
The gap between their policy conclusions from the same analytical observation
reflects their different institutional roles (voter vs. non-voter) and
personal inflation bars.
