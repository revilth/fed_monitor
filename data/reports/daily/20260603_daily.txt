1.  BETH M. HAMMACK  |  Cleveland Fed President  |  Just Voter
    "It Takes Two to Make an Economy Go Right"
    City Club of Cleveland, June 2, 2026
    CLASSIFICATION: Type A — Full Economic Outlook speech
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  Consumer spending rising at a moderate pace; AI business investment
  strong; Cleveland Fed SORCE indices show strengthening conditions.
  HAWKISH ADDITION: Hammack argues financial conditions are supportive
  of growth "rather than holding it back," and that "policy may not
  be restrictive" — the real FFR has declined as inflation rose over
  the last few months. This is an argument that the current nominal
  rate is functionally insufficient in real terms.
  Direction: neutral-hawkish lean (financial conditions framing)

LABOR MARKET
  "The unemployment rate was 4.3 percent in April, similar to where
  it was last summer. This is right around my definition of full
  employment in the long term, when labor demand and labor supply
  are in balance." Claims and mass layoff notices remain low; no
  forward-looking deterioration signal. Hammack's characterization
  diverges from the Apr 29 statement's "job gains have remained low"
  language — she simply omits job growth weakness from her framing.
  Direction: hawkish lean (full employment framing removes dovish leg)

INFLATION
  "Inflation is too high and is moving higher." Headline PCE: 3.8%
  in April; core PCE: 3.3%, "well above levels from six months or
  a year ago" — the trajectory is adverse, not merely elevated.
  Broad-based price pressures across goods and nonhousing services;
  services ex-housing "has been stuck at an elevated rate and has
  shown little improvement over the last two years" — a structural
  argument against the look-through case. Inflation expectations
  risk flagged explicitly: "sixth year of elevated inflation" may
  embed expectations; "decisive action" warranted if expectations
  de-anchor.
  Direction: hawkish (acceleration framing; duration argument;
  structural persistence in services; expectations urgency)

MONETARY POLICY
  Base case: hold. Hammack supports keeping rates steady given
  uncertainty. But the forward signal is hawkish: "if recent data
  trends continue, it may soon be appropriate for policy to act to
  address the growing risks of persistently elevated inflation."
  Explicit diagnosis: "monetary policy may not be sufficiently
  restrictive to bring inflation down to 2 percent in a timely
  fashion." Urgency logic: "if we wait for definitive evidence that
  high inflation has become embedded, it may require larger policy
  adjustments, at greater cost" — invokes Volcker episode explicitly.
  Scenario framing: EXPLICIT AND NAMED. Scenario 1 (hold): oil shock
  fades, balance labor/inflation risks. Scenario 2 (hike): inflation
  persists if energy costs stay elevated, broad-based pressures
  continue → "more restrictive monetary policy could well be needed."
  Cut signal status: ABSENT. Easing bias explicitly opposed (Apr 29
  dissent). Hike signal: PRESENT — conditional but named.
  Direction: hawkish (first explicit conditional hike signal from
  Hammack; "may soon be appropriate to act")

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH — significant escalation from May 1 dissent
  statement. Hammack has moved from "remove the easing bias" to
  "may soon need to raise rates." The temporal dimension ("may soon")
  is new; the diagnosis that policy "may not be sufficiently
  restrictive" is the clearest articulation of the hike case from
  any voter since the April 29 FOMC.

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (May 1, dissent statement):   HAWKISH — May 1
  supported the hold explicitly with no forward rate signal. June 2
  introduces conditional hike language and argues real rates have
  fallen. Escalation in inflation concern (acceleration vs. broad-
  based); financial conditions framing added.

vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (Mar 6, embedded outlook): HAWKISH — Mar 6
  characterized rates as "around neutral" with "two-sided risks to
  rates" (symmetric). June 2: rates "may not be restrictive," real
  rate declining, hike signal present. The symmetric risk framing
  from March has resolved decisively hawkish in response to the
  energy shock + continued broad inflation.

vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC: Hammack is the third voter in 11 days to
  deploy explicit hike-ready language (Waller May 22: "I am prepared
  to raise rates"; Cook May 27: similar; Hammack June 2: "may soon
  be appropriate to act"). She occupies the hawkish flank alongside
  Logan (May 10) and Kashkari (Apr 29 dissent record; no post-FOMC
  transcript recovered). Bowman remains the dovish outlier. The
  committee median (Paulson, May 19: inflation-first bar) has not
  updated since mid-May.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "may soon be appropriate for policy to act" — FIRST from Hammack;
    temporal hike signal. If Logan echoes "may soon" before Jun 6,
    the hike coalition has explicit pre-FOMC coordination signal.
  • "monetary policy may not be sufficiently restrictive" — the
    cleanest hike-logic argument from any voter: not just that rates
    could go higher, but that current rates are insufficient. Watch
    for Waller adoption of this specific diagnostic.
  • "inflation is too high and is moving higher" — acceleration
    framing, not yet in the FOMC statement (statement uses "elevated
    in part reflecting energy prices"). If Warsh echoes "moving
    higher" in any pre-blackout remarks, the June 18 statement is
    likely to drop the energy-attribution framing.
  • "decisive action" — first use in 2026 by any voter; references
    expectations de-anchoring risk. Volcker framing intentional.
  • "sixth year of elevated inflation" — duration phrase, consistent
    with Barkin/Logan prior usage. Hammack adopts it explicitly here.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → HIKE COALITION NOW AT THREE VOTERS: Waller (May 22), Cook
    (May 27), and Hammack (Jun 2) have all used explicit hike-ready
    language in an 11-day window. If Logan and Kashkari (both with
    no recoverable post-FOMC Type A speech) hold similar views,
    the hike camp may have 5 voters. The June 18 FOMC is 15 days
    away; the blackout begins June 6. Three questions for the analyst:
    (1) Does Warsh's silence before blackout indicate alignment with
        the hike-ready majority or deliberate ambiguity?
    (2) Is the June 18 outcome a HOLD + removal of easing bias, or
        an actual RATE HIKE?
    (3) Does Hammack's "real FFR has declined" calculation hold when
        cross-checked against current PCE data (3.8% headline) vs.
        nominal FFR (4.25%)? Real rate is ~0.45% — near or below
        neutral by most estimates. Analyst should verify.
