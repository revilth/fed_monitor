LABOR MARKET
  Waller recalibrates the labor break-even downward: lower immigration
  means the economy can sustain job growth well below historical norms
  without the labor market weakening. "I am going to have to get used to
  payroll numbers that are lower than I am accustomed to seeing in a
  growing economy." This rejects the dovish narrative that low payrolls
  signal recession risk. He also flags the consumer confidence downside
  — record-low Michigan sentiment, demand destruction risk — but treats
  it as a scenario input, not a current signal.
  Direction: neutral — recalibrates break-even (mildly hawkish) while
  acknowledging demand destruction risk (mildly dovish). Net: unchanged.

INFLATION
  Waller introduces the most important new analytical framework from any
  voter in 2026: "Learning from that experience [2021-22], I will be
  cautious when faced with a sequence of transitory shocks. While
  intellectually it makes sense to look through each shock, with a
  sequence of shocks, policymakers need to be more vigilant." This
  explicitly raises the bar for the committee's reflexive "look through"
  approach — the central pillar of the current holding consensus. He also
  names the stagflation tail risk (same week as Williams) and invokes the
  five-year inflation credibility clock. His dovish anchor: underlying
  inflation ex-tariffs is "running close to 2 percent," so the structural
  disinflation story is intact.
  Direction: hawkish — "sequence of shocks" framework is new and goes
  beyond committee consensus on how to treat stacked transitory shocks.

MONETARY POLICY
  Waller is the only voter in 2026 to present explicit two-scenario
  analysis. BENIGN (energy resolves): "I was more inclined toward cuts to
  support the labor market later this year." ADVERSE (conflict persists):
  "that may mean maintaining the policy rate at the current target range
  if the risks to inflation outweigh those to the labor market." The
  adverse scenario is the most hawkish statement from any voter in 2026:
  an explicit willingness to hold even if labor weakens, if inflation
  risks dominate. Note the full arc: Waller dissented at Jan 28 preferring
  a cut, did not dissent at Mar 18, and now says he would hold through
  labor weakness in adverse conditions. The energy shock drove this
  entire shift.
  Direction: hawkish — goes beyond committee's symmetric dual-risk framing;
  adverse scenario sets inflation above labor in priority.

NET SIGNAL: NEUTRAL-HAWKISH — most hawkish voter speech since the
  energy shock began; "sequence of shocks" framework and explicit
  hold-through-labor-weakness in adverse scenario are new
