Date: April 29, 2026  |  Type: E — FOMC Press Conference
Chair: Jerome H. Powell  [Final press conference as Chair — May 15 end date]
Decision: HOLD  |  FFR: 3.50–3.75% (unchanged)
Dissenters: Miran (preferred cut); Hammack, Kashkari, Logan (opposed
  easing bias language). First 4-dissent meeting since October 1992.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
OPENING STATEMENT [Committee language]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  "Economic activity expanding at a solid pace." Consumer spending
  resilient; business fixed investment "expanding at a brisk pace."
  Housing remains weak. No change from March 18 framing.
  Direction: neutral — unchanged.

LABOR MARKET
  "Job gains have remained low, on average, and the unemployment rate
  has been little changed in recent months." Change vs. Mar 18: "on
  average" added — acknowledges monthly variability. "Though labor demand
  has clearly softened as well" — unchanged from Mar 18 opening statement;
  not a new addition. Unemployment at 4.3% in March.
  Direction: neutral — unchanged characterization; only "on average" is new.

INFLATION
  "Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase in
  global energy prices." Change vs. Mar 18: drops "somewhat" — stronger
  characterization. Total PCE 3.5%, core PCE 3.2% over 12 months ending
  March. Core "largely reflects effects of tariffs on goods." Near-term
  expectations "have risen this year, likely because of the substantial
  rise in oil prices." Most longer-term measures remain at 2%.
  → Three-part attribution: energy for headline, tariffs for core,
    energy for near-term expectations. All three support patience.
  Direction: mixed — drops "somewhat" (hawkish) but attribution
  framework structures the case for look-through (dovish).

MONETARY POLICY — STATEMENT
  Hold maintained. "Conflict in the Middle East has added to uncertainty.
  In the near term, higher energy prices will push up overall inflation."
  "We are well positioned to determine the extent and timing of
  additional adjustments." Closing: Warsh cleared the Senate Banking
  Committee this morning; Powell will stay on as governor.
  Direction: neutral — unchanged formula; but three hawkish dissenters
  on easing bias language signals committee tension.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Q&A [Powell's own voice — key unscripted signals]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

On pre-cut conditions — KEY (Q2):
  Powell named two explicit requirements before cuts can be considered:
  see the "backside" of the energy shock (energy must peak and decline)
  AND "progress on tariffs" (tariff pass-through must dissipate, which
  he expects "in the next two quarters"). He applied the stacking logic
  directly: "We're already looking through the tariff shock, so [looking
  through energy] requires more caution." This is Waller's Apr 17
  "sequence of transitory shocks" framework articulated by the Chair.

On easing bias (Q2 follow-up, Q15) — KEY:
  "The number of people who could support changing to a more neutral
  stance has increased over the intermeeting period... it's a much closer
  thing on the committee than it was in March." "The center is moving
  toward a more neutral place." "Of course we will move to a neutral
  bias before a hiking bias." Powell confirmed the sequencing and
  acknowledged the easing bias is precarious, but said there is "no
  rush" given high uncertainty. "Conceivably could come as soon as the
  next meeting" (Warsh's call).

On whether policy is restrictive (Q6, Q9):
  "Nobody's calling for a hike right now." The three dissenters objected
  to guidance language, not rate level — all agreed with the hold. On
  restrictiveness: "maybe mildly restrictive or neutral" — consistent
  with Mar 18 ("high end of neutral or mildly restrictive"). The range
  is unchanged; the subtle shift is that Mar 18 Powell expressed a
  preference for staying "mildly restrictive" while Apr 29 he presents
  neutral as equally plausible without that preference.

On Fed independence (Q1, Q12, Q13, Q15):
  Staying as governor because "that is really what is driving this" —
  the legal attacks, not the DOJ investigation alone ("widespread concerns
  among colleagues that these things may continue"). Has not been in
  contact with Warsh since a dinner in January. Will not be a "shadow
  chair." On removing Reserve Bank presidents over policy disagreements
  (Waller's Apr 21 speech raised this): "That would be the beginning
  of the end of the Fed's ability to make monetary policy independently.
  If every administration could come in and do that, you're just another
  cabinet agency. Chris [Waller] said the same thing."

On the economy (final Q):
  No evidence yet of gas-price drag on consumer spending — credit card
  data, retail sales still solid. "People are still spending." Expects
  eventual drag if energy prices stay elevated ("logically you will").

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
NET SIGNAL: NEUTRAL-HAWKISH — hawkish on pre-cut conditions
  (stacking logic, named requirements) and easing bias trajectory
  ("closer than March," "could come next meeting"); partially offset
  by "mildly restrictive or neutral" characterization of current policy
  and confirmation that no hike is on the table.

vs. PREVIOUS PRESS CONFERENCE (Mar 18):
  GROWTH:     Unchanged — solid, housing weak.
  LABOR:      NEUTRAL — "labor demand has clearly softened" was already
              in Mar 18 opening statement; unchanged. Only "on average"
              is new — a minor acknowledgment of monthly variability.
  INFLATION:  HAWKISH — drops "somewhat"; explicit PCE data cited;
              energy attribution structured to support patience.
  MONETARY:   HAWKISH AT MARGIN — three hawkish dissenters on easing
              bias (vs. zero in March); "much closer" easing bias vote;
              "next meeting" removal possible.
  NEUTRAL RATE: Both meetings landed in the same range ("high end of
              neutral / mildly restrictive"). Mar 18 was already explicit:
              "high end of neutral or mildly restrictive... borderline
              between restrictive and not." Subtle difference: Mar 18
              Powell expressed a preference for keeping policy "mildly
              restrictive"; Apr 29 he said "maybe mildly restrictive or
              neutral" without that preference. A real but small shift.

vs. CHAIR'S PREVIOUS SPEECH (Mar 30, Harvard):
  March 30: "wait and see," "series of shocks" concern framed
  abstractly. April 29 Q&A: the same "series of shocks" concept has
  become explicit pre-cut conditions ("backside of energy + tariff
  progress"). The concept has hardened from concern to conditionality.
  Also: March 30 used "well anchored beyond the short term" (cautious
  qualifier); April 29 uses standard "longer-term expectations consistent
  with 2%" — slightly less cautious on expectations framing.

vs. COMMITTEE SINCE PREVIOUS FOMC:
  The Waller Apr 17 "sequence of shocks" framework is now the Chair's
  public framing. The three hawkish dissenters represent a real majority
  shift — Powell confirmed the committee center has moved. Consumer
  spending data (no slowdown yet) validates the patience posture.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "Mildly restrictive or neutral" — consistent with Mar 18 range.
    The subtle shift is dropping the Mar 18 preference for staying
    "mildly restrictive." Not a clean directional signal on its own;
    watch whether Jefferson or Williams adopt the "or neutral" framing.
  • "Backside of the energy shock" — pre-cut condition. Track when
    energy prices actually peak; that is the trigger for reopening.
  • "Next two quarters" — tariff pass-through deadline (end Q3 2026).
    Core PCE ex-energy is the test series to watch.
  • "Conceivably could come as soon as the next meeting" — Warsh's
    first statement is now the live event. June FOMC = first test.
  • "Beginning of the end" on removing Reserve Bank presidents —
    bright line named by Powell and Waller jointly.
  • "Unusual and uncomfortable kind of balance" (labor market) —
    softer than "roughly in balance." Track in subsequent speeches.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → Three questions: (1) "Mildly restrictive or neutral" from the Chair
    means the committee's hawkish dissenters are not arguing rates are
    too low — they are arguing the forward guidance is too explicit. Does
    this shift the bar for the next rate action (cut or hold), and how
    does Warsh frame policy restrictiveness in his first communication?
    (2) The easing bias survived by a narrow majority but "the center is
    moving." A single additional bad inflation print before June likely
    tips the balance. Watch core PCE (April data, released ~May 28).
    (3) Powell + Waller alignment on Reserve Bank president removal:
    this was unambiguous. Does the incoming Warsh administration adopt
    or soften this position? The answer has implications for the Fed's
    institutional independence beyond the current legal battles.
