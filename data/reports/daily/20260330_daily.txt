━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  Brief and structural. Powell praises US economic dynamism compared
  to other large economies for a student audience — not a cycle-
  specific signal. No current growth characterization.
  Direction: neutral.

LABOR MARKET
  Powell acknowledges the dual-risk framing in plain language:
  "there's sort of downside risk to the labor market, which suggests
  keep rates low." He notes low job creation and the low-hire
  environment to students — consistent with the committee's "little
  changed" framing. No incremental signal beyond committee consensus.
  Direction: neutral.

INFLATION
  Powell echoes the committee's tariff consensus ("one-time price
  increase," "half to a full percentage point") but adds an important
  new element: an explicit articulation of the "series of supply
  shocks" risk. "You can have a series of these supply shocks and
  that can lead the public to start expecting higher inflation over
  time...so you monitor that very very carefully." He maintains the
  dovish anchor — "inflation expectations do appear to be well
  anchored beyond the short term" — but his qualifier "beyond the
  short term" is slightly more cautious than the standard unqualified
  "well anchored" boilerplate used by other officials.
  Direction: neutral with mild hawkish undertone — "series of shocks"
  monitoring concern stated by the Chair himself.

MONETARY POLICY
  "We feel like our policies are in a good place for us to wait and
  see how that turns out" — plain-English version of "well positioned."
  "We're not really facing it [the hard choice] yet because we don't
  know what the economic effects will be." Explicit patience.
  Direction: neutral — patient hold confirmed in unscripted language.

NET SIGNAL: NEUTRAL — consistent with committee consensus; "series
  of shocks" concern articulated by Chair

  energy shock and dual-risk framing align precisely with Jefferson and

  • "Well anchored beyond the short term" — Powell's qualifier is

2.  WILLIAMS  |  NY Fed President  |  Troika
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  Williams expects GDP growth close to 2.5% for 2026, supported by
  fiscal tailwinds, favorable financial conditions, and AI investment.
  No recession concern. Brief treatment — not the focus.
  Direction: neutral.

LABOR MARKET
  "Promising signs of stabilization." Unemployment at 4.3%, back to
  July 2025 level. Low-hire, low-fire environment noted. Survey-based
  measures of labor demand (Conference Board, NY Fed) have not yet
  confirmed the stabilization — a cautionary signal he will continue
  to monitor. Still more optimistic than Bowman's fragility framing.
  Direction: neutral — stabilization confirmed with soft-data caveat.

INFLATION
  Maintains all prior anchors: tariff effects are one-time, no
  second-round effects, underlying inflation ex-imports moving in
  right direction. Energy shock ADDED as new factor — "will likely
  boost overall inflation in coming months, but these effects should
  partially reverse later this year, assuming oil prices come down."
  2026 inflation forecast revised up from ~2.5% (Mar 3) to ~2.75%.
  Direction: mild hawkish vs. Mar 3 — energy shock added; forecast
  revised up.

MONETARY POLICY
  ⚠ KEY DEVELOPMENT — CUT SIGNAL RETRACTED:
  Mar 3: "Further reductions in the federal funds rate will eventually
    be warranted..." ← explicit cut signal
  Mar 30: [ABSENT] ← deliberately retracted
  "Well positioned to balance the risks" replaces the cut signal.
  The energy shock appears to have driven this withdrawal.
  Direction: hawkish shift vs. Mar 3 — cut signal gone.

NET SIGNAL: NEUTRAL — stabilization on labor; mild hawkish on
  inflation; cut signal deliberately retracted

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (Mar 3):    HAWKISH — cut signal gone; inflation
  forecast revised up; energy shock added as new constraint.
vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (Mar 3): Same — Mar 3 IS the pre-FOMC baseline.
vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:    Williams is now fully aligned with
  Jefferson's dual-risk holding formula. The committee's most forward-
  leaning Troika voice has pulled back to center.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "Further reductions will eventually be warranted" — ABSENT.
    Retraction is the most important Troika language development since
    the FOMC. Track whether it returns post-April FOMC.
  • "Well positioned to balance the risks" — stable formula replacing
    the cut signal. Now consistent across Jefferson and Williams.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → Williams' Mar 3 cut signal was the most forward-leaning Troika
    statement of 2026. Its retraction by Mar 30 is the most important
    single language shift of the year so far. Was this a deliberate
