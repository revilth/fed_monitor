1.  ANNA PAULSON  |  Philadelphia Fed President  |  Just Voter
    Median voter — highest-weight just-voter reading
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  GDP running at ~2% potential; Q1 solid with AI-driven business
  investment as bright spot. Consumer spending decelerated (Q1
  real 1.6% vs 2.1% last year). Gas prices up >50% YTD are
  squeezing households; consumers trading down and using more
  credit. Forward-looking caution is visible in surveys and
  business conversations, but aggregate spending data has not
  broken down — "few signs that consumers are pulling back
  sharply in the aggregate."
  Direction: neutral — resilient but decelerating; no growth
  alarm, but pace of activity is moderating.

LABOR MARKET
  Unemployment 4.3% in April — "remarkably steady and near what
  I consider to be full employment for many months." Hard data
  verdict: stable and consistent with full employment. Soft data:
  surveys show elevated labor pessimism (NY Fed: 44% probability
  of higher unemployment a year ahead, up from 34% in Jan 2025).
  Historical framing: the current 1ppt rise in UR from cycle low
  is the only non-recession instance since 1948 — unusual
  but not yet alarming. AI-driven labor anxiety flagged as a
  monitoring item. Base case: UR stable through year-end.
  Direction: neutral — stable hard data; elevated soft data
  pessimism acknowledged but not adopted as policy-relevant.

INFLATION
  "Inflation is too high" — her opening verdict. Headline PCE
  3.5%, core 3.2% in March (vs. 2.8% core at her March 27
  baseline). April data "point to continuing pressures." Three
  supply-shock drivers: tariffs, Middle East conflict (energy),
  shipping disruptions. She explicitly calls these "classic
  supply shocks" but rejects conventional look-through by
  asking whether amplification forces are present. Her three-
  factor assessment: (1) activity level — not amplifying; (2)
  long-term expectations — "currently in a good place" (note:
  conditional, not unconditional "well anchored"); (3) policy
  stance — "mildly restrictive" providing a buffer. Net: she
  can hold through the shock but is watching expectations closely.
  Direction: hawkish lean — elevated inflation, rising from her
  March baseline; supply shocks not labeled transitory; personal
  caution on expectations ("currently" hedge retained).

MONETARY POLICY
  Hold verdict: "Policy is mildly restrictive and that
  restrictiveness is helping to keep inflation pressures in
  check while the labor market remains stable." [Bottom line]
  Cut condition: "Assuming the labor market remains in balance,
  rate cuts would only become appropriate once we have seen
  sustained progress on inflation." — Dual requirement: labor
  stability maintained AND sustained disinflation trajectory.
  KEY SIGNAL: "I think it is healthy that market participants
  have taken on board scenarios where the funds rate remains
  unchanged for an extended period, as well as scenarios where
  further tightening becomes necessary." — Paulson explicitly
  validates both extended hold AND further tightening. She
  endorses the market's hawkward repricing (3 cuts → hold/
  tighten) as "largely aligning" with her own thinking.
  Direction: hawkish lean — "mildly restrictive" is appropriate;
  cuts require "sustained" disinflation; tightening not ruled out.

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH LEAN — Paulson's first post-FOMC economic
  outlook speech raises the cut bar vs. her March pre-FOMC
  baseline. With core PCE at 3.2% and rising, supply shocks
  she declines to dismiss as transitory, and explicit tightening
  scenario validation, the committee's assessed median voter has
  moved materially in the hawkish direction since March.

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (Mar 27):             HAWKISH — March was
  cautious-hawkish at 2.8% core ("more cautious" bar); May is
  hawkish with explicit tightening validation at 3.2% core.
  Labor framing shifted from "no signs labor contributing to
  inflation" (dovish) to neutral (stable but watching soft data).

vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (Mar 27):          same — HAWKISH shift.
  Pre-FOMC baseline was her most dovish assessment (labor not
  adding inflation pressure). The May speech acknowledges a
  more challenging inflation environment and explicitly declines
  to rule out further tightening.

vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:              Paulson now in line with
  the hawkish dissenters' direction — not their position. Logan
  (May 10), Collins (May 13), and Hammack (May 1) have all
  skewed hawkish post-FOMC. Paulson's tightening validation
  brings the committee's assessed median into hawkish-lean
  territory, which would be a meaningful regime signal if the
  committee's center is moving rather than just the extremes.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "Sustained progress on inflation" — Paulson's cut condition.
    If she replaces "sustained" with "further" or "some," that
    is a dovish signal; if other members adopt "sustained," the
    bar has spread.
  • "Extended hold" / "further tightening" scenario validation
    — first explicit Paulson endorsement of tightening. If
    echoed by other Just Voters, it marks a regime shift.
  • "Currently long run expectations are in a good place"
    — conditional hedge retained from March. Unconditional
    "well anchored" would be dovish signal; softening of the
    "currently" hedge would signal escalating concern.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → The committee's assessed median voter has now explicitly
    validated further tightening scenarios. This is the first
    post-FOMC Type A speech from Paulson. The hawkward move
    in her framing — from cautious hold at 2.8% core in March
    to tightening-scenario validation at 3.2% core in May —
    represents the most important committee-median signal since
    the April 29 FOMC. Key question: does Paulson's "sustained
    progress on inflation" bar imply cuts are impossible for
    the rest of 2026, or does it allow for a fall move if the
    Middle East shock recedes quickly? Her own scenario
    language ("if the conflict is resolved soon") implies the
    latter — a fast resolution could open a path. But in the
    base case, 2026 cuts appear off the table from the median
    voter's perspective.
