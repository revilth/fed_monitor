FOMC MINUTES — April 28–29, 2026  |  Type D
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Methodology: Policymakers' views only (Participants' Views and Committee
Policy Actions sections). Three comparisons: (1) vs. April 29 press
conference opening statement, (2) vs. March 18 minutes, (3) vs. April 29
statement.

GROWTH
  Solid pace, fully aligned with the opening statement. AI-related fixed
  investment robust; consumer spending resilient. No divergence between
  minutes and committee-approved communication.
  Direction: neutral — no change vs. March

LABOR MARKET
  Most participants: hard data suggests stabilization. Downside risks still
  flagged by most, but "most" is a smaller group than March's "vast majority"
  — a slight dovish softening in risk language. The opening statement added
  "labor demand has clearly softened as well," which is sharper than the
  minutes' collective characterization; that phrase reflects Powell's
  committee-approved summary judgment, not a direct participant quote.
  AI-driven hiring delays remain a named risk in both March and April.
  Direction: neutral — marginally less extreme downside risk framing vs. March

INFLATION
  "Vast majority" see increased risk inflation takes longer to return to 2%.
  Similar framing to March but uses "risk" and "take longer" — modestly
  more hawkish. Software prices flagged as contributing to core but possibly
  not predictive of future inflation — partial look-through for tech category.
  Expectations anchor holding; fragility risk is named but not yet elevated.
  Direction: hawkish lean — timeline extended, though language similar to March

MONETARY POLICY — HEADLINE FINDING
  The primary signal is the March → April shift in the distribution of views:

    TIGHTENING support:   "some" (March) → "majority" (April)
    EASING BIAS opposition: "some" (March) → "many" (April)
    CUT support:           "many" (March) → "several" (April)

  These are meaningful distributional shifts across one intermeeting period.
  The committee's effective center of gravity has moved hawkish.

  The easing bias survived not because most members preferred it on substance,
  but because a majority agreed there was no urgency to signal a change when
  so much remained uncertain about the Middle East. Powell Q&A: "conceivably
  could come as soon as the next meeting" (June 16–17).

  Cut threshold named by "several": "clear indications that disinflation is
  firmly back on track" or "solid signs" of labor weakness. High bar.
  Tightening threshold named by majority: "inflation...continue to run
  persistently above 2 percent." This is the operative reaction function.

  Neutral rate language ("within the range of plausible estimates") is
  UNCHANGED from March — not a new signal. Powell's personal Q&A read —
  "higher end of neutral or perhaps mildly restrictive" — is more specific
  but unscripted. The minutes phrase is deliberately vague and encompasses
  views from slightly restrictive to neutral.

  Powell Q&A added two explicit pre-cut conditions not in the minutes:
  "the back side of the energy shock AND progress on tariffs before we
  even thought about reducing rates." This two-condition framework and
  the stacking logic ("already looking through tariffs, more caution
  needed on energy") are unscripted personal signals — informative but
  not committee-approved language.
  Direction: hawkish — tightening is majority view; cuts require high bar

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH
  The minutes confirm what the three hawkish dissenters' vote already
  implied: the committee's substance was more hawkish than the easing-bias
  statement language conveyed. The March → April distributional shift
  (tightening from minority to majority; cuts from majority to minority)
  is the core analytical finding. Easing bias removal at June 16–17 is
  now the baseline expectation.

vs. PRESS CONFERENCE OPENING STATEMENT:
  The opening statement's "additional adjustments" easing bias was a
  minority-preferred formulation on substance, preserved for timing.
  The opening statement omits: majority tightening view, "firmly back
  on track" cut threshold, near-miss on easing bias removal. Powell's
  unscripted Q&A was more informative than either the minutes or the
  statement — particularly the two-condition pre-cut framework.

vs. MARCH 18 MINUTES:
  Tightening faction: "some" → "majority" (significant escalation)
  Cut support: "many" → "several" (notable decline)
  Easing bias opposition: "some" → "many" (clear escalation)
  Neutral rate language: UNCHANGED (same phrase in both minutes)
  Labor risk: marginally softer ("most" vs. "vast majority")
  Inflation: modestly more hawkish timeline framing

vs. APRIL 29 STATEMENT:
  Statement omits majority tightening view and cut threshold language.
  The statement's symmetric framing ("attentive to both sides") understates
  the actual skew toward tightening in the minutes.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "firmly back on track" — the cut threshold. Watch for Troika adoption.
  • "policy firming would likely become appropriate" — majority reaction
    function. Absent from all post-FOMC speeches so far. Warsh's first
    speech will be key.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → The March → April shift in distribution is large for a single
    meeting. Is it primarily energy-driven (potentially transitory) or
    a durable reassessment of inflation persistence? This distinction
    matters: a cease-fire in the Middle East would rapidly change the
    energy inflation picture, potentially snapping the tightening
    faction back toward neutral — unless the "persistently above 2%"
    framing reflects a view about inflation beyond energy.
  → Powell's two-condition pre-cut framework is unscripted. Does it
    represent committee consensus, or his personal framing? Warsh's
    first communication will tell us whether this framework carries.
