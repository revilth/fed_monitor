GROWTH
  Overall growth backdrop is solid: Q4 GDP at 1.4% (annual rate),
  private domestic final purchases at 2.4%, and Waller expects above
  2% for the first half of 2026. Manufacturing surveys show a broad
  January pickup. The key concern is compositional: "I worry that
  the still-solid spending increases lately may be driven by stock-rich
  households and thus be masking weakness in the still substantial
  share of spending that relies on lower- and middle-income people."
  Retailers confirm the divergence — higher-income customers resilient,
  lower/middle income starting to trade down or pull back. Growth looks
  fine in aggregate but the base is narrowing.
  Direction: neutral — solid headline, flagged compositional concern.

LABOR MARKET
  The central question of the speech: is the strong January jobs report
  (+130K total, +172K private) signal or noise? Waller lays out why he
  is skeptical: job gains were concentrated in healthcare and construction
  (~20% of employment); ADP reported only 22K for January; Challenger
  counted 108K layoff announcements — the most since 2009 for a January.
  His baseline remains: "close to zero net job creation over 2025
  indicates a weak, and fragile job market." He rates the two outcomes —
  genuine labor market turn vs. noisy January — as "close to a coin flip."
  Direction: neutral-dovish — fragile baseline, high uncertainty on
  whether January represents a genuine turn.

INFLATION
  Waller's inflation framework is his "look through" doctrine: "Traditional
  central bank wisdom suggests that we should 'look through' tariffs. I did
  this when they went up and will do so if they come down." He estimates
  underlying inflation (ex-tariffs) is "close to the FOMC's 2 percent goal."
  At this date there is no energy shock, no "sequence of transitory shocks"
  framing. Inflation, in his view, is essentially solved at the structural
  level — it is only the transitory tariff effects keeping the headline
  above target.
  Direction: dovish — "look through" doctrine intact; underlying
  inflation near target.

MONETARY POLICY
  Waller presents explicit two-scenario analysis. If February data confirms
  the January turnaround, he would hold. If January proves to be noise and
  February disappoints, a 25bp cut at the March meeting "would be
  appropriate." At this date he is symmetrically uncertain — a "coin flip"
  — with labor as the deciding variable. This is a dissenter who remains
  open to holding if the data improve.
  Direction: neutral-dovish — conditional cut signal present for the
  weak-labor scenario; coin-flip uncertainty overall.
