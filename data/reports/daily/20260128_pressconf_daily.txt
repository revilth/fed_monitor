━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  "The US economy expanded at a solid pace last year and is coming
  into 2026 on a firm footing." "Consumer spending has been resilient
  and business fixed investment has continued to expand." Housing
  remains weak. Government shutdown weighed on Q4 but effects
  reversing. GDP forecast in SEP: median 2.4% for 2026.
  Direction: neutral to positive — solid baseline.

LABOR MARKET
  Statement language: "job gains have remained low, the unemployment
  rate has shown some signs of stabilization." Unemployment 4.4%.
  Private payrolls +29K/month (ex-government). Immigration-driven
  labor force slowdown partially explains low headline job gains.
  "Labor demand has clearly softened as well." "Indicators suggest
  that conditions may be stabilizing after a period of gradual
  softening."
  Direction: neutral — stabilization characterized cautiously.

INFLATION
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." PCE ~2.9%, core PCE 3.0%.
  "These elevated readings largely reflect inflation in the goods
  sector which has been boosted by the effects of tariffs. In
  contrast, disinflation appears to be continuing in the services
  sector." Near-term inflation expectations declined from peaks.
  Direction: neutral — tariff goods inflation offset by services
  disinflation; expectations stable.

MONETARY POLICY — STATEMENT
  Hold at 3.50–3.75%. "Since last September, we've lowered our policy
  rate 75 basis points, bringing it within a range of plausible
  estimates of neutral." Key phrase: "within a range of plausible
  estimates of neutral" — explicit statement that rates are no
  longer clearly restrictive. Forward guidance: "well positioned to
  determine the extent and timing of additional adjustments."
  Direction: neutral — patient hold; neutral rate characterization
  removes urgency to cut.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

On dissents (Miran and Waller both voted to cut):
  "I welcome that at the FOMC...it helps us make better decisions."
  Powell does not distance himself from the dissenters' views; treats
  dissent as healthy deliberation.

On the growth picture:
  "Clear improvement in the outlook for growth...the data have come
  in and sentiment...suggesting that this year starts off on a solid
  footing for growth." More optimistic than his statement language.

On forward policy path:
  "The economy is growing at a solid pace. The unemployment rate has
  been broadly stable and inflation remains somewhat elevated. So,
  we'll be looking to our goal variables and letting the data light
  the way for us." → No timeline or bias toward cuts or hikes.

On the neutral rate / further cuts:
  When pressed on whether the 16-of-19 SEP dots below current rate
  signal further cuts needed: Powell notes "we're well positioned
  to address the risks on both sides." Declines to signal direction.

NET SIGNAL: NEUTRAL — committee holds; two dissenters prefer cuts;
  policy now "within range of neutral"; patient meeting-by-meeting

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "Within a range of plausible estimates of neutral" — the committee
    is officially saying rates are no longer restrictive. This is the
    most important phrase of the January statement. Track whether
    subsequent speeches characterize rates as at neutral (Goolsbee
    Feb 24, Logan Feb 10) or still somewhat restrictive (Bowman).
  • "Job gains have remained low, unemployment has shown some signs
    of stabilization" — the labor market characterization. Compare
    against March 18.
  • Two dissenters (Miran, Waller) — Waller drops his dissent by
    March 18; Miran continues. Track whether Waller's reversal signals
    a shift in his analytical framework.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → The two dissenters (Miran and Waller) make this the most
    internally divided FOMC hold since 2023. Analyst to assess:
    was the Waller dissent a genuine view on labor market risk, or
    a tactical vote? His Feb 23 speech explains it as a "coin flip"
    on whether January data was signal or noise.
