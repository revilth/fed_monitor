1.  CHRISTOPHER J. WALLER  |  Governor  |  Just Voter
    First post-FOMC economic outlook speech; pre-FOMC baseline Apr 17
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  GDP growing at 2% pace (Q1 and estimated Q2). AI investment "torrid"
  with "no signs of slowing." Consumer spending resilient in aggregate
  despite Michigan sentiment at all-time record low. Waller notes income
  bifurcation: tax refunds concentrated in higher-income households
  while lower-income households bear the gas price burden. Targeted
  April softness in autos, furniture, and department stores — "I will
  be watching carefully to see whether this persists." No growth alarm
  sounded; aggregate picture echoes committee boilerplate.
  Direction: neutral — solid aggregate growth; income bifurcation and
  discretionary softness flagged as monitoring items, not verdicts.

LABOR MARKET
  "After worrying about a deterioration in the labor market through
  most of 2025, recent data has indicated to me that labor supply and
  demand have finally come into a rough balance." Unemployment 4.3%
  (stable; near his long-run estimate). 115,000 jobs in April; 3-month
  avg 48,000 — low by historical standard but explicitly reframed as
  consistent with near-zero labor force growth. Hiring ticked up in
  March to highest level in over a year. POLICY IMPLICATION: "I don't
  see the prospect of a weakening labor market as the dominant force
  that should be guiding monetary policy in the months ahead." This
  removes the labor-downside leg from his April 17 conditional framework.
  Direction: hawkish lean — "finally stabilized" replaces the Apr 17
  "alternating positive and negative" framing; labor risk deprioritized
  as a policy driver.

INFLATION
  PCE inflation estimated at ~3.8% YoY (highest in 3 years); core PCE
  ~3.3% YoY (highest in 2.5 years). Verdict: "Inflation is not headed
  in the right direction." April CPI +0.6%: energy +3.8%, but also
  groceries +0.7%, apparel +0.6%, services ex-energy +0.5% — all
  "sizable monthly growth rates." BREADTH SIGNAL: "About half of the
  categories of goods and services prices tracked in consumer inflation
  have been up 3 percent or more this year, a historically large share."
  This is a diffusion concern beyond the energy shock narrative.
  Manufacturing input price index at 84.6 — matching April 2022
  pandemic supply chain crisis peak. Waller explicitly flags that the
  FOMC "has been unable to achieve our stated inflation target for more
  than five years" — joining Non Voters in the duration-framing chorus
  but now at Just Voter weight. Introduces BAYESIAN FRAMEWORK to explain
  why a sequence of individually-transitory shocks can still raise
  longer-term inflation expectations: agents revise the probability of
  future positive shocks upward when observing repeated positive shocks,
  even if they believe each is individually transitory. Longer-term
  inflation expectations "still relatively low and appear well anchored"
  but 1-5 year expectations "have moved up since the beginning of 2026."
  Apr 17's ex-tariff dovish anchor ("underlying inflation close to 2%")
  is ABSENT — data have overtaken it.
  Direction: HAWKISH — strongest inflation assessment from any voter in
  2026; breadth, duration, expectations, and PCE-level concerns combined.

MONETARY POLICY
  Hold: "my current policy position is to hold rates steady for the
  near term." Cut bar: "improvement on inflation or a significant
  deterioration in the labor market" — a materially higher bar than
  Apr 17's BENIGN conditional ("cuts to support the labor market later
  this year"). EASING BIAS REMOVAL: "I would support removing the
  'easing bias' language in our policy statement to make it clear that
  a rate cut is no more likely in the future than a rate increase."
  HIKE RISK: "I can no longer rule out rate hikes further down the road
  if inflation does not abate soon." "If I believe inflation expectations
  start to become unanchored, I would not hesitate to support an increase
  in the target range." Next-move framing: "The next move, whether it is
  a hike or cut, will depend on the data" — symmetric framing is itself
  hawkish given market pricing. Cut signal: ABSENT (Apr 17 benign
  conditional closed). Hike signal: CONDITIONAL on expectations
  unanchoring or persistent inflation. Scenario framing: no explicit
  named scenarios as in Apr 17 — replaced with a single "patient hold"
  track with hike risk building on the upside.
  Direction: HAWKISH — first voter to call for easing bias removal;
  first voter to explicitly name hike risk; benign conditional removed.

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH — Most hawkish Just Voter speech of the 2026
  cycle. Clear escalation from Waller's own Apr 17 baseline, which was
  already the committee's most hawkish economic outlook speech.

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (Apr 17, 2026):       HAWKISH — Material escalation
  on all four topics. Labor: "finally stabilized" vs. "alternating
  positive and negative." Inflation: 3.8% PCE vs. underlying "close
  to 2% ex-tariffs." Policy: no cut signal vs. BENIGN conditional;
  hike risk named; easing bias removal called for.

vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (Apr 17, 2026):   same — Apr 17 IS the pre-FOMC
  baseline (Apr 29 reference FOMC). Full trajectory in one comparison:
  Waller has moved from a conditional dovish scenario to an explicit
  hawkish hold with hike risk. The energy shock data have closed the
  benign exit ramp.

vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:             Waller is now the most hawkish
  signal in the post-FOMC dataset, superseding even Logan's May 10
  "inflation still too high" framing. Logan and Hammack (Apr 29
  dissenters on easing bias) set up the call for removal; Waller is
  now the first post-FOMC voter to explicitly advocate it. The
  committee's post-FOMC output is uniformly hawkish-lean:
  Paulson (May 19), Logan (May 10), Collins (May 13), Barkin (May 21)
  — all hawkish lean. Waller (May 22) is the strongest of the five.
  No dovish signals from any scored post-FOMC speech.

    PRIORITY PHRASE of the post-FOMC cycle. If Paulson, Jefferson,
    or Williams adopt this language before June 17-18, statement change
    is the base case outcome. Language cycle stage: DISPERSED (one voter).
  • "I can no longer rule out rate hikes" — first voter to name hike
    risk explicitly. If Logan or Hammack (Apr 29 hawkish dissenters)
    follow, this becomes a coalition signal rather than an outlier.
  • "Five years above our 2 percent target" — crossed from Non Voter
    (Goolsbee, Collins, Barkin) to Just Voter (Waller) tier in one
    speech. Language cycle stage: EMERGING SHARED, entering voter tier.
  • Bayesian expectations framework — provides intellectual scaffolding
    for why multiple individually-transitory shocks still justify policy
    action. Watch for other officials to cite this reasoning.
