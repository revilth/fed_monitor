━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  Waller flags demand destruction risk from the energy shock via the
  Michigan consumer confidence reading — "its lowest-ever reading" — and
  notes that if confidence deteriorates to the point where consumers begin
  to economize, it could translate into a meaningful pullback in spending.
  This is the descendant of his Feb 23 concern about lower/middle-income
  consumer weakness masking aggregate spending strength. It is framed as
  a scenario risk, not a current signal — spending has not yet collapsed.
  Direction: mild dovish lean — energy-driven demand risk flagged.

LABOR MARKET
  Waller recalibrates the break-even downward: lower immigration means
  the economy can sustain historically low job growth without the labor
  market weakening. "I am going to have to get used to payroll numbers
  that are lower than I am accustomed to seeing in a growing economy."
  This rejects the dovish narrative that low payrolls signal recession
  risk — it is a structural shift, not cyclical weakness. He acknowledges
  this is a somewhat hawkish reframing: lower break-even means the
  committee needs less job creation to declare labor stable.
  Direction: neutral — structural break-even reframing offsets low
  headline payroll numbers; no fragility language.

INFLATION
  Waller introduces the most important new analytical framework from any
  voter in 2026: "Learning from that experience [2021-22], I will be
  cautious when faced with a sequence of transitory shocks. While
  intellectually it makes sense to look through each shock, with a
  sequence of shocks, policymakers need to be more vigilant." This
  explicitly raises the bar for the "look through" approach that was his
  unchallenged baseline as recently as Feb 23. He also invokes the
  five-year credibility clock ("seen inflation above 2 percent now for
  five years") as the reason vigilance is warranted. His dovish anchor
  remains: underlying inflation ex-tariffs is "running close to 2 percent"
  — the structural disinflation story is intact beneath the shocks.
  Direction: hawkish — "sequence of shocks" framework is new and
  qualifies the "look through" doctrine he held as recently as Feb 23.

MONETARY POLICY
  Waller presents explicit, named two-scenario analysis — more deliberate
  than Williams' descriptive risk framing the day before.

  BENIGN SCENARIO (energy shock resolves): "If the Strait of Hormuz
  opens...I was more inclined toward cuts to support the labor market
  later this year when the outlook is more steady." A conditional dovish
  signal for 2026 if geopolitics cooperate.

  ADVERSE SCENARIO (conflict persists): "that may mean maintaining the
  policy rate at the current target range if the risks to inflation
  outweigh those to the labor market." The most hawkish statement from
  any voter in 2026: explicit willingness to hold even if labor weakens,
  if inflation risks dominate. This is asymmetric — inflation beats labor
  in his adverse case — going beyond the committee's symmetric dual-risk
  framing.

  Full arc: dissented Jan 28 preferring cut → did not dissent Mar 18 →
  now states he would hold through labor weakness in the adverse scenario.
  The energy shock and the February jobs disappointment drove this reversal.
  Direction: hawkish — adverse scenario sets inflation above labor in
  priority; the "coin flip" of Feb 23 is gone.

NET SIGNAL: NEUTRAL-HAWKISH — most hawkish voter speech since the
  energy shock began; "sequence of shocks" framework and hold-through-
  labor-weakness in adverse scenario are the key new additions

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (Mar 18 dissent, no economic outlook speech):
  No direct comparable. Next best: Feb 23 "Labor Market Data: Signal
  or Noise?" — see pre-FOMC comparison below.

vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (Feb 23):  DRAMATIC HAWKISH SHIFT across all
  dimensions. On inflation: "look through" tariffs unchallenged (Feb 23)
  → "sequence of transitory shocks / more vigilant" (Apr 17). On monetary
  policy: "coin flip, lean toward cut if labor disappoints" (Feb 23)
  → "hold even if labor weakens" in adverse scenario (Apr 17). On growth:
  Feb 23 consumer weakness was a concern but not yet a scenario risk;
  Apr 17 flags it explicitly as a demand-destruction threshold. Only his
  dovish anchor — underlying inflation close to 2% ex-tariffs — survives
  intact from Feb 23 to Apr 17. The February jobs report (which did not
  confirm January's strength) and the late-February energy shock account
  for the full reversal in 7 weeks.

vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:     Hawkish end of Just Voters. "Sequence of
  transitory shocks" is more vigilant than the Troika's "dual risk, well
  positioned" formula. Cook, Barr, and Paulson share the inflation-first
  lean, but none went as far as "hold even if labor weakens." Only Bowman
  (proactive cuts) and Miran (persistent dissenter) remain clearly pro-cut.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "Sequence of transitory shocks / more vigilant" — most important new
    phrase from any voter in 2026. Also appeared in Barkin (Mar 27) as
    "series of supply shocks." Independently articulated by two officials.
    At the dispersed-to-emerging stage of the language cycle. If Jefferson
    or Williams adopt it, it becomes committee-wide and would represent
    a meaningful hawkish shift in how the committee treats stacked shocks.
  • "Hold even if labor weakens" (adverse scenario) — explicit; no other
    voter has gone this far. Track whether it spreads or remains an outlier.
  • Stagflation risk: Waller explicitly structures this as a named scenario
    with a named policy response. Williams described the same risk one day
    earlier (Apr 16) but in descriptive terms without naming it as a
    scenario — a subtle but meaningful distinction in how deliberate each
    is about the tail risk.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → Three questions for analyst review: (1) Is Waller an outlier or a
    leading indicator? If Jefferson or Williams adopt the "sequence of
    shocks" framing, it signals a committee-wide hawkish shift. (2) Does
    "sequence of transitory shocks" conflict with the Troika's "tariffs are
    one-time" consensus, or can both coexist? (3) If the energy shock
    resolves and Waller's benign scenario materializes, how quickly does
    his conditional cut signal re-emerge — and does it come in time to
    influence the June FOMC?
