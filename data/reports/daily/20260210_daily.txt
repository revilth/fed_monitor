GROWTH
  Economic activity "rebounded strongly since the first half of last
  year." GDP grew 4.4% in Q3 2025. Tracking estimates show solid Q4
  growth even adjusting for the government shutdown. Consumer spending
  and business investment strong. No concern on growth.
  Direction: neutral — robust backdrop.

LABOR MARKET
  Labor market "now appears to be stabilizing, and the downside
  risks appear to have meaningfully dissipated." Monthly job gains
  near the break-even level needed to keep unemployment stable.
  Unemployment ticked down to 4.4% in December. More optimistic
  than Bowman's fragility framing; similar to Jefferson's "roughly
  in balance."
  Direction: neutral — stabilization confirmed.

INFLATION
  "I am not yet fully confident inflation is heading all the way
  back to 2 percent." Sees several reasons progress could be slower
  than expected: tariff pass-through still incomplete; core non-
  housing services inflation moved sideways in 2025; annual price
  resets could surprise in early 2026. Key phrase: "After last year's
  rate cuts, it is uncertain how restrictive policy currently is."
  Model-based neutral rate estimates: real fed funds now sits
  "squarely within the range." Market-based estimates suggest neutral
  "not far from the current policy rate." → "Our current stance of
  policy may be very close to neutral."
  Direction: hawkish — questions whether rates are still restrictive.

MONETARY POLICY
  "Right now, I am more worried about inflation remaining stubbornly
  high." Sets a high bar for further cuts: needs both labor market
  cooling AND inflation progress simultaneously. "If so [inflation
  coming down, labor stable], this would tell me that our current
  policy stance is appropriate and no further rate cuts are needed."
  Direction: hawkish — inflation-first; very close to neutral framing
  removes the mechanical case for further cuts.

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH — most hawkish 2026 regional speech to date;
  "very close to neutral" is the key phrase
