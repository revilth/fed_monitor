CROSS-DISSENTER ANALYSIS — ALL THREE STATEMENTS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

All three dissenters published formal written statements today.
Comparing them reveals independent reasoning — not coordinated
talking points — which makes the shared conclusion more significant.

SHARED CONCLUSION:
  The "additional adjustments" language implies the next move is a
  cut. All three consider this no longer appropriate given the
  current outlook. All three want guidance that signals the next
  move could be either a cut or a hike.

WHERE THEY DIFFER:

LABOR MARKET — three distinct reads:
  Hammack: "near my estimate of full employment" → no slack, no risk
  Logan:   "stable, low unemployment, gains keeping pace with growth" → neutral
  Kashkari: "somewhat above my estimate of full employment" → modest slack

  → Kashkari is the most dovish of the three on labor, yet still
    dissented. This matters: he is not dismissing the labor downside.

INFLATION — two distinct framings:
  Hammack: "broad based" — rejects energy attribution entirely
  Logan:   "meaningfully above 2% even before energy" — pre-existing
             inflation separate from the energy shock
  Kashkari: most analytical — benign scenario has core PCE at 3%
             for three consecutive years; adverse has unanchoring risk

  → Logan and Kashkari both isolate pre-existing inflation from
    the energy shock. This directly undermines the committee
    statement's "in part reflecting energy" framing.

HAWKISHNESS RANKING (most to least):
  1. Kashkari — explicit "series of hikes" in adverse scenario, even
     if labor weakens. Went from one projected SEP cut (March) to
     explicit hike advocate. Furthest hawkish reach of any voter.
  2. Logan — symmetric guidance, hike on the table, forward guidance
     framed as active policy tool.
  3. Hammack — briefest, but "broad based" inflation framing and
     "near full employment" labor are the most hawkish characterizations
     on those two dimensions.

KEY FINDING — NOT COORDINATED:
  Despite sharing a conclusion, the three use different analytical
  frameworks, different inflation characterizations, and different
  labor assessments. This is three independently hawkish officials
  converging on the same policy conclusion — a more durable
  hawkish bloc than if the statements were uniform.
