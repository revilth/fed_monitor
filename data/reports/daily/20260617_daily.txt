GROWTH (HAWKISH-LEAN)
  Prior: "Recent indicators suggest that economic activity has been expanding
          at a solid pace."
  New:   "Economic activity is expanding at a solid pace despite elevated
          uncertainty that owes, in part, to the conflict in the Middle East.
          Productivity growth and capital investment are strong."
  Changes: (1) "Recent indicators suggest…has been expanding" → "is expanding"
               — more direct, present-tense, no hedging.
           (2) ADDED: "despite elevated uncertainty…conflict in the Middle East"
               — names the geopolitical source; not a demand-side growth concern.
           (3) ADDED: "Productivity growth and capital investment are strong."
               — new supply-side resilience sentence; removes growth-slowdown
               rationale for cuts.
  Direction: HAWKISH-LEAN.

LABOR (HAWKISH — most consequential single change)
  Prior: "Job gains have remained low, on average, and the unemployment rate
          has been little changed in recent months."
  New:   "Job gains have kept pace with the workforce, and the unemployment
          rate has changed little."
  Change: "remained low, on average" → "kept pace with the workforce."
  This reframes soft payrolls as ADEQUATE given slower labor-supply growth
  (immigration decline, participation) — the exact opposite of Powell's Apr 29
  Q&A admission "labor demand has clearly softened." The committee is no longer
  flagging labor as a reason to cut.
  Direction: HAWKISH.

INFLATION (STRONGLY HAWKISH — multiple layered additions)
  Prior: "Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase in
          global energy prices."
  New:   "Inflation remains elevated relative to the Committee's 2 percent
          goal, in part reflecting supply shocks that have driven price
          increases in certain sectors, including energy. The Committee will
          deliver price stability."
  Changes: (1) "is elevated" → "remains elevated" — persistence framing.
           (2) ADDED: "relative to the Committee's 2 percent goal" — re-anchors
               the target explicitly.
           (3) "recent increase in global energy prices" → "supply shocks that
               have driven price increases in certain sectors, including energy"
               — the Waller/Barkin "sequence of supply shocks" framing is now
               IN the statement. Language cycle: DISPERSED → EMERGING →
               COMMITTEE-WIDE → IN STATEMENT (cycle completed).
           (4) ADDED: "The Committee will deliver price stability." — declarative
               commitment; unusual formulation for an FOMC statement.
  Direction: STRONGLY HAWKISH.

  New:   "The Committee decided to maintain the target range for the federal

OPENING STATEMENT — COMMITTEE CONSENSUS (approved language)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

GROWTH
  Warsh opened with the June 17 statement text (confirmed verbatim in opening).
  Genuinely new vs. Powell's April 29 opening: explicit Middle East naming
  and the "Productivity growth and capital investment are strong" sentence.
  Powell's Apr 29 opening: "solid pace" + "business fixed investment has
  continued to expand at a brisk pace" + "housing remains weak." Warsh's
  version drops housing weakness and reframes investment as structural strength.
  Direction: HAWKISH-LEAN.

LABOR
  Warsh opening carried "kept pace with the workforce" — fully new vs. Powell
  Apr 29 opening ("job gains have remained low"). Powell's Apr 29 Q&A admission
  "labor demand has clearly softened" is entirely absent from Warsh's opening.
  Direction: HAWKISH — removes the labor-market-softening rationale for easing.

INFLATION
  Four-part inflation block — ALL elements are new vs. Powell's Apr 29 opening:
  (1) "Inflation remains elevated relative to the Committee's 2 percent goal,
      in part reflecting supply shocks that have driven price increases in
      certain sectors, including energy."
  (2) "The Committee will deliver price stability."
  (3) "We recognize that inflation has been running well ahead of the Fed's
      long-stated inflation goal of 2%. That's been going on for more than
      five years."
  (4) "Persistently high prices are a burden for the American people, but the
      recent past need not be prologue."
  Note: the word is "prologue" (confirmed from multiple sources) — the
  provisional alert contained the truncation "prolog," now corrected.
  The "five years above 2%" framing is the first time any Chair has used
  explicit duration language in an opening statement. It signals the bar for
  "mission accomplished" has been intentionally raised.
  "The recent past need not be prologue" signals deliberate discontinuity from
  the Powell-era approach. Absent from any prior Powell opening statement
  (confirmed absent Apr 29).
  Direction: STRONGLY HAWKISH.

MONETARY POLICY / STATEMENT STRUCTURE
  Warsh explicitly acknowledged the statement changes in the opening:
  "It's a bit shorter, a bit simpler, and it dispenses with some older language."
  "Absent, also, is so-called forward guidance, which we agreed was not well
  suited to the current policy conjuncture."
  Note: "current policy conjuncture" is narrow — applies to now, not necessarily
  permanently. Warsh's long-held academic critique of forward guidance predates
  this conjuncture. [Inference: the diplomatic framing may understate the
  permanence of the removal.]

  FIVE TASK FORCES ANNOUNCED (new element; no precedent in prior openings):
  Charged with reviewing: (1) communications, (2) balance sheet, (3) data
  ("use and reliance on existing data sources"), (4) productivity and jobs,
  (5) causes of inflation. Charge (verbatim): "Start with first principles.
  Ask hard questions. Examine current practice. Consider alternatives. And
  ultimately propose next steps for policymaker consideration." Timeline: most
  to conclude "this fall or by end of year."

  PRICE STABILITY COMMITMENT (bridging opening to Q&A):
  "The commitment to deliver is strong, unanimous, and unambiguous, and that's
  I think an important message we've missed for five years, and we're going to
  fix that."
  The candid acknowledgment the Fed "missed" this message for five years is a
  direct (if implicit) critique of Powell-era forward guidance and the 2020 AIT
  framework. This is committee-level language (opening statement), not personal.
  Direction: HAWKISH (structure + institutional reset).

This is Warsh's first press conference as Chair. The Q&A establishes his

KEY Q&A SIGNALS (6 bullets where the Q&A added to or went beyond the opening):

1. ON THE 2% TARGET — zero-tolerance framing goes beyond the statement:
   "That is the Federal Reserve's long-held objective of 2%. The 'two' is the
   left of the decimal point. For now, 'zero' is to the right."
   [Confirmed verbatim by web search across multiple sources.]
   Additional context: "I see no reason until we have reestablished our
   commitment and ability to deliver on the 2% inflation objective to revisit
   that." — explicitly rules out target reconsideration before 2% is hit.
   Signal: Warsh's personal bar for "done" on inflation is exactly 2.0%, not
   "near 2%." The zero-tolerance framing is harder than any Powell Q&A in
   2025–2026. ANALYST FLAG: verify exact phrasing against official transcript.

2. ON THE DOT PLOT / SEP — confirms abstention; signals reform:
   "I, however, have refrained from offering any projections of my own,
   consistent with my long-held views on the SEP, at least as currently
   structured."
   "I did not submit a dot for me. It's not helpful in the conduct of policy."
   (a) Confirms Warsh is the missing 19th → 18th dot (March 19 vs June 18).
   (b) "At least as currently structured" is a key hedge — signals the SEP
   format is under active review via the communications task force.
   (c) Explicitly characterizing a core communication tool as "not helpful" is
   a strong signal it will be reformed or eliminated — likely by September.

3. ON THE HIKE SIGNAL IN THE DOTS:
   "Despite the recent pullback in oil, half of the members of the FOMC expect
   rate hikes as soon as this year, reflecting strong labor market and inflation
   data."
   The opening statement carries no tightening bias. The Q&A quantifies the
   latent hike risk explicitly ("half," "as soon as this year") and attributes
   it to BOTH labor AND inflation, despite the SEP's unemployment projection
   going DOWN. Warsh reads the labor market as no longer a dovish offset —
   consistent with the "kept pace with the workforce" reframe.

4. ON FEWER PRESS CONFERENCES — new signal not in provisional:
   Warsh hinted at reducing the frequency of press conferences from the current
   8-per-year schedule (one per meeting under Powell): "when you have one, you
   want to make sure you have something important to say."
   If Warsh returns to 4 press conferences per year (the pre-2019 format), the
   inter-meeting information environment changes substantially — fewer scheduled
   Chair signals, higher weight on non-press-conference communications, more
   ambiguity between meetings. This is consistent with the communications
   task force mandate.
   ANALYST FLAG: Confirm whether this was a direct statement of intent to reduce
   frequency or a general communication philosophy remark.

5. ON THE DUAL MANDATE — implicit rejection of Phillips-curve tradeoff:
   "I don't believe that we have a cruel choice. I don't share the view that
   was expressed a few generations ago that the Federal Reserve chairman show
   up at the podium and say you've got to choose, and you're going to have to
   decide whether you're willing to tolerate higher inflation to put more people
   at work."
   "What I believe is if we do our job, we can make strong growth, low prices
   and strong employment mutually compatible."
   This is Warsh's explicit rejection of the Volcker/Phillips-curve tradeoff
   framing. Combined with the "kept pace with the workforce" statement reframe,
   it signals he will NOT concede a labor-market sacrifice in pursuing price
   stability. This is meaningful: it defends against the political argument
   that the holding stance will cost jobs — Warsh preemptively contests the
   premise. Absent from the opening statement; this is personal Q&A signaling.

6. ON DATA QUALITY — task force context:
   "Most of the data that central bankers and other government officials in the
   United States consume come with old-fashioned survey methods."
   "I'm really open-minded that there is a lot of new data sources, that we
   can learn from the private sector, from reforms in the official sector and
   new analytic techniques."
   This goes beyond announcing a data task force — Warsh is signaling that the
   data the Fed currently uses to make decisions (including the SEP projections)
   may be methodologically inadequate. Potential policy implication: if data
   quality is suspect, the bar for acting on individual data prints is higher —
   a hawkish lean (don't react to one or two soft reports). Also signals the
   SEP projections themselves may carry reduced weight under Warsh.
