[First post-April-29 economic outlook speech from Bowman. She gave
 zero Type A speeches from February 1 through May 28 — a 119-day
 gap spanning the Iran conflict onset, two FOMC meetings (Mar 19,
 Apr 29), and the committee's shift toward a hawkish split with
 explicit hike signals from Waller and Cook. Her January 30 stance
 (most dovish voter; 3-cut SEP; "fragile" labor; active cut advocacy)
 was the only prior baseline.]

GROWTH
  Bowman describes the economy as having "been resilient" with GDP
  growing at a "moderate to solid pace, supported by strong AI-related
  business investment." Growth has slowed since last summer on softer
  real personal income and winter weather weighing on consumer spending.
  Uniquely, she credits "supportive supply-side policies, including
  less restrictive regulations and lower business taxes" as growth-
  supportive — no other scored speaker has made this framing.
  Direction: neutral — resilient-but-moderating; AI investment offset.

LABOR MARKET
  KEY SHIFT: Bowman upgrades from January 30's "increasingly fragile
  and could continue to deteriorate" to "appears to have become more
  stable in recent months, [but] there are still signs of fragility."
  Hard data supports: unemployment at 4.3% in April, stronger payroll
  gains year-to-date. Soft data caution held: job-finding rate "on a
  downward path," employment concentrated in healthcare/social assistance,
  long-term unemployment at 25% reflecting "a labor market with low
  dynamism." The stabilization is acknowledged but not celebrated;
  structural fragility language is retained beneath the surface.
  Direction: neutral (slight hawkish lean vs. January 30, which was
  the most dovish labor characterization from any voter in early 2026).

INFLATION
  Bowman directly confronts the deterioration: "Progress on lowering
  inflation appears to have stalled" with total PCE at 3.8% and core
  PCE at 3.3% — "up noticeably since September." She attributes the
  headline rise to energy (Iran) and tariff/software idiosyncrasies.
  Crucially, she maintains her look-through analytical frame: the
  trimmed mean PCE is "closer to 2 percent since last September," and
  after removing tariffs and software, core PCE would have "continued
  to hover only a bit above 2 percent." This is a slight upward revision
  from her January 30 "close to 2 percent," but the framework is
  unchanged. She expects the Iran energy shock to be temporary: "once
  the conflict is resolved, supply disruptions will ease, leaving a
  temporary imprint in PCE inflation and minimal impacts on domestic
  economic activity." Risk caveat: if disruptions "persist well into
  the second half of the year, we could start to see broader effects
  on inflation."
  Direction: neutral (slight hawkish lean vs. January 30). Acknowledges
  deterioration but maintains the most dovish inflation interpretation
  on the committee via look-through and trimmed-mean anchoring.

MONETARY POLICY
  Two key signals:
  (1) "I appreciated and supported keeping the language from the
      March post-meeting statement about additional policy rate
      adjustments." — EXPLICIT EASING BIAS ENDORSEMENT. Bowman is
      the only scored voter who has stated she supports the easing
      bias language that Waller/Cook want to remove and that Logan,
      Hammack, and Kashkari dissented against on April 29. This is
      a meaningful committee anchor: the easing bias has at least
      one explicit defender at the voter level.
  (2) "our moderately restrictive policy stance" — CARRIED FORWARD
      from January 30. All other scored speakers describe policy as
      "well positioned" or equivalent neutral-hold language. Bowman's
      "moderately restrictive" characterization implies she sees more
      room to ease than the committee median, consistent with her
      higher estimate of the neutral rate.
  Look-through endorsed: "It is appropriate to look through temporarily
  elevated inflation readings largely due to higher energy prices,
  provided that we remain credible in our commitment to achieve our
  inflation goal and one-off tariff effects wane, as I expect."
  Conditional escape: she would reconsider if oil prices prove
  persistent or if broader second-round effects materialize.
  Cut signal: RETRACTED — no mention of cuts, no SEP disclosure,
  no "ready to adjust" language. The January 30 3-cut SEP advocacy
  has been quietly withdrawn.
  Hike signal: ABSENT — she does not echo Waller or Cook's "prepared
  to raise rates" language and is implicitly opposed to it via the
  easing bias endorsement.
  Direction: neutral-dovish — supports holding, opposes tightening,
  defends easing optionality.

NET SIGNAL: NEUTRAL-DOVISH — Bowman remains the committee's most
  explicitly dovish voter. She has moved hawkish relative to her
  own January 30 stance (cut signal retracted, labor upgraded,
  inflation acknowledged as stalled) but holds a clearly different
  position from the hawkish lean of Jefferson, Paulson, and Barkin,
  and is in direct opposition to Waller and Cook's explicit hike
  signals. Her endorsement of the easing bias and "moderately
  restrictive" characterization make her the committee's dovish
  anchor heading into the June 6 blackout.

  (Jan: "confident inflation will come down"); cut signal retracted;
  SEP disclosure dropped; 3-cut projection not mentioned.

vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (Jan 30, 2026):  HAWKISH LEAN — same as prior
  (Jan 30 is both prior speech and pre-FOMC baseline).

vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:  Bowman is the dovish outlier. While
  Waller, Cook have issued hike signals and Logan/Jefferson/Paulson
  lean hawkish, Bowman explicitly defends the easing bias and
  describes policy as "moderately restrictive." She and Jefferson
  (whose "not prejudged" language is more cautious than a hike signal)
  are the two voters most resistant to a June tightening move. The
  committee split heading into blackout: hike camp (Waller, Cook),
  hawkish lean (Logan, Jefferson, Paulson, Barkin [non-voter]),
