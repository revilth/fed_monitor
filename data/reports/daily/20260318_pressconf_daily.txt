OPENING STATEMENT [Committee language]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  "The US economy has been expanding at a solid pace." SEP median:
  real GDP 2.4% for 2026 and 2.3% for 2027 — "somewhat stronger than
  projected in December." Consumer spending resilient, business
  investment expanding. Housing still weak. No recession concern.
  Direction: neutral to positive — GDP forecast revised up.

LABOR MARKET
  Statement language: "unemployment rate has been little changed in
  recent months" — note change from Jan 28's "shown some signs of
  stabilization." Feb unemployment: 4.4%. "Job gains have remained
  low." Same immigration/labor force explanation. SEP median: 4.4%
  unemployment end 2026, edges down after.
  Direction: neutral — characterization slightly softened from
  "signs of stabilization" to "little changed."

INFLATION
  "Inflation remains somewhat elevated." PCE 2.8%, core PCE 3.0%.
  NEW: "Near-term measures of inflation expectations have risen in
  recent weeks, likely reflecting the substantial rise in oil prices
  caused by the supply disruptions in the Middle East."
  → This is the first FOMC statement to explicitly note rising near-
    term inflation expectations. A meaningful addition.
  NEW: "The implications of developments in the Middle East for the
  US economy are uncertain."
  SEP median: PCE inflation 2.7% for 2026 (higher than implied Dec),
  2.2% for 2027.
  Direction: mild hawkish — energy shock now in the statement;
  near-term expectations flagged as rising.

MONETARY POLICY — STATEMENT
  Hold maintained. "The implications of events in the Middle East are
  uncertain. In the near term, higher energy prices will push up
  overall inflation, but it is too soon to know the scope and
  duration of the potential effects on the economy."
  SEP median rate path: 3.4% end 2026, 3.1% end 2027 — UNCHANGED
  from December despite higher inflation forecast. 4-5 people moved
  from 2 cuts to 1 cut, but median held.
  Direction: neutral — unchanged median rate path; uncertainty
  justifies patience.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Q&A [Powell's own voice — key unscripted signals]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

On "looking through" energy shocks — KEY:
  "Of course it is kind of standard learning that you look through
  energy shocks, but that's always been dependent on inflation
  expectations remaining well anchored. And I think now it's also
  dependent on what you mentioned, which is that broader context of
  five years now of inflation above target. We have to keep all of
  those things in mind and the question of looking through, when it
  does arise, will be one to approach not lightly."
  → Powell is conditioning the "look through" doctrine on (a) anchored
    expectations AND (b) the five-year above-target context. This is
    the Chair's own version of the "series of shocks" concern — he is
    NOT automatically endorsing the look-through approach. This is
    the most important Q&A statement in this press conference.

On the SEP rate path being unchanged despite higher inflation:
  "Nobody knows. The economic effects could be bigger or smaller.
  People wrote down something that seemed to make sense...if we were
  ever going to skip an SCP, this would be a good one because we
  just don't know." → Unusual candor: the SEP is less informative
  than normal given the uncertainty.

On the inflation forecast write-up:
  Partly energy; also "we haven't seen the progress that we had hoped
  for on core goods and on tariffs." → Acknowledges the baseline
  disinflation story is behind schedule, not just an energy effect.

On why median rate path unchanged despite cutting fewer officials:
  4-5 participants moved from 2 cuts to 1 cut; this was offset.
  → The distribution shifted hawkish even if the median held.

  LABOR:     SLIGHT SOFTENING — "little changed" replaces "some signs
             of stabilization." Marginally more cautious.
  INFLATION: HAWKISH — energy shock added; near-term expectations

  MONETARY:  UNCHANGED on rate path median. One dissenter (Miran)

  OVERALL:   MILD HAWKISH shift driven entirely by energy shock and

vs. CHAIR'S PREVIOUS SPEECH (Jan 11 Type B):
  N/A for economic content. The Jan 28 press conference was
  effectively Powell's only prior economic communication. The
  March 18 Q&A represents the first time Powell himself articulates
  the "look through" conditioning concern — not done in January.

vs. COMMITTEE SINCE PREVIOUS FOMC:
  The Waller dissent reversal is the key committee development.
  Waller dissented in January to cut; he did not dissent in March.
  His Feb 23 speech explains why: February data partially confirmed
  the January labor market upturn, giving him reason to hold.
  The energy shock (late February) also changed the calculus.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "Look through always dependent on anchored expectations AND
    five-year context" — Powell conditions the committee's core
    doctrine. This is his personal unscripted addition, not in the
    written statement. Watch whether it appears in post-FOMC speeches
    from Jefferson or Williams. [Note: it appeared independently in
    Goolsbee Mar 7 and Barkin Mar 27 — the concept was already
    circulating before this press conference.]
  • Labor language: "little changed" (Mar 18) vs. "some signs of
    stabilization" (Jan 28). The softening of the characterization
    is the key labor signal in the statement.
  • Near-term expectations "risen in recent weeks" — first FOMC
    statement acknowledgment of expectation drift. Track whether
    this strengthens or reverses in the April statement.
  • "Too soon to know scope and duration" of energy effects —
    explicit uncertainty; justifies the unchanged rate path.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → Three questions for analyst review: (1) Powell's "look through
    not lightly" in the Q&A is the Chair signaling more hawkish
    vigilance than the statement's neutral language suggests. Does
    this represent a personal shift or committee-wide recalibration?
    (2) The SCP distribution shift (4-5 moved from 2 cuts to 1 cut)
    is more hawkish than the unchanged median suggests — this is the
    real signal in the dots. (3) Waller dropping his dissent: was
    this data-driven (Feb labor market improved) or energy-shock-
    driven? His Apr 17 speech answers this.
