GROWTH
  Brief treatment — not the focus. Jefferson sees "the economy as
  continuing to grow, led by resilient consumer spending and healthy
  business investment." GDP expanded ~2% in 2025; he expects a similar
  or slightly faster pace in 2026. On the positive side: AI infrastructure
  investment, high pace of new business formation, deregulation. On the
  negative side: elevated energy prices could weigh on consumer and
  business spending if sustained. No material shift from prior speeches.
  Direction: neutral — solid baseline with energy-driven uncertainty.

LABOR MARKET
  Jefferson's verdict is unchanged: "I see the labor market as roughly
  in balance" — but he again appends "susceptible to adverse shocks," a
  qualifier first added Mar 26 and now durable across two speeches. Despite
  a strong March jobs headline (+178K), he deliberately emphasizes the soft
  3-month average (~70K/month) and adds explicit caution: "I remain
  cautious about that assessment [of stabilization]." One good month does
  not move him. He is choosing the pessimistic read of an ambiguous signal.
  Direction: mild dovish lean — downside risk consistently flagged;
  resistant to upside surprises in the data.

INFLATION
  He states his prior expectation ("disinflationary process would resume
  once higher tariffs are no longer pushing up consumer prices"), then
  immediately concedes the energy shock complicates it: "The recent increase
  in energy prices, however, will apply some upward pressure on headline
  inflation, at least in the near term. The ongoing trade policy uncertainty
  and geopolitical tensions pose upside risk to my inflation forecast."
  "Upside risk to my inflation forecast" — first used Mar 26, now durable —
  is a personal admission that his baseline has deteriorated. He is not
  revising his inflation forecast outright, but flagging the upside risk.
  Direction: slight hawkish lean vs. pre-FOMC; unchanged vs. Mar 26.

MONETARY POLICY
  Verbatim committee formula: "our current policy stance is well-positioned
  to respond to a range of outcomes." His dual-risk framing ("downside risk
  to labor, upside risk to inflation") is locked in as his stable
  characterization — identical to Mar 26. No cut signal; no new guidance.
  Direction: unchanged.

NET SIGNAL: NEUTRAL — patient hold confirmed; no movement on either side

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (Mar 26):    UNCHANGED across all four topics.
vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (Feb 6):  HAWKISH on inflation — Feb 6 was confident
  disinflation would resume; energy shock and "upside risk to my forecast"
  are new. DOVISH on labor — Feb 6 did not flag explicit downside risks;
  Apr 7 has "susceptible to adverse shocks" and "cautious about
  stabilization." On growth and monetary policy: unchanged. Net: more
  cautious on both sides of the mandate → hold confirmed.
vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:     Troika center lane alongside Williams. More
  patient than Bowman (who wants proactive cuts); less hawkish than Waller
  (who would hold even if labor weakens). His symmetric dual-risk formula
  is now shared with Williams — this is the official Troika holding language.

    If it appears in the April 29 FOMC statement, the committee has formally
    upgraded its labor downside risk assessment beyond "little changed."
  • "Upside risk to my inflation forecast" — personal, not yet in statement
