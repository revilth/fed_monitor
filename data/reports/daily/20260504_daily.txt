━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  Forecasts 2–2.25% real GDP in 2026 and 2027. Consumer spending
  resilient, business investment robust (AI-driven). Residential
  construction and federal government spending declining but being
  offset. PMI flash surveys show "abrupt, sharp increases in delivery
  times and input prices" — supply-chain disruptions emerging.
  A "new round of tariffs in the coming months" flagged as additional
  upside risk to import prices.
  Direction: neutral — solid baseline with emerging supply-side risks.

LABOR MARKET
  Williams' overall verdict: "conflicting signs — Much of the hard
  data points to stabilization, while some of the soft data suggest
  continued gradual slowing." Hard data steady: 4.3% unemployment
  unchanged 9 months, payroll growth consistent with labor force
  growth, UI claims low and stable. Soft data subsection: Conference
  Board job availability trending down; NY Fed job security gap not
  stabilizing; NFIB position-filling difficulty falling — "Together,
  these indicators suggest increasing labor market slack." This
  conclusion is specific to the soft data indicators listed, not a
  characterization of the labor market overall. Critically, Williams
  distinguishes the current environment from 2021-22: "the labor
  market is not adding to inflation pressures," ruling out the
  compounding effect the dissenters fear.
  Direction: neutral — overall "conflicting signs"; soft data
  subsection concludes indicators suggest increasing slack.

INFLATION
  PCE at 3.5% in March; tariffs and energy account for ~1 percentage
  point. Three-layer look-through case: (1) tariff pass-through
  "mostly completed in the next few months" and will fade;
  (2) "underlying inflation outside of imported goods and energy has
  so far remained stable" with "no signs of significant second-round
  effects"; (3) medium- and longer-term inflation expectations
  "solidly anchored." Forecasts 3% inflation in 2026, 2% in 2027
  as tariff and energy effects "move into the rearview mirror."
  Acknowledges a "new round of tariffs in the coming months" as
  a complication but still frames it as temporary. This directly
  contradicts Hammack's "broad based" framing and Logan's
  "pre-existing above 2%" argument.
  Direction: neutral with dovish lean — look-through fully
  articulated; "stable underlying" counters the dissenters' narrative.

MONETARY POLICY
  Echoes FOMC statement: "current stance of monetary policy is well
  positioned to balance the risks to our maximum employment and price
  stability goals." No cut signal — absence confirmed; no new forward
  guidance. Symmetric risk framing: "risks to both sides of our
  mandate have increased." Patient hold.
  Cut signal status: ABSENT — no change from Mar 30 retraction.
  Direction: neutral — patient hold confirmed.

NET SIGNAL: NEUTRAL with dovish lean — Troika holdline maintained;
  dovish labor signal ("increasing slack") offset by continued absence
  of cut signal.

  data subsection now concludes indicators "suggest increasing labor
  market slack" (more explicit than Apr 16); unchanged on inflation
