1.  THOMAS I. BARKIN  |  Richmond Fed President  |  Non Voter
    First post-FOMC speech; pre-FOMC baseline Mar 27
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  Economy described as "remarkably resilient" — a materially
  more positive characterization than his March 27 "fog" framing.
  Barkin enumerates multiple positive indicators: consumer spending
  up (including non-gas), corporate profits high, AI investment at
  "full steam," manufacturing surveys rebounded, S&P records, payrolls
  bouncing back. Two structural explanations for oil resilience:
  reduced U.S. oil import dependence and fiscal/AI investment offsets.
  Hedge: "at least for now" on offset durability; behavioral note that
  demand support rests partly on consumer assumption of transitoriness.
  Direction: neutral — clearly improved vs. March; no growth alarm,
  but resilience is conditional and behavioral factors are fragile.

LABOR MARKET
  Hard data improved: "Payroll growth has started to bounce back"
  — contrast with March 27's "employment growth is near zero."
  Forward risks explicit and structured. Businesses: gains from
  attrition-based productivity — the question is whether "conviction
  to reduce headcount more actively" builds. Consumers: UMich
  sentiment at an all-time record low in May, but spending has
  "persisted." Barkin names the three conditions for consumer
  pullback: real wage growth slowing, tax refunds fading, trade-down
  options exhausted. Descriptive scenario framing (not named/explicit).
  Direction: neutral — hard data improved; forward risk articulated
  on both labor supply and consumer demand sides.

INFLATION
  Exact PCE data provided: headline 3.5%, core 3.2% YoY in March —
  consistent with the April 29 FOMC statement's "elevated" language.
  Long-term inflation expectations: "well anchored" (unconditional —
  slightly more sanguine than Paulson's "currently in a good place"
  hedge from May 19). Near-term expectations up on gas prices:
  acknowledged but not alarmed. KEY SIGNAL: "With inflation above
  our 2 percent target for over five years now, it's worth asking
  whether the cumulative impact of so many waves risks loosening
  the anchor." — Duration risk question raised; not resolved. SECOND
  KEY SIGNAL: PRICING POWER REVERSAL — In March 27, Barkin reported
  businesses unable to pass through costs ("nine in ten...won't be
  able to...consumers tired of high prices"). In May 21, he reports
  "renewed determination by businesses to pass these highly visible,
  conflict-driven cost increases on." Directional reversal in his
  ground-level pricing intelligence — from cost-absorption to
  pass-through attempt.
  Direction: hawkish lean — duration framing added; pricing reversal;
  PCE data higher than March baseline.

MONETARY POLICY
  Hold endorsed: "little clarity on the duration and impact of this
  latest supply shock, it made sense to give ourselves some time."
  Boilerplate echoed: "Fed is well positioned to respond as
  appropriate." Two-sided risk framing: "rough seas that pressure
  the employment side...inflation side...or conceivably both."
  Scenario framing is DESCRIPTIVE — risks named but no pre-committed
  policy response (unlike Waller's Apr 17 BENIGN/ADVERSE explicit
  named scenarios). Cut signal: ABSENT. Hike signal: ABSENT.
  Direction: neutral — patience/hold framing; symmetric risk language;
  no new directional guidance.

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH LEAN — Barkin's first post-FOMC speech is more
  hawkish than his March 27 pre-FOMC baseline. Growth improved (less
  fog, more resilience), PCE higher (3.2% core vs. ~2.8% March), and
  two new hawkish additions: duration framing ("five years above
  target") and a reversal in his pricing-power ground intelligence
  (businesses now attempting pass-through). Policy stance unchanged
  but underlying conditions are more inflation-weighted.

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (Mar 27, 2026):      HAWKISH — Growth improved;
  inflation higher; pricing-power signal reversed from disinflationary
  to pass-through; duration concern added. Policy stance unchanged.

vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (Mar 27, 2026):   same — HAWKISH shift.
  Mar 27 was the pre-FOMC baseline. The full post-FOMC trajectory
  from Barkin is therefore captured in a single comparison: his view
  has moved hawkish on inflation substance while hold conviction
  on policy is maintained.

vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:             Barkin now aligned with the
  broader post-FOMC hawkish lean. His "five years above target"
  duration framing joins Goolsbee (May 12) and Collins (May 13)
  using the same phrase. His pricing-power reversal adds a
  qualitatively distinct and uniquely credible signal — Barkin's
  on-the-ground business intelligence is the most reliable real-time
  pricing data the Fed produces outside of official statistics.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "Five years above our 2 percent target" — Goolsbee (May 12),
    Collins (May 13), Barkin (May 21). Three Non Voters in 10 days.
    Language cycle stage: EMERGING SHARED (Non Voter tier). Has not
    yet appeared from a Just Voter or Troika member. If Paulson or
    Williams adopts this framing, it marks a committee-wide upgrade
    in duration concern.
  • "Renewed determination to pass...cost increases" — Barkin's
    reversal of his own March signal. One official's observation,
    but Barkin's ground-level business contacts make this worth
    tracking against CPI/PPI sub-components over next 4–6 weeks.
  • "Cumulative impact of so many waves risks loosening the anchor"
    — duration/anchor concern raised as a question, not a verdict.
    If Barkin or other officials shift from question to assertion
    (i.e., "anchor has loosened"), that would be a significant
    hawkish escalation.

ANALYST FLAG: YES
  → Barkin's pricing-power reversal demands tracking. He is the
    committee's most direct conduit for small/mid-sized business
    pricing behavior, and in March he provided the committee's most
    credible disinflationary signal (firms losing pricing power).
    That signal has now reversed. If this persists in subsequent
    Barkin speeches, it corroborates upside PCE risk beyond the
    current energy shock and strengthens the case for extended hold
    or eventual tightening — consistent with the direction Paulson
    validated explicitly on May 19.
