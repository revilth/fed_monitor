━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

GROWTH
  GDP growing at ~2% in Q1 2026, consistent with prior year pace.
  Business investment is "a bright spot," led by AI infrastructure
  buildout. Consumption growth has moderated (1.6% real rate in Q1,
  down from 2.1% in 2025), though she characterizes the overall
  picture as "resilient." AI demand offsets are cited as sustaining
  activity despite energy shock headwinds.
  Direction: neutral — solid pace with noted consumption softening

LABOR MARKET
  Hard data: unemployment at 4.3%, "stable and consistent with full
  employment." Low-hiring/low-firing balance described as historically
  anomalous — the only post-1948 instance of a >1pp unemployment rise
  outside a recession. Soft data diverges: surveys show rising
  pessimism, and corporate contacts report growing AI-driven pressure
  toward layoffs. She holds a benign base case ("labor market will
  remain stable") but flags AI-driven labor demand reduction as a new
  downside risk. Labor is explicitly NOT the driver of inflation.
  Direction: neutral with dovish tail risk flag

INFLATION
  "Inflation is too high." Headline PCE 3.5%, core 3.2% (March).
  April data point to continuing pressure from energy and tariffs.
  Frames both as supply shocks, then formally assesses whether to
  look through: (1) demand — not materially adding to inflation;
  (2) expectations — long-run anchored but "watching closely";
  (3) policy stance — "mildly restrictive" doing the work.
  Concludes: current stance is appropriate. She exculpates labor
  from inflation: "continued high inflation does not appear to be
  driven by labor market pressures." Key concern is expectations
  fragility given five years of above-target inflation.
  Direction: hawkish lean — elevated data, expectations flagged

MONETARY POLICY
  Rate characterization unchanged: "mildly restrictive." Holds are
  clearly appropriate now: "monetary policy is in a good place."
  Explicit cut bar: "rate cuts would only become appropriate once we
  have seen sustained progress on inflation." Most important signal:
  she explicitly validates market pricing of "scenarios where the
  funds rate remains unchanged for an extended period, as well as
  scenarios where further tightening becomes necessary." This is new
  language — she has not endorsed tightening scenarios before in the
  2026 corpus. No easing bias language is echoed (compare: she voted
  for the April 29 easing bias language three weeks ago).
  Direction: hawkish lean

NET SIGNAL: HAWKISH LEAN — Paulson has modestly escalated from her
  March 27 pre-FOMC baseline and notably from her April 29 vote
  posture. The explicit endorsement of tightening scenarios is the
  key escalation. Rate characterization ("mildly restrictive") and
  inflation-first cut bar ("sustained progress") are unchanged, but
  she has now added an explicit upper-tail acknowledgment.

vs. PRIOR SPEECH (March 27, 2026):       HAWKISH — inflation data harder
  (3.2% core vs. 2.8%); endorses tightening scenarios; more explicit
  on cut bar. Labor soft data and AI risks are new additions.
vs. PRE-FOMC SPEECH (March 27, 2026):    HAWKISH — same as above.
vs. COMMITTEE SINCE FOMC:                Paulson sits with Barkin and
  Collins (May 13) in the neutral-to-hawkish zone of post-April 29
  speakers. She is more hawkish than her April 29 vote implied.
  Logan, Kashkari, Hammack remain more hawkish (dissented). Jefferson
  and Williams have not spoken since Paulson's May 19 speech.

LANGUAGE TO WATCH:
  • "rate cuts would only become appropriate once we have seen
    sustained progress on inflation" — explicit cut bar from the
    estimated median voter. More restrictive than the April 29 easing
    bias language she voted for. If Jefferson or Williams adopt this
    formulation, it confirms committee-level shift.
  • "scenarios where further tightening becomes necessary" — new
    from Paulson. Currently DISPERSED (only her; parallels Logan's
    hawkish framing). Watch for Troika adoption.
  • "mildly restrictive" — now used by both Paulson and Collins (May 13).
    Emerging shared descriptor for current rate stance among
    non-Troika voters.
